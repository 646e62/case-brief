<P class="CourtofAppealforOntario">COURT OF APPEAL FOR ONTARIO</P>

<P class="CITATION">CITATION: R. v. Reeve, 2020 ONCA 381</P>

<P class="CITATION">DATE: 20200610</P>

<P class="DOCKET">DOCKET: C67010</P>

<P class="Namesofpanelofjudges">Fairburn, Nordheimer and
Harvison Young JJ.A.</P>

<P class="BETWEEN">BETWEEN</P>

<P class="Namesofpanelofjudges">Her Majesty the Queen</P>

<P class="Partys">Respondent</P>

<P class="and">and</P>

<P class="Namesofpanelofjudges">Daniel Reeve</P>

<P class="Partys">Appellant</P>

<P class="Nameofcounsel">Erin Dann and Angela Ruffo, for the

<P class="Nameofcounsel">Catherine Weiler, for the respondent</P>

<P class="Heardandreleasedorally">Heard: May 26, 2020 by

<P class="Onappealfrom">On appeal from the sentence imposed on

sitting without a jury.</P>

<P class="Judge">Fairburn J.A.:</P>

<P class="Heading" style="margin-left:14.2pt;text-indent:-14.2pt">I.<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp;  </SPAN>&nbsp;OVERVIEW</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="1" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="1" data-viibes-start="0">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par1">1</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>The appellant ran a financial investment company
and had a good reputation in the financial industry. He got into financial
difficulty in 2007. Over the next two and a half years, he perpetrated a
large-scale fraud on 41 unsuspecting clients. As found by the sentencing judge,
he used the victims&rsquo; money for a number of purposes, including business and
personal expenses, as well as payments to his ex-wife to fulfill a large
outstanding support order. As is often the case in classic Ponzi schemes, the
appellant also used some of the victims&rsquo; money to pay back other victims, ones
who were becoming suspicious about what had happened to their money. By taking
the money of some to pay others, the appellant successfully avoided coming to
the attention of the police for a significant period of time.&nbsp; At the end of
the day, the victims were out over $10 million and even larger sums had been
put at risk.</P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="2" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="2" data-viibes-start="1" data-viibes-end="0">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par2">2</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>The appellant was convicted of fraud over $5,000
and theft over $5,000. He was sentenced to the maximum term of 14 years&rsquo;
imprisonment. Given that he had already spent 71 months in pre-sentence
custody, counsel agreed that he should receive credit in the amount of 8.9
years. He was provided with an additional 1.1 year of credit due to what the
trial judge deemed &ldquo;harsh conditions&rdquo; during the 71 months he had spent in
remand in accordance with the principle laid down in <EM>R. v. Duncan</EM>,
<A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2016/2016onca754/2016onca754.html">2016 ONCA 754</A>, at para. <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2016/2016onca754/2016onca754.html#par6">6</A>.&nbsp; </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="3" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-message-pen" data-noteup-count="2"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="3" data-viibes-start="2" data-viibes-end="1">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par3">3</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>A restitution order pursuant to <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="738" ss="1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec738subsec1_smooth">s. 738(1)</A>(a) of
the <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html"><EM>Criminal Code</EM>, R.S.C. 1985, c. C-46</A>, issued in the amount of
$10,887,885. In addition, a fine in lieu of forfeiture was ordered in the same
amount as the restitution order. The appellant was given ten years following
the completion of his term of imprisonment to pay the fine. In default of
payment of the fine, pursuant to <SPAN class="reflex2-link" link-count="1" link1="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec462.37subsec4" title1="Criminal Code" concatenated-id="12792" s="462.37" ss="4" data-link-type="weak">s. 462.37(4)</SPAN>(a)(vii), the appellant was
ordered to serve the maximum term of ten years&rsquo; imprisonment. The fine in lieu
of forfeiture is to be reduced by any amount paid pursuant to the restitution
order: <EM>R. v. Waxman</EM>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2014/2014onca256/2014onca256.html">2014 ONCA 256</A>, at para. <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2014/2014onca256/2014onca256.html#par31">31</A>. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="4" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="4" data-viibes-start="3" data-viibes-end="2">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par4">4</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>The appellant pursues both a conviction and
sentence appeal. Given the time sensitivity attaching to the sentence appeal,
the court agreed to hear it first.&nbsp; On May 29, 2020, leave to appeal the
sentence was granted, the appeal was granted, and the sentence was varied to
ten years&rsquo; incarceration. No other aspect of the sentence was appealed.
Accordingly, the sentence was affirmed in all other respects. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="5" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="5" data-viibes-start="4" data-viibes-end="3">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par5">5</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>Written reasons were to follow. These are those
reasons.</P></div>

<P class="Heading">II. THE REASONS FOR SENTENCE</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="6" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="6" data-viibes-start="5" data-viibes-end="4">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par6">6</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>The sentencing judge spent a good portion of his
reasons reviewing the devastating impact that the fraud had on the victims.
About this there is no dispute. The sentencing judge described the victims as
&ldquo;disabled, the elderly, the grieving spouse, the emotionally vulnerable, the
close long-time friends, the loyal client, and complete strangers&rdquo;. Many of
them lost their life savings to the appellant. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="7" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="7" data-viibes-start="6" data-viibes-end="5">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par7">7</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>The sentencing judge concluded that there were
no mitigating circumstances operative in this case. In contrast, he concluded
that &ldquo;virtually every aggravating circumstance recognized by the <EM><A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html">Criminal
Code</A></EM> and the case law&rdquo; was present, pointing to:</P></div>

<P class="MsoListBulletCxSpFirst" style="margin-left:70.9pt;text-indent:-14.2pt">&bull;<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN>the amount of the fraud and large number of
victims; </P>

<P class="MsoListBulletCxSpMiddle" style="margin-left:70.9pt;text-indent:-14.2pt">&bull;<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN>the potential to adversely affect investor
confidence in the financial market; </P>

<P class="MsoListBulletCxSpMiddle" style="margin-left:70.9pt;text-indent:-14.2pt">&bull;<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN>the fact that the appellant took advantage of
the high regard in which he was held in the investment community, breaching the
trust of his clients and the industry licencing requirements; </P>

<P class="MsoListBulletCxSpMiddle" style="margin-left:70.9pt;text-indent:-14.2pt">&bull;<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN>the appellant&rsquo;s lack of remorse and empathy for
his victims (as further discussed below); </P>

<P class="MsoListBulletCxSpMiddle" style="margin-left:70.9pt;text-indent:-14.2pt">&bull;<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN>the serious victim impact; </P>

<P class="MsoListBulletCxSpMiddle" style="margin-left:70.9pt;text-indent:-14.2pt">&bull;<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN>the length of time over which the fraud had been
perpetrated; </P>

<P class="MsoListBulletCxSpMiddle" style="margin-left:70.9pt;text-indent:-14.2pt">&bull;<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN>the appellant&rsquo;s motivation being rooted in his
inflated ego and extravagant lifestyle; and </P>

<P class="MsoListBulletCxSpLast" style="margin-left:70.9pt;text-indent:-14.2pt">&bull;<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN>the fact that the appellant &ldquo;exploited the fear
and panic created by the 2008-2009 worldwide financial crisis &hellip; by convincing
clients/victims, who trusted him, to cash in their losing portfolios and
convert the monies into &lsquo;safer&rsquo; investments&rdquo;.</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="8" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="8" data-viibes-start="7" data-viibes-end="6">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par8">8</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>The sentencing judge considered prior
authorities where sentences had been imposed in like situations, but ultimately
concluded that they were of little assistance because of what he perceived to
be the unique circumstances of this case and this offender. Ultimately, he
concluded that the appropriate disposition was the maximum 14-year sentence.</P></div>

<P class="Heading">III. USING A LACK OF REMORSE AS AN
AGGRAVATING FACTOR</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="9" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-message-pen" data-noteup-count="1"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="9" data-viibes-start="8" data-viibes-end="7">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par9">9</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>The appellant raises numerous grounds of appeal.
It is only necessary to deal with one. It relates to the use of the lack of
remorse as an aggravating factor on sentence.</P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="10" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-message-pen" data-noteup-count="1"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="10" data-viibes-start="9" data-viibes-end="8">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par10">10</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>The appellant apologized at the sentencing
hearing for the fact that the victims had &ldquo;suffered at [his] hands&rdquo;. The
sentencing judge rejected that apology as &ldquo;hollow&rdquo;. He found that the appellant
had a complete lack of remorse. It was open to the trial judge to make that
finding. The difficulty is with how that finding was put to use. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="11" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-message-pen" data-noteup-count="6"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="11" data-viibes-start="10" data-viibes-end="9">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par11">11</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>A genuine expression of remorse can constitute
an important mitigating consideration at the time of sentencing. When an
offender demonstrates, through actions and/or words, that he or she is
genuinely remorseful for his or her conduct, it can show that the offender has
some insight into his or her past actions and takes responsibility for them.
Taking responsibility for past conduct is an important step toward
rehabilitation and gives cause for hope that the offender may be set on a path
of change. The greater the genuine insight into past offending behaviour, the
greater the cause for hope. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="12" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-message-pen" data-noteup-count="8"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="12" data-viibes-start="11" data-viibes-end="10">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par12">12</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>While a genuine expression of remorse can serve
to mitigate a sentence, the opposite is not true. An offender cannot be
punished for a lack of remorse. The reason is clear. Punishing an accused for
failing to express remorse comes &ldquo;perilously close&rdquo; to punishing him or her for
exercising the right to make full answer and defence: <EM>R. v. Valentini</EM>
(1999), <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onca/doc/1999/1999canlii1885/1999canlii1885.html">1999 CanLII 1885 (ON CA)</A>, 43 O.R. (3d) 178 (C.A.), at para. <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/on/onca/doc/1999/1999canlii1885/1999canlii1885.html#par83">83</A>. Even after a guilty verdict, an
accused is entitled to maintain his or her innocence and cannot be punished for
maintaining that stance. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="13" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-message-pen" data-noteup-count="1"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="13" data-viibes-start="12" data-viibes-end="11">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par13">13</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>Crown counsel emphasizes that, while remorse
cannot typically be used as an aggravating factor on sentence, there are
limited exceptions to the rule. This case is said to fall within those
exceptions. Specifically, Crown counsel points to the fact that a lack of
remorse may shine a light on the &ldquo;likelihood of future dangerousness&rdquo;, as well
as inform the applicability of sentencing principles involving specific
deterrence and rehabilitation: <EM>Valentini</EM>, at para. <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/on/onca/doc/1999/1999canlii1885/1999canlii1885.html#par82">82</A>; <EM>R. v.
P.(B.)</EM> (2004), <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2004/2004canlii33468/2004canlii33468.html">2004 CanLII 33468 (ON CA)</A>, 190 O.A.C. 354,&nbsp; at para. <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2004/2004canlii33468/2004canlii33468.html#par2">2</A>. See also: <EM>R. v. F.(J.)</EM>,
<A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2011/2011onca220/2011onca220.html">2011 ONCA 220</A>, 105 O.R. (3d) 161, aff&rsquo;d on other grounds in <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2013/2013scc12/2013scc12.html">2013 SCC 12</A>, [2013]
1 S.C.R. 565, at para. <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2013/2013scc12/2013scc12.html#par85">85</A>; <EM>R. v. Shah</EM>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2017/2017onca872/2017onca872.html">2017 ONCA 872</A>, at para. <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2017/2017onca872/2017onca872.html#par8">8</A>.
Crown counsel maintains that the trial judge&rsquo;s references to the absence of
remorse should be understood as references to the appellant&rsquo;s &ldquo;attitude toward
the crime&rdquo;, an attitude that underscored his &ldquo;likelihood of future
dangerousness&rdquo;.&nbsp; </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="14" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-message-pen" data-noteup-count="3"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="14" data-viibes-start="13" data-viibes-end="12">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par14">14</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>As supported by the authorities just cited, the
absence of remorse will sometimes be relevant in the sentencing process. That
does not mean, though, that someone can be punished for failing to show
remorse. While a lack of remorse may, in rare circumstances, inform potential
future dangerousness, which can in turn inform the application of some
sentencing principles, such as the suitability of emphasizing rehabilitation,
it must never be used as an aggravating factor that is deserving of punishment.
</P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="15" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-message-pen" data-noteup-count="1"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="15" data-viibes-start="14" data-viibes-end="13">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par15">15</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>Despite Crown counsel&rsquo;s capable argument on this
point, I cannot read the sentencing judge as having used the absence of remorse
in the limited way that she suggests. In my view, the reasons for sentence make
clear that the appellant was actually punished for his lack of remorse in the
face of what the trial judge perceived to be a strong prosecution case. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="16" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-message-pen" data-noteup-count="1"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="16" data-viibes-start="15" data-viibes-end="14">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par16">16</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>The second paragraph of the sentencing judge&rsquo;s
reasons following the heading &ldquo;Aggravating Circumstances&rdquo; reads as follows:</P></div>

<P class="CQuote">Mr. Reeve has <U>absolutely no remorse</U> or
empathy for the victims. &hellip; During his trial evidence, Mr. Reeve <U>insisted he
had done nothing wrong despite the absolutely overwhelming evidence of
fraudulent intent and fraudulent conduct</U> deliberately perpetrated by Mr.
Reeve over the indictment period from 2007-2009. [Emphasis added.]</P>

<P class="CQuote">The evidence at this trial and subsequent
sentencing confirm the following comments found at page 9 of the presentence
report:</P>

<P class="DSubquote">The subject does not take responsibility
for his offences, and <U>shows no remorse</U> for any of his offences. Of
concern the subject appears to have little to no empathy for the victims&rsquo;
losses. <U>He denies any intent to defraud investors in any of his companies</U>.
[Emphasis added.]</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="17" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-message-pen" data-noteup-count="1"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="17" data-viibes-start="16" data-viibes-end="15">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par17">17</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>Later in the reasons, the absence of remorse was
directly linked to the decision to impose the maximum custodial term:</P></div>

<P class="CQuote">When the fraud was done and the money was
gone, many, if not most, of the victims, were left with lives of complete devastation,
absolute destitution and utter despair, which in many cases continues to this
day. <U>Mr. Reeve, like a true predator, walked away, until his arrest, with
absolutely no empathy or remorse for the suffering and scarring left behind.</U></P>

<P class="CQuote"><U>If that scenario does not cry out for a
maximum sentence, what does</U>? [Emphasis added.]</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="18" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-message-pen" data-noteup-count="1"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="18" data-viibes-start="17" data-viibes-end="16">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par18">18</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>These passages stand in direct opposition to the
rule that an accused cannot be punished for an absence of remorse. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="19" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-message-pen" data-noteup-count="1"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="19" data-viibes-start="18" data-viibes-end="17">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par19">19</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>Not only was the appellant&rsquo;s lack of remorse
specifically identified as an aggravating factor, but its strength as an
aggravating factor was directly linked to his having chosen to make full answer
and defence in the face of what the sentencing judge saw as &ldquo;absolutely
overwhelming evidence&rdquo; of his guilt. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="20" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-message-pen" data-noteup-count="1"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="20" data-viibes-start="19" data-viibes-end="18">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par20">20</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>Punishing a person for maintaining their
innocence, based on an after-the-fact determination that the prosecution had a
strong case, could do serious harm to the criminal justice system. Accused
persons are entitled to put the Crown to its proof and cannot be punished or
seen to be punished after-the-fact, simply because the Crown met that burden.
Accused must be able to assert the right to full answer and defence,
unencumbered by fear of future implications. To proceed otherwise would
seriously undermine that fundamental right. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="21" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="21" data-viibes-start="20" data-viibes-end="19">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par21">21</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>The fact that the accused was punished for his
lack of remorse in the face of a strong Crown case, and the sheer strength of
that factor in the sentencing decision, is evidenced in the rhetorical question
put just prior to the maximum custodial sentence being announced. The trial
judge asked, &ldquo;If that scenario does not cry out for a maximum sentence, what
does?&rdquo; The &ldquo;scenario&rdquo; he was referring to had just been stated in the previous
paragraph: walking away from the devastated victims with &ldquo;absolutely no empathy
or remorse for the suffering and scarring left behind.&rdquo; </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="22" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="22" data-viibes-start="21" data-viibes-end="20">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par22">22</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>In my view, the reasons lead to the inescapable
conclusion that, among other things, the appellant was punished for his failure
to show remorse, including by exercising his right to a trial.</P></div>

<P class="Heading">IV. DID THE ERROR IN PRINCIPLE HAVE AN IMPACT
ON THE SENTENCE?</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="23" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="23" data-viibes-start="22" data-viibes-end="21">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par23">23</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>It is an error in principle to use the absence
of remorse as an aggravating factor for which the accused should be punished.
Where an error in principle is found to have had an impact on the sentence, the
appellate court must perform its own sentencing analysis to determine a fit
sentence: <EM>R. v. Lacasse</EM>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2015/2015scc64/2015scc64.html">2015 SCC 64</A>, [2015] 3 S.C.R. 1089, at para.
<A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2015/2015scc64/2015scc64.html#par43">43</A>; <EM>R. v. Friesen</EM>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2020/2020scc9/2020scc9.html">2020 SCC 9</A>, at para. <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2020/2020scc9/2020scc9.html#par27">27</A>. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="24" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="24" data-viibes-start="23" data-viibes-end="22">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par24">24</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>The question here is whether the trial judge&rsquo;s
error in using the absence of remorse as an aggravating factor had an impact on
the sentence imposed. Crown counsel argues that it did not, emphasizing that
virtually every statutory and common law aggravating factor was present in this
case, justifying the maximum sentence imposed. Therefore, even if remorse was
wrongfully described as an aggravating factor, the 14-year sentence was still
called for and this court should not interfere. I disagree. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="25" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="25" data-viibes-start="24" data-viibes-end="23">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par25">25</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>On the sentencing judge&rsquo;s own words, it was the
victims&rsquo; suffering and the appellant&rsquo;s lack of remorse for that suffering that
called for the maximum term of imprisonment. Accordingly, I conclude that the
error in principle is inextricably linked to the imposition of the maximum
custodial term imposed in this case. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="26" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="26" data-viibes-start="25" data-viibes-end="24">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par26">26</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>The question now becomes: what factors should
the court take into account in sentencing the appellant afresh?</P></div>

<P class="Heading">V. SENTENCING THE APPELLANT AFRESH</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="27" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="27" data-viibes-start="26" data-viibes-end="25">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par27">27</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>In the event that this court sentences the
appellant afresh, consistent with the Crown&rsquo;s position at trial, Crown counsel
maintains that the maximum term of imprisonment should be imposed. Consistent
with his position at trial, the appellant maintains that a custodial term of
between eight to ten years&rsquo; imprisonment is appropriate.</P></div>

<P class="MsoHeading7">(a)<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">
</SPAN>Proportionality and Parity Work in Tandem</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="28" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="28" data-viibes-start="27" data-viibes-end="26">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par28">28</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>Proportionality and parity are key sentencing
principles. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="29" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="29" data-viibes-start="28" data-viibes-end="27">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par29">29</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>Sentences must be proportionate to the gravity
of the offence and the degree of responsibility of the offender: <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="718.1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec718.1_smooth"><EM>Criminal
Code</EM>, s. 718.1</A>. The principle of parity must also be taken into account,
involving the idea that &ldquo;similar offenders who commit similar offences in
similar circumstances should receive similar sentences&rdquo;: <EM>Friesen</EM>, at
para. <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2020/2020scc9/2020scc9.html#par31">31</A>; <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="718.2" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec718.2_smooth"><EM>Criminal Code</EM>, s. 718.2</A>(b). </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="30" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="30" data-viibes-start="29" data-viibes-end="28">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par30">30</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>While the trial judge referred to some
authorities that had been provided to him by counsel during the sentencing
hearing, he found that they were not &ldquo;helpful due to the individual
circumstances of each particular case.&rdquo; In the end, he disregarded those
authorities altogether and imposed a sentence well above any sentence that has
previously been imposed for like offenders in like circumstances.&nbsp; </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="31" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-message-pen" data-noteup-count="1"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="31" data-viibes-start="30" data-viibes-end="29">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par31">31</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>The fact that each crime has its own unique
circumstances, and is committed by its own unique offender, does not mean that
parity has no role to play in the sentencing process. While sentencing ranges
cannot be seen as &ldquo;straitjackets&rdquo;, and under or overshooting a range will not
on its own give rise to a demonstrably unfit sentence, parity remains a strong
principle of sentencing, one that exists as an expression of the principle of
proportionality: Friesen, at paras. <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2020/2020scc9/2020scc9.html#par32">32, 37, 108</A>; <EM>Lacasse</EM>, at paras.
<A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2015/2015scc64/2015scc64.html#par58">58, 60-61</A>. Consequently, the principle of proportionality is respected, in
part, by referring to sentences imposed in other cases, sentences that reflect
the &ldquo;collective experience and wisdom of the judiciary&rdquo;: <EM>Friesen</EM>, at
para. <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2020/2020scc9/2020scc9.html#par33">33</A>.</P></div>

<P class="MsoHeading7">(b)<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">
</SPAN>Guidance From Prior Authorities</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="32" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="32" data-viibes-start="31" data-viibes-end="30">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par32">32</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>The parties point to <EM>R. v. Lacroix</EM>,
<A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/qc/qccs/doc/2009/2009qccs4519/2009qccs4519.html">2009 QCCS 4519</A>, [2009] R.J.Q. 2569, as the high-water mark for sentences in
large-scale frauds. In <EM>Lacroix</EM>, Wagner J. (as he then was) imposed a
13-year sentence. Mr. Lacroix pled guilty, but only on the eve of trial. He
defrauded 9,200 victims over a four-year period. The victims lost almost $100
million and the impact on them was devastating. The ill-gotten gains funded the
accused&rsquo;s lavish lifestyle. There was a breach of trust, and the sentencing
judge found beyond a reasonable doubt that the accused&rsquo;s actions &ldquo;adversely
affected the Canadian economy and shook investors&rdquo;: <EM>Lacroix</EM>, at para.
<A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/qc/qccs/doc/2009/2009qccs4519/2009qccs4519.html#par37">37</A>. Wagner J. referred to the case as &ldquo;unprecedented in the annals of Canadian
legal history&rdquo;: at para. 4. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="33" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="33" data-viibes-start="32" data-viibes-end="31">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par33">33</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>In <EM>R. v. Erez</EM>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2019/2019onca204/2019onca204.html">2019 ONCA 204</A>, the
appellant was convicted of a large-scale Ponzi-like scheme involving numerous
victims over several years, resulting in over $6 million in losses. He had a
prior criminal record for &ldquo;fraud-related offences&rdquo; and committed the &ldquo;bulk of
the present fraudulent transactions&rdquo; while serving a conditional sentence or on
probation for previous forgeries. While he pled guilty, the mitigating effect
of that plea was attenuated as he worked to have the guilty plea struck. He
failed in that endeavour and then unsuccessfully appealed on the basis that the
plea judge erred in refusing to strike the plea. Like this case, the victim
impact was devastating, and the appellant used the money for his own personal
gain. He received an eight-year sentence, described by this court as the &ldquo;top
of the sentencing range&rdquo;. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="34" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="34" data-viibes-start="33" data-viibes-end="32">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par34">34</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>In <EM>R. v. Eizenga</EM>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2011/2011onca113/2011onca113.html">2011 ONCA 113</A>, 273
O.A.C. 98, the appellant engaged in a very serious breach of trust, committing
a large-scale fraud involving several hundred victims and about $37 million.
Much of the money had been moved off-shore. Although he pled guilty, he later
challenged that plea. This court found that the plea was valid. While he
appealed from the restitution order, he did not appeal from the eight-year
custodial term that had been imposed after trial. This court commented on the
fact that the prospects for his rehabilitation appeared good. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="35" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="35" data-viibes-start="34" data-viibes-end="33">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par35">35</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>Crown counsel also points to the recent
sentencing decision in <EM>R. v. Holden</EM>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onsc/doc/2020/2020onsc132/2020onsc132.html">2020 ONSC 132</A>, where Dambrot J.
imposed a 12-year sentence for a large-scale fraud involving a Ponzi scheme.
The total amount of the fraud in that case was $54,159,737 with 65 victims,
many of whom were vulnerable and unsophisticated. The victim impact was
devastating; many of the victims lost their life savings. The accused used the
money for his own self-enrichment and to advance the Ponzi scheme. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="36" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="36" data-viibes-start="35" data-viibes-end="34">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par36">36</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>Importantly, unlike this case, the accused in
Holden had both a criminal and a <EM><A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="484-current-1" href="/en/on/laws/stat/rso-1990-c-s5/latest/rso-1990-c-s5.html">Securities Act</A></EM> record when he
committed the crimes for which he was sentenced: <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="484-current-1" href="/en/on/laws/stat/rso-1990-c-s5/latest/rso-1990-c-s5.html"><EM>Securities Act</EM>,
R.S.O. 1990, c. S.5</A>. In 1995 he was convicted of 46 <EM><A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="484-current-1" href="/en/on/laws/stat/rso-1990-c-s5/latest/rso-1990-c-s5.html">Securities Act</A></EM>
offences.&nbsp; In 2000, he pled guilty to three counts of fraud over $5,000 and was
sentenced to six years in custody for what this court described as a &ldquo;massive
complex fraud perpetrated on hundreds of victims&rdquo;: <EM>R. v. Holden</EM>,
<SPAN class="reflex3-missing" concatenated-id="reflex1-1725566" data-path="/en/reflex/1725566.html">[2000] O.J. No. 3481 (C.A.)</SPAN>. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="37" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="37" data-viibes-start="36" data-viibes-end="35">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par37">37</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>Counsel also points to sentences imposed by
trial courts, including in other provinces, for large-scale Ponzi-related
frauds: <EM>R. v. Johnson</EM>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ab/abqb/doc/2010/2010abqb546/2010abqb546.html">2010 ABQB 546</A>, rev&rsquo;d <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ab/abca/doc/2010/2010abca392/2010abca392.html">2010 ABCA 392</A>, 265 C.C.C.
(3d) 443 (13 years decreased to 10 years on appeal); <EM>R. v. Jones</EM>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/qc/qccq/doc/2010/2010qccq851/2010qccq851.html">2010
QCCQ 851</A> (joint submission for 11 years involving over $50 million in losses
and over 150 victims); <EM>R. v. Lewis</EM>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onsc/doc/2014/2014onsc4188/2014onsc4188.html">2014 ONSC 4188</A> (seven years)<A href="#_ftn1" name="_ftnref1" title=""><SPAN class="MsoFootnoteReference"><SPAN style="font-size:13.0pt;font-family:
&quot;Arial&quot;,&quot;sans-serif&quot;">[1]</SPAN></SPAN></A>.&nbsp; The accused stood in
significant positions of trust in each of those cases and the victim impact was
equally devastating to the victim impact in this case.&nbsp; </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="38" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="38" data-viibes-start="37" data-viibes-end="36">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par38">38</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>The appellant also points to <EM>R. v. Drabinsky</EM>,
<A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2011/2011onca582/2011onca582.html">2011 ONCA 582</A>, 107 O.R. (3d) 595, where this court referred to the trial
judge&rsquo;s determination of a 5- to 8-year range for premeditated frauds involving
public companies. While this court referred to the fact that the range may
fluctuate somewhat at both ends of that spectrum, crimes of this nature were
said to normally attract &ldquo;significant penitentiary terms&rdquo;: at para. 164.&nbsp; </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="39" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-message-pen" data-noteup-count="1"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="39" data-viibes-start="38" data-viibes-end="37">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par39">39</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>Crown counsel says that <EM>Drabinksy</EM> must
be approached with caution, particularly given that the offences for which the
appellants were sentenced were committed at a time prior to the increase in the
maximum sentence for fraud over $5,000 from 10 to 14 years: <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="380" ss="1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec380subsec1_smooth">s. 380(1)</A>(a), <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html"><EM>Criminal
Code</EM>, R.S.C. 1985 c. C-46</A>, as amended by S.C. 2004, c. 3, s. 2. Crown
counsel relies upon the recent comments in <EM>Friesen</EM>, at para. <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2020/2020scc9/2020scc9.html#par97">97</A>, where
the majority points to increases in maximum sentences as reflecting
Parliament&rsquo;s desire for &ldquo;such offences to be punished more harshly&quot;. An
increase in maximum sentence should be understood, therefore, as &ldquo;shifting the
distribution of proportionate sentences for an offence&rdquo;: Friesen, at para. <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2020/2020scc9/2020scc9.html#par97">97</A>.
Therefore, Crown counsel correctly points out that those authorities that were
decided at a point in time prior to the increase in the maximum sentence must
be considered in that light. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="40" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="40" data-viibes-start="39" data-viibes-end="38">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par40">40</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>One thing becomes clear through a review of the
previous authorities. While there is a fairly broad range of sentence for
large-scale frauds of this nature involving significant breaches of trust, in
the 8- to 12-year range, a 14-year sentence has not been imposed, even in cases
where the facts were more egregious than the ones here. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="41" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="41" data-viibes-start="40" data-viibes-end="39">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par41">41</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>Of course, there are all manner of aggravating
and mitigating factors that can apply in a case that will land the sentence
lower or higher within that range, or that may drive the sentence below or
above that range. Even so, the historical portrait painted by the range
provides good guidance when sentencing for offences of this nature.</P></div>

<P class="MsoHeading7">(c)<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">
</SPAN>The Appropriate Disposition</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="42" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="42" data-viibes-start="41" data-viibes-end="40">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par42">42</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN><EM>Friesen</EM> is
clear in its guidance to appellate courts. Despite the need to sentence afresh
where an error in principle that had an impact on the sentence is found, a
large degree of deference still applies. As stated by the court in <EM>Friesen</EM>,
at para. <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2020/2020scc9/2020scc9.html#par28">28</A>:</P></div>

<P class="CQuote">[I]n sentencing afresh, the appellate court
will defer to the sentencing judge&rsquo;s findings of fact or identification of
aggravating and mitigating factors, to the extent that they are not affected by
an error in principle. This deference limits the number, length, and cost of
appeals; promotes the autonomy and integrity of sentencing proceedings; and
recognizes the sentencing judge&rsquo;s expertise and advantageous position.
[Citations omitted.]</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="43" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="43" data-viibes-start="42" data-viibes-end="41">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par43">43</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>Taking this deferential approach, I accept the
sentencing judge&rsquo;s findings of fact, including, as previously reviewed, about
the amount of the fraud, the number of victims, the appellant&rsquo;s abuse of a
position of trust as well as his professional obligations, the length of time
over which the fraud was perpetrated, the appellant&rsquo;s honing in on many
particularly vulnerable people, the fact that he personally benefited from the
crime, and the trail of human suffering left in the appellant&rsquo;s wake.&nbsp; </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="44" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-message-pen" data-noteup-count="2"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="44" data-viibes-start="43" data-viibes-end="42">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par44">44</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>There is a reason that denunciation and general
deterrence are the primary sentencing principles when it comes to large-scale
Ponzi-related frauds of this nature. While serious frauds may not involve
physical violence, it is a mistake of serious proportion to think that they do
not leave just as many seriously wounded behind, often with financial and
mental scars that will never heal. The futures they worked so hard to build are
stolen from them because they trusted a professional who they justifiably
believed had their best interests in mind.&nbsp;&nbsp; </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="45" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="45" data-viibes-start="44" data-viibes-end="43">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par45">45</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>The devastating impact of frauds of this nature
stretch beyond the direct victims who unwittingly find themselves in harm&rsquo;s
way. They have the potential to adversely affect the stability of the Canadian
economy, financial systems and markets, as well as investor confidence in such
markets. That is why <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="380.1" ss="1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec380.1subsec1_smooth">s. 380.1(1)</A>(b) of the <EM><A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html">Criminal Code</A></EM> requires that
sentencing judges take this factor into account as an aggravating factor on
sentence. While there was no direct evidence that this fraud impacted the
Canadian economy, some of the victims addressed the fact that their confidence
in investing had been shaken to the core. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="46" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="46" data-viibes-start="45" data-viibes-end="44">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par46">46</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>Crown counsel places fresh evidence before the
court, arguing that it assists with demonstrating the danger that the appellant
continues to pose for the future. The fresh evidence consists of a two-page
police affidavit and a Parole Board decision, revoking the appellant&rsquo;s day parole.
<A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="687" ss="1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec687subsec1_smooth">Section 687(1)</A> of the <EM><A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html">Criminal Code</A></EM> allows the court on a sentence
appeal to receive evidence it thinks &ldquo;fit to require or receive&rdquo;. The
well-known criteria for admitting fresh evidence on a conviction appeal also
apply on sentencing: <EM>Lacasse</EM>, at paras. <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2015/2015scc64/2015scc64.html#par115">115-16</A>; <EM>R. v. Palmer</EM>,
<A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/1979/1979canlii8/1979canlii8.html">1979 CanLII 8 (SCC)</A>, [1980] 1 S.C.R. 759. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="47" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="47" data-viibes-start="46" data-viibes-end="45">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par47">47</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>The evidence suggests that the appellant
originally received accelerated day parole. In violation of his parole
officer&rsquo;s direction, he pursued the publication and sale of a book that is
described as containing strategies on becoming financially secure. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="48" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="48" data-viibes-start="47" data-viibes-end="46">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par48">48</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>The appellant also met with a person who is
described as being &ldquo;financially vulnerable&rdquo; and suggested an arrangement to
lend her money to buy into a series of informational courses. She would then
assist him in soliciting others to buy the book and take the courses at a cost
of $5,000 per person. She reported that discussion to the police. The police
then reported the matter to the appellant&rsquo;s parole officer. No criminal charges
were laid. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="49" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="49" data-viibes-start="48" data-viibes-end="47">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par49">49</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>In its decision revoking parole, the Parole
Board refers to positive aspects of the appellant&rsquo;s release into the community,
his lack of any criminal charges, compliance with specific conditions of
release and positive behaviour in the Community Residential Facility where he
was staying. The decision also makes reference to the willingness of the
appellant&rsquo;s employer to continue to work with him and, if needed, provide more
structure in the working environment. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="50" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="50" data-viibes-start="49" data-viibes-end="48">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par50">50</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>Despite that positive behaviour, the Board
concluded that having pursued the book matter, contrary to his parole officer&rsquo;s
specific direction, the appellant showed a lack of insight into his offending
cycle and prior behaviours. In the end, the Board revoked the appellant&rsquo;s
parole because of his deemed risk to the community.&nbsp; </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="51" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="51" data-viibes-start="50" data-viibes-end="49">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par51">51</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>Crown counsel argues that the fresh evidence
should be admitted because it has a direct bearing on the question of what
constitutes a fit sentence. The evidence is said to underscore that the
appellant&rsquo;s attitude toward the offences remains unchanged, that he has no
respect for authority, continues with deceptive behaviour and has little hope
for rehabilitation. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="52" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="52" data-viibes-start="51" data-viibes-end="50">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par52">52</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>The appellant cautions this court about using
the fresh evidence. He opposes the characterization of some of the evidence,
particularly the reference to the person the appellant met as being
&ldquo;financially vulnerable&rdquo;. The appellant argues that the allegations constitute
aggravating factors and, given how the Crown is endeavouring to use them, they
must be proven beyond a reasonable doubt: <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="724" ss="3" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec724subsec3_smooth"><EM>Criminal Code</EM>, s. 724(3)</A>(e);
<EM>R. v. Gardiner</EM>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/1982/1982canlii30/1982canlii30.html">1982 CanLII 30 (SCC)</A>, [1982] 2 S.C.R. 368. The appellant argues that the
Crown has failed to do so. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="53" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="53" data-viibes-start="52" data-viibes-end="51">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par53">53</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>It is important to start with the observation
that the appellant can only be punished for the conduct that he was convicted
of. He cannot be punished, or be seen to be punished, for conduct that is
alleged to have occurred a year after his sentence was imposed. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="54" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="54" data-viibes-start="53" data-viibes-end="52">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par54">54</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>As well, I would observe that when a person
breaches their parole, the Parole Board has the authority to deliver a decisive
response, one that will often have a direct impact on the offender&rsquo;s liberty
interest. That is what happened in this case. The appellant&rsquo;s breach of parole
was acted upon and he was reincarcerated. Indeed, his last almost ten months of
incarceration have been the direct result of the revocation of his parole as a
result of the activity that is now said to be relevant fresh evidence informing
the fitness of sentence to be imposed on appeal. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="55" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="55" data-viibes-start="54" data-viibes-end="53">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par55">55</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>As noted in <EM>R. v. Sipos</EM>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2014/2014scc47/2014scc47.html">2014 SCC 47</A>,
[2014] 2 S.C.R. 423, at para. <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2014/2014scc47/2014scc47.html#par30">30</A>, &ldquo;[f]resh evidence addressing events that have
occurred between the time of sentencing and the time of appeal may raise
difficult issues which bring competing values into sharp relief.&rdquo; There are
clear institutional limitations placed upon appellate courts, such that
deciding sentencing appeals based upon &ldquo;after-the-fact developments could both
jeopardize the integrity of the criminal process by undermining its finality
and surpass the appropriate bounds of appellate review&rdquo;: <EM>Sipos</EM>, at
para. <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2014/2014scc47/2014scc47.html#par30">30</A>. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="56" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="56" data-viibes-start="55" data-viibes-end="54">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par56">56</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>In my view, the fresh evidence in this case
could, at its highest, do no more than demonstrate a continued lack of insight
by the appellant into his offences which, even this many years later, could
signal a potential risk of reoffending: <EM>R. v. Boone</EM>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2020/2020onca154/2020onca154.html">2020 ONCA 154</A>, at
para. <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2020/2020onca154/2020onca154.html#par28">28</A>. We do not need fresh evidence to satisfy us of this point. The trial
and sentencing record are clear in this regard. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="57" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="57" data-viibes-start="56" data-viibes-end="55">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par57">57</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>Crown counsel also argues that virtually every
aggravating factor was present in this case, justifying the maximum sentence.
Those factors have been previously reviewed. They are rightly described as
aggravating factors on sentence. The fact is, though, that these aggravating
factors are present in almost all frauds of this nature. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="58" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="58" data-viibes-start="57" data-viibes-end="56">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par58">58</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>The task is not to simply check off the
aggravating factors with a view to imposing the maximum sentence if each box is
ticked. The key is to consider the circumstances underlying each factor and
position it on the scale of seriousness. If proportionality and parity are to
have meaning, calibrating the seriousness of the aggravating factors is
critical to the sentencing exercise. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="59" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="AParaNumbering" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="59" data-viibes-start="58" data-viibes-end="57">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par59">59</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>Accepting the trial judge&rsquo;s view of the
aggravating factors in this case, removing the factor that was infected by
error, looking at the facts as he found them, and considering them against
prior authorities, specifically this court&rsquo;s prior authorities, I find that a
fit sentence is one of ten years.</P></div>

<P class="Heading">VI. CONCLUSION</P>

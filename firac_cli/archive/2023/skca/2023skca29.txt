<H1>I.<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN><SPAN style="text-transform:none">INTRODUCTION </SPAN></H1>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="1" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="1" data-viibes-start="0"><A name="Beginning">[</A><A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par1"><A name="Beginning">1</A></A><A name="Beginning">]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN>On September 21, 2019, Destin Mosquito, Mohamad Al-Zawahreh,
Kevin Nataucappo and two other people forcibly entered a dwelling house on
Howell Avenue in Saskatoon for the purpose of committing an assault. Mr.
Mosquito was armed with a loaded, sawed-off shotgun. The gun discharged,
resulting in the death of Mr. Nataucappo. The shooting was unintentional. </A></P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="2" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="2" data-viibes-start="1" data-viibes-end="0">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par2">2</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN>On May 3, 2021, a judge of the Court of Queen&rsquo;s
Bench for Saskatchewan [sentencing judge] accepted Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s guilty plea
to two charges arising from that home invasion, which were as follows: </P></div>

<P class="Lista">(a)<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN>breaking, entering and committing the indictable
offence of assault with a weapon, contrary to <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="348" ss="1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec348subsec1_smooth">s. 348(1)</A>(b) of the <I><A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html">Criminal
Code</A></I>; and</P>

<P class="Lista">(b)<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN>committing manslaughter by unlawfully causing
the death of Mr. Nataucappo contrary to <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="236" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec236_smooth">s.&nbsp;236</A> of the <I><A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html">Criminal Code</A></I>.
</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="3" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="3" data-viibes-start="2" data-viibes-end="1">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par3">3</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN>That same afternoon, the sentencing judge heard
the parties&rsquo; sentencing submissions and, in a decision that was delivered
immediately thereafter, determined that Mr.&nbsp;Mosquito should serve seven years
in custody for the manslaughter and five years concurrent for breaking and
entering. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="4" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="4" data-viibes-start="3" data-viibes-end="2">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par4">4</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN>The Crown has applied for leave to appeal
pursuant to <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="676" ss="1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec676subsec1_smooth">s.&nbsp;676(1)</A>(d) of the <I><A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html">Criminal Code</A>. </I>It alleges that the
sentencing judge committed numerous errors in principle that impacted the
sentence and that the sentences imposed are demonstrably unfit. I have
concluded that leave should be granted but that the appeal should be dismissed.
In my opinion, the sentencing judge committed no errors in principle that
impacted the sentence and the sentence is not demonstrably unfit. My reasons
for these conclusions follow. </P></div>

<H1>II.<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN><SPAN style="text-transform:none">BACKGROUND</SPAN></H1>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="5" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="5" data-viibes-start="4" data-viibes-end="3">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par5">5</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN>The home invasion which resulted in Mr.
Nataucappo&rsquo;s death was undertaken at the behest of Mr.&nbsp;Al-Zawahreh,
who had been assaulted and forcibly ejected from the Howell Avenue residence
earlier that day following an altercation with several people. Mr.&nbsp;Al&#8209;Zawahreh
related his version of those events to Mr. Mosquito and several other people,
with the result that Mr.&nbsp;Mosquito, Mr. Nataucappo and two others decided
they would go to the Howell Avenue residence with Mr.&nbsp;Al&#8209;Zawahreh to
help him retaliate. Several of the five participants armed themselves with
weapons. Mr. Mosquito made the fateful decision to take a loaded sawed-off
shotgun despite being subject to an order pursuant to <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="109" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec109_smooth">s.&nbsp;109</A> of the <I><A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html">Criminal
Code</A></I>, which prohibited him from possessing firearms or ammunition. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="6" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="6" data-viibes-start="5" data-viibes-end="4">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par6">6</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN>There were several people present at the Howell
Avenue house when the home invasion occurred, including more than one child. Very
shortly after Mr. Mosquito and the other intruders entered, a fight broke out
between Mr. Al-Zawahreh and one of the occupants. Although it is not clear
exactly what occurred in the ensuing melee, it is undisputed that the gun in
the possession of Mr.&nbsp;Mosquito discharged. The shot struck Mr. Nataucappo
in the back. Mr. Mosquito and his other associates immediately fled the scene,
abandoning Mr. Nataucappo, whose wound would prove to be fatal. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="7" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="7" data-viibes-start="6" data-viibes-end="5">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par7">7</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN>Mr. Mosquito, who was then 21 years old, was
arrested and charged shortly after these events occurred and has remained in
custody since he was first remanded on September&nbsp;22, 2019. In April 2020,
the Deputy Attorney General for Saskatchewan preferred an indictment pursuant
to <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="577" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec577_smooth">s.&nbsp;577</A> of the <I><A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html">Criminal Code</A></I> against Mr. Mosquito and three other
people, all of whom were charged with the manslaughter and breaking and
entering offences described above. Mr.&nbsp;Mosquito was separately charged on
the same indictment with two additional offences, those being unlawful
possession of a loaded prohibited firearm contrary to <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="95" ss="1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec95subsec1_smooth">s.&nbsp;95(1)</A>(a) of the <I><A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html">Criminal
Code</A></I> and breaching a <SPAN class="reflex2-link" link-count="1" link1="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec109" title1="Criminal Code" concatenated-id="12792" s="109" data-link-type="weak">s.&nbsp;109</SPAN> firearms prohibition order contrary to
<A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="117.01" ss="1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec117.01subsec1_smooth">s.&nbsp;117.01(1)</A> of the <I><A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html">Criminal Code</A></I>. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="8" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="8" data-viibes-start="7" data-viibes-end="6">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par8">8</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN>Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s guilty pleas to the manslaughter
and break and enter charges were entered on March 10, 2021. Crown counsel
advised the Court that the Crown would apply to stay the two firearms charges
on the completion of sentencing but that counsel had agreed that the evidence relating
to those counts would be facts for the purpose of sentencing. The sentencing
hearing was then adjourned to May 3, 2021, to enable the preparation of a pre&#8209;sentence
report with a particular emphasis on <I>Gladue</I> factors, as requested by
defence counsel (see: <I>R v Gladue</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/1999/1999canlii679/1999canlii679.html">1999 CanLII 679 (SCC)</A>, [1999] 1 SCR 688). </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="9" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="9" data-viibes-start="8" data-viibes-end="7">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par9">9</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN>On May 3, 2021, after concluding his inquiry
pursuant to <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="606" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec606_smooth">s.&nbsp;606</A> of the <I><A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html">Criminal Code</A></I>, the sentencing judge
accepted Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s guilty pleas and proceeded with the sentencing hearing.
The pre&#8209;sentence report had been filed and reviewed in advance by the
sentencing judge. Crown counsel tendered Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s criminal record, noting
that he had three convictions for crimes of violence, and two convictions for
break and enter. She relied heavily on the decision in <I>R v Sand</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/sk/skqb/doc/2019/2019skqb123/2019skqb123.html">2019
SKQB 123</A> [<I>Sand</I>], which also related to the death of one of the
perpetrators of a home invasion and had resulted in a nine-year sentence for
manslaughter and seven years concurrent for break and enter with the intent to
commit an indictable offence. Crown counsel submitted that Mr.&nbsp;Mosquito
should receive the same nine-year sentence. It was her position that 12 years would
have been a fit sentence in light of his high moral culpability and the
aggravating factors in play but that a lesser sentence was appropriate in light
of the guilty plea, <I>Gladue</I> factors and the effect of SARS&#8209;CoV-2 [Covid]
on Mr. Mosquito during the 18 months he spent in remand. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="10" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="10" data-viibes-start="9" data-viibes-end="8">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par10">10</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>At the close of her submissions, Crown counsel
advised the sentencing judge that she had been mistaken when she had said that the
offender in <I>Sand</I><I> </I>had no criminal record, as he had seven convictions for
violent crimes, including robbery, threats and assault causing bodily harm. In
a noteworthy example of professional courtesy, defence counsel had brought this
misstep to her attention in an off&#8209;the&#8209;record discussion rather
than raising it in the course of his submissions. There was no suggestion that
Crown counsel had intentionally attempted to mislead the Court. She apologized
for her error, and she referred to that record as &ldquo;a big factor&rdquo; affecting his
sentence. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="11" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="11" data-viibes-start="10" data-viibes-end="9"><A name="_Hlk125705399">[</A><A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par11"><A name="_Hlk125705399">11</A></A><A name="_Hlk125705399">]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>Defence counsel contended that a sentence in the
range of five to seven years would be appropriate. He noted Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s
youth. He referred to various aggravating and mitigating factors. He emphasized
that Mr. Mosquito had contracted Covid twice while on remand and had been
subject to unusual restrictions as a result of the virus, including having been
in lockdown for days at a time. He too stressed the significance of </A><A name="_Hlk125705399"><I>Gladue</I></A><A name="_Hlk125705399">
factors. </A><A name="_Hlk125705399">He argued that the </A><A name="_Hlk125705399"><I>Sand</I></A><A name="_Hlk125705399"> decision </A><A name="_Hlk125705399">is readily distinguishable.</A></P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="12" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="12" data-viibes-start="11" data-viibes-end="10">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par12">12</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>Before delivering his judgment, the sentencing
judge asked Mr. Mosquito if he had anything to say. Mr. Mosquito apologized to
Mr. Nataucappo&rsquo;s family, stating that he knew what it was like to lose a loved
one suddenly to a horrible accident and acknowledging that he was the reason
for their pain. He said that he saw this as an opportunity to change his life
for his own family and for Mr. Nataucappo. </P></div>

<H1>III.<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN><SPAN style="text-transform:none">THE SENTENCING
DECISION</SPAN></H1>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="13" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="13" data-viibes-start="12" data-viibes-end="11">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par13">13</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>The sentencing judge did not adjourn after
hearing from defence counsel and Mr.&nbsp;Mosquito, choosing instead to
immediately deliver his oral decision. He began by confirming that the
principles specified in <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="718" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec718_smooth">ss.&nbsp;718</A>, <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="718.1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec718.1_smooth">718.1</A> and <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="718.2" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec718.2_smooth">718.2</A> of the <I><A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html">Criminal Code</A></I>
instructed him as to the things he must consider when imposing sentence, and
that he was mandated to balance the seriousness of the offence against Mr.
Mosquito&rsquo;s personal circumstances. He noted that manslaughter included a range
of circumstances from close to murder to accidental death, and commented that,
in his view, this case fell somewhere in the middle of that spectrum. He
suggested that the positions of both the Crown and the defence as to the
appropriate sentence had some merit and were grounded in principle and an
assessment of the facts. He characterized both as attempts to describe a
sentence that would recognize the loss of a life but averred that he must also
account for what is appropriate for Mr. Mosquito. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="14" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="14" data-viibes-start="13" data-viibes-end="12">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par14">14</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>The sentencing judge then stated that, if the
circumstances had been different, the sentence could have been either higher or
lower, ranging between four years and twelve years in custody. As he put it,
&ldquo;my responsibility is to find an appropriate balance in dealing with all of the
things that I have to consider, the circumstances of the offence and also
equally particularly the circumstances of Mr. Mosquito&rdquo;. He commented that the
judgment in <I>Sand</I><I> </I>was compelling and that there were parallels between
that case and this one. However, he also pointed out that there were
distinctions and said that he wanted &ldquo;to talk about the aggravating and the mitigating
circumstances that counsel have talked about&rdquo;. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="15" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="15" data-viibes-start="14" data-viibes-end="13">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par15">15</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>The sentencing judge then addressed those
circumstances. He stated that &ldquo;taking a gun to &hellip; a gathering where violence
could well have erupted &hellip; [t]hat&rsquo;s a very, very aggravating circumstance&rdquo;. He
said that he accepted Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s claim that he did not intend to harm
anyone but said that taking a loaded firearm was &ldquo;an extremely serious matter&rdquo;.
He then referred to Crown counsel&rsquo;s &ldquo;very telling&rdquo; submission that &ldquo;young
people&rsquo;s thought processes don&rsquo;t necessarily catch up with the years and the
responsibility that should be there&rdquo; and remarked that he had to bear in mind
that they sometimes think differently than older or more mature people. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="16" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="16" data-viibes-start="15" data-viibes-end="14">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par16">16</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>The sentencing judge then reiterated that it was
a significant aggravating circumstance to take a firearm to a group of people
&ldquo;where violence was likely to occur&rdquo;. As he put it, the fundamental mistake
that Mr. Mosquito made was to go along in support of Mr. Al-Zawahreh. In his
view, Mr. Mosquito had learned from this and would have made a different
decision today but that it was for him as the sentencing judge to find a
resolution that is fair to &ldquo;the memory of Kevin Nataucappo, fair to society and
fair to Mr. Mosquito himself&rdquo;. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="17" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="17" data-viibes-start="16" data-viibes-end="15">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par17">17</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>The sentencing judge then addressed mitigating
circumstances. He commented that there was a &ldquo;particularly compelling <I>Gladue</I>
picture of Mr. Mosquito&rdquo; and that, in light of what he had faced, it would have
been hard for Mr. Mosquito to have made decisions &ldquo;earlier in [his] life&rdquo; other
than the ones he had made, such as those relating to substance abuse. He
referred to the &ldquo;comprehensive&rdquo; pre&#8209;sentence report and the &ldquo;<I>Gladue</I>
canvass&rdquo; that it had provided to him, confirming that s.&nbsp;718.2(e) required
that he take this into account. He noted that this provision is not a formula
that calls for &ldquo;a discount on a sentence, but it is a reflection &hellip; of a court
trying to assess the road that in this case you, Mr. Mosquito, have walked&rdquo;. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="18" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="18" data-viibes-start="17" data-viibes-end="16">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par18">18</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>The sentencing judge then dealt with the impact
of Covid. He described Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s Covid experience as a &ldquo;significant&rdquo;
issue, noting that he was troubled by the fact that Mr. Mosquito had contracted
Covid twice while on remand and that he had, as a result, also suffered from
the imposition of more restrictions on his freedom than other inmates. He then
said this: </P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">... Physically you
obviously became ill, I mean, it&rsquo;s not for you and I to discuss today, how ill
you became on any given occasion, that&rsquo;s a privileged matter for you and your
health providers, but I know one thing, that you&rsquo;ve experienced it twice
physically and there has to have been in your mind, Mr. Mosquito, a dread, an
uncertainty of what COVID could perhaps do to you. You&rsquo;re living under it; it&rsquo;s
a cloud over you during those times. Is it something that could be fatal to
you? You don&rsquo;t know that, you don&rsquo;t know how sick you may become in that
regard, but you experienced it and to me that is a very significant mitigating
factor in the sentence that I&rsquo;m going to impose on you today.</P>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">So there&rsquo;s two
mitigating factors: one of them is a particularly compelling <I><SPAN style="font-family:&quot;Times-Italic&quot;,sans-serif">Gladue</SPAN></I> picture; secondly, your personal COVID exposure. When you look at
the statistics in Canada, the world, the death rate of people suffering from
COVID it happens, it happens throughout all of the communities in that regard.
And when someone contracts it you don&rsquo;t know how serious it&rsquo;s going to be and
you&rsquo;ve contracted it twice, not once. So again those are two very significant
mitigating factors in my view.</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="19" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="19" data-viibes-start="18" data-viibes-end="17">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par19">19</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>The sentencing judge next described Mr.
Mosquito&rsquo;s age as a third mitigating factor. He stated that Mr. Mosquito had
not used the best judgment, but that he knew he had made mistakes and was
trying to learn from them. He identified the guilty pleas as a fourth
mitigating factor, commenting that Mr. Mosquito had made a good decision by
taking responsibility for what he had done. He commented that it demonstrated
that Mr. Mosquito could become a better father and member of society, as did his
participation in STR8 UP and ministry programs while on remand. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="20" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="20" data-viibes-start="19" data-viibes-end="18">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par20">20</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>At this juncture, the sentencing judge
summarized his view as to aggravating and mitigating circumstances, commenting
as follows:</P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">So I&rsquo;ve taken longer
than probably I needed to, to talk about aggravating circumstances and, of
course, the ultimately aggravating circumstances is that a life was lost with
the gun, taking the gun with you. But the mitigating circumstances are also the
ones that I&rsquo;ve talked about. Your case is in my view somewhat similar to Mr.
Sand, but it&rsquo;s quite a bit different in that regard. Your guilty plea, the
entry of the guilty plea, your COVID experience, your particularly compelling <I>Gladue</I>
picture in that regard suggests to me that <I>Gladue </I>and <I>Ipeelee </I>and
Section 718.2 require me to take all of those things into account and provide
you with a sentence that is appropriate under the circumstances.</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="21" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="21" data-viibes-start="20" data-viibes-end="19">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par21">21</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>Finally, the sentencing judge stated that, but
for the mitigating factors, he would have imposed a sentence equivalent to that
imposed on Mr. Sand. He opined that the situation of Devin&nbsp;Wesaquate, one
of the people who was with Mr. Mosquito that night and who had previously been
sentenced, was different, as Mr.&nbsp;Wesaquate had not entered the Howell
Avenue residence and accordingly had no control over what happened inside. He
felt that these differences meant that neither the nine-year <I>Sand</I>
sentence nor the five-year sentence imposed on Mr.&nbsp;Wesaquate represented
an appropriate yardstick. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="22" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="22" data-viibes-start="21" data-viibes-end="20">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par22">22</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>In the result, the sentencing judge imposed a
sentence of seven years, less remand credit at a rate of 1.5 to 1. He explained
that it was his opinion that Covid should be dealt with as a mitigating factor
and not by way of enhanced remand credit. He also made a DNA order pursuant to
<A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="487.051" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec487.051_smooth">s.&nbsp;487.051</A> of the <I><A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html">Criminal Code</A></I> and imposed a lifetime firearms,
ammunition and explosives prohibition pursuant to <SPAN class="reflex2-link" link-count="1" link1="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec109" title1="Criminal Code" concatenated-id="12792" s="109" data-link-type="weak">s.&nbsp;109</SPAN>.</P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="23" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="23" data-viibes-start="22" data-viibes-end="21">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par23">23</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>When the sentencing judge asked counsel if they
had anything further, Crown counsel noted that the break, enter and commit conviction
had not been dealt with. The sentencing judge imposed a five-year sentence for
that conviction, to be served concurrently. </P></div>

<H1>IV.<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN><SPAN style="text-transform:none">STANDARD OF REVIEW</SPAN></H1>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="24" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="24" data-viibes-start="23" data-viibes-end="22">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par24">24</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>The standard of review on a sentence appeal is
that specified in <I>R v Lacasse</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2015/2015scc64/2015scc64.html">2015 SCC 64</A>, [2015] 3 SCR 1089 [<I>Lacasse</I>],
which was affirmed as follows in <I>R v Friesen</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2020/2020scc9/2020scc9.html">2020 SCC 9</A>, [2020] 1 SCR
424 [<I>Friesen</I>]: </P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">[26]&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; As this
Court confirmed in <I>Lacasse</I>, an appellate court can only intervene to
vary a sentence if (1) the sentence is demonstrably unfit (para.&nbsp;41), or
(2) the sentencing judge made an error in principle that had an impact on the
sentence (para.&nbsp;44). Errors in principle include an error of law, a
failure to consider a relevant factor, or erroneous consideration of an
aggravating or mitigating factor. The weighing or balancing of factors can form
an error in principle &ldquo;[o]nly if by emphasizing one factor or by not giving
enough weight to another, the trial judge exercises his or her discretion
unreasonably&rdquo; (<I>R. v. McKnight</I> (1999), <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onca/doc/1999/1999canlii3717/1999canlii3717.html">1999 CanLII 3717 (ON CA)</A>, 135 C.C.C. (3d) 41 (Ont. C.A.), at
para.&nbsp;<A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/on/onca/doc/1999/1999canlii3717/1999canlii3717.html#par35">35</A>, cited in <I>Lacasse</I>, at para.&nbsp;<A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2015/2015scc64/2015scc64.html#par49">49</A>). Not every error in
principle is material: an appellate court can only intervene if it is apparent
from the trial judge&rsquo;s reasons that the error had an impact on the sentence (<I>Lacasse</I>,
at para.&nbsp;<A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2015/2015scc64/2015scc64.html#par44">44</A>). &hellip;</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="25" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="25" data-viibes-start="24" data-viibes-end="23">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par25">25</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>In this case, the requirement that the impact on
sentence must be &ldquo;apparent from the trial judge&rsquo;s decision&rdquo; is of particular
significance (<I>Friesen</I> at para <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2020/2020scc9/2020scc9.html#par26">26</A>). There is a thorough and helpful
discussion of this requirement &ndash; which is stated in disarmingly simple terms
but can be difficult to apply &ndash; in <I>R v Williams</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/sk/skca/doc/2020/2020skca108/2020skca108.html">2020 SKCA 108</A> at paras
<A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/sk/skca/doc/2020/2020skca108/2020skca108.html#par27">27&ndash;32 and 49&ndash;53</A>, 393 CCC (3d) 325. As Schwann J.A. there explained: </P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">[29]&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; <I>Lacasse</I>
describes the phrase impact on sentence in various ways, relying, in large
measure, on the approach taken by the Quebec Court of Appeal in <I>R v Gavin</I>,
<A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/qc/qcca/doc/2009/2009qcca1/2009qcca1.html">2009 QCCA 1</A> [<I>Gavin</I>], and <I>R v Sidhu</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/qc/qcca/doc/2009/2009qcca2441/2009qcca2441.html">2009 QCCA 2441</A> [<I>Sidhu</I>],
specifically endorsing the following passage from <I>Gavin</I>: &ldquo;unless the
Court finds that the sentence imposed was harsher because of [the alleged
error] &hellip; this error in principle had no real effect on the sentence&rdquo; (at
para&nbsp;35). Adopting the language in <I>Sidhu</I>, the Court in <I>Lacasse</I>
also observed as follows: </P>

<P class="QuoteQuote" style="text-indent:0in">[46]&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; The
Quebec Court of Appeal also adopted this reasoning in <I>R. v. Sidhu</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/qc/qcca/doc/2009/2009qcca2441/2009qcca2441.html">2009
QCCA 2441</A>. As in <I>Gavin</I>, the trial judge had considered lack of remorse
as an aggravating factor (para. 23 (CanLII)), but the Court of Appeal found
that this error was not determinative and had had no effect on the sentence
(para. 24). Since the sentence the judge imposed would not have been different
had there been no mistake in that respect (para.&nbsp;26), the error was not
reviewable (para. 55). Thus, rather than simply substituting its opinion for
that of the trial judge because he had made an error in principle, the Court of
Appeal limited itself to considering whether, independently of that error, the
sentence was unreasonable or demonstrably unfit. On finding that that was not
the case, it decided not to intervene (para. 55). </P>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">[30]&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; A helpful
analysis of the concept of impact on sentence can be found in <I>R v Agin</I>,
<A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/bc/bcca/doc/2018/2018bcca133/2018bcca133.html">2018 BCCA 133</A> at paras <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/bc/bcca/doc/2018/2018bcca133/2018bcca133.html#par49">49&ndash;53</A>, 361 CCC (3d) 258 [<I>Agin</I>], where the Court
of Appeal for British Columbia reviewed the various ways in which the phrase
impact on sentence has been applied in that province. In the end, that Court
settled on the following formulation: </P>

<P class="QuoteQuote" style="text-indent:0in">[52]&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; In our
view, the question is whether the error was material in that it had an impact
on the sentence imposed and the sentence would have been different had there
been no error. In other words, did the error impact the judge&rsquo;s analysis in
imposing the sentence: <I>R. v. Eakins</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/bc/bcca/doc/2016/2016bcca194/2016bcca194.html">2016 BCCA 194</A>, at para. <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/bc/bcca/doc/2016/2016bcca194/2016bcca194.html#par21">21</A>. This
accords with the rationale, outlined at para. 43 of <I>Lacasse</I>, that
requiring an impact aims to prevent appellate courts from intervening where an
error cannot be said to have impacted the sentence, as to do so would undermine
the deference owed to sentencing judges as well as public policy goals
animating appellate review, including finality. </P>

<P class="QuoteQuote" style="text-indent:0in">&hellip;</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="26" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="26" data-viibes-start="25" data-viibes-end="24">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par26">26</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>Based on this review of the law, Schwann J.A.
held that the error that had been committed &ldquo;had a direct bearing on the
sentence imposed on Mr. Williams&rdquo; (at para 33). She reached that conclusion after
closely analyzing the sentencing decision and finding that the erroneous
conclusion that Mr. Williams was a mid-level drug dealer &ldquo;permeated the
entirety of the sentencing judge&rsquo;s reasoning&rdquo;, and that &ldquo;[t]he frequency with
which mid-level dealing or analogous statements appear in the decision makes it
almost impossible for the sentence not to have been impacted by this error&rdquo; (at
para 34). </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="27" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="27" data-viibes-start="26" data-viibes-end="25">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par27">27</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>I note that there are some appellate decisions
which have suggested that an error in principle must have a <I>material</I>
impact on the sentence before appellate intervention is permissible: see, for
example, <I>R v Choi</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/bc/bcca/doc/2022/2022bcca90/2022bcca90.html">2022 BCCA 90</A> at para <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/bc/bcca/doc/2022/2022bcca90/2022bcca90.html#par8">8</A>; and <I>R v May</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/bc/bcca/doc/2018/2018bcca391/2018bcca391.html">2018 BCCA
391</A> at para <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/bc/bcca/doc/2018/2018bcca391/2018bcca391.html#par26">26</A>. On a similar note, in <I>R v Orwin</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2017/2017onca841/2017onca841.html">2017 ONCA 841</A> at para <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2017/2017onca841/2017onca841.html#par52">52</A>,
the court commented that the error must affect the sentence &ldquo;in something more
than just an incidental way&rdquo;. With respect, it is my view that language of this
kind &ndash; by defining materiality in terms of the <I>extent</I> of the impact an
error had on sentence &ndash; could be understood as a different test than that
described in <I>Lacasse</I> and <I>Friesen</I>. The standard of review, as I
understand it, already states that an error <I>is </I>material if it impacted
the sentence and that, if there is such an error, then the appellate court must
sentence anew.</P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="28" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="28" data-viibes-start="27" data-viibes-end="26">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par28">28</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>In order to intervene, an appellate court must
be convinced not only that the sentencing judge erred but that the reasons
demonstrate that the error had an impact on sentence. The second of these
requirements represents a complete materiality hurdle that must be cleared
before an appellate court may intervene. There is no need to add an additional
materiality requirement on top of the standard affirmed in <I>Friesen</I>. This
is particularly so because sentencing reasons often demonstrate that a balance has
been struck which takes account of multiple factors, but in a fashion that does
not clearly disclose how, if an error in principle has occurred, the changed
perception of a specific factor (or factors) would affect the bottom&#8209;line
decision as to a fit sentence. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="29" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="29" data-viibes-start="28" data-viibes-end="27">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par29">29</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>By way of example, if an appellate court
concludes that an offender would have been sentenced to a lesser term of
imprisonment but for an error of law, but it is unable to say how much less, why
would it dismiss the appeal? Why would it not consider the sentence afresh?
That reconsideration constitutes justice in accordance with the law for the
individual offender, which is the lodestar for both trial and appellate courts.
If the appellate court concludes it would have nonetheless imposed the same
sentence as the sentencing judge based on the principles of sentencing and the relevant
factors, it is entitled to affirm the fitness of that sentence and to dismiss
the appeal under <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="687" ss="1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec687subsec1_smooth">s. 687(1)</A>(b) of the <I><A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html">Criminal Code</A></I>. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="30" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="30" data-viibes-start="29" data-viibes-end="28">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par30">30</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>As to demonstrable unfitness, being the second
basis for appellate intervention, Wagner J. (as he then was) provided this
guidance in <I>Lacasse</I>: </P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">[52]&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; It is
possible for a sentence to be demonstrably unfit even if the judge has made no
error in imposing it. As Laskin J.A. mentioned, writing for the Ontario Court
of Appeal, the courts have used a variety of expressions to describe a sentence
that is &ldquo;demonstrably unfit&rdquo;: &ldquo;clearly unreasonable&rdquo;, &ldquo;clearly or manifestly
excessive&rdquo;, &ldquo;clearly excessive or inadequate&rdquo;, or representing a &ldquo;substantial
and marked departure&rdquo; (<I>R. v. Rezaie</I> (1996), <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onca/doc/1996/1996canlii1241/1996canlii1241.html">1996 CanLII 1241 (ON CA)</A>, 31&nbsp;O.R. (3d) 713
(C.A.), at p. 720). All these expressions reflect the very high threshold that
applies to appellate courts when determining whether they should intervene
after reviewing the fitness of a sentence.</P>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">[53]<A name="_Hlk126841010">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; This inquiry must be focused on the fundamental
principle of proportionality stated in&nbsp;<A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="718.1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec718.1_smooth">s. 718.1</A> of the <I><A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html">Criminal Code</A></I>,
which provides that a sentence must be &ldquo;proportionate to the gravity of the
offence and the degree of responsibility of the offender&rdquo;. A sentence will
therefore be demonstrably unfit if it constitutes an unreasonable departure
from this principle. ...</A></P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="31" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="31" data-viibes-start="30" data-viibes-end="29">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par31">31</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>Another aspect of the process of appellate review
bears emphasis in this case; that is, an appellate court must read the reasons
of a trial or sentencing judge in a functional and contextual manner. The test
that is applied where the challenge is to the sufficiency of reasons is
instructive. In <I>R v G.F</I>., <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2021/2021scc20/2021scc20.html">2021 SCC 20</A>, 404 CCC (3d) 1, for example,
Karakatsanis J. summarized the law on this point as follows: </P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">[69]&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; This Court
has repeatedly and consistently emphasized the importance of a functional and
contextual reading of a trial judge's reasons when those reasons are alleged to
be insufficient: &hellip; Appellate courts &hellip; must assess whether the reasons, read in
context and as a whole, in light of the live issues at trial, explain what the
trial judge decided and why they decided that way in a manner that permits
effective appellate review. As McLachlin&nbsp;C.J. put it in [<I>R v R.E.M.</I>,
<A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2008/2008scc51/2008scc51.html">2008 SCC 51</A>, [2008] 3 SCR 3], &ldquo;The foundations of the judge&rsquo;s decision must be
discernable, when looked at in the context of the evidence, the submissions of
counsel and the history of how the trial unfolded&rdquo;: para. 17. And as Charron&nbsp;J.
stated in [<I>R v Dinardo</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2008/2008scc24/2008scc24.html">2008 SCC 24</A>, [2008] 1 SCR 788], &ldquo;the inquiry
into the sufficiency of the reasons should be directed at whether the reasons
respond to the case&rsquo;s live issues&rdquo;: para. 31.</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="32" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in;page-break-after:avoid" data-viibes-parag="32" data-viibes-start="31" data-viibes-end="30">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par32">32</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>In their joint concurring reasons in <I>G.F.</I>,
Brown and Rowe&nbsp;JJ. elaborated on this principle, commenting as follows: </P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">[109]&nbsp;&nbsp;  &hellip; [A]n
appellate reviewer&rsquo;s role is not discharged by giving trial reasons for
judgment a once&#8209;over&#8209;lightly perusal, but by <I>reading</I> and <I>considering</I>
the trial judgment in order to assess whether, in light of the evidence and
arguments at trial, it shows that the trial judge discerned and decided the
live issues so as to permit meaningful appellate review ([<I>R v Sheppard</I>,
<A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2002/2002scc26/2002scc26.html">2002 SCC 26</A>, [2002] 1 SCR 869], at para. 28; <I>R.E.M.</I>, at para. <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2008/2008scc51/2008scc51.html#par57">57</A>). &hellip;</P>

<P class="QuoteReference" style="text-indent:0in">(Emphasis in
original) </P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="33" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="33" data-viibes-start="32" data-viibes-end="31">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par33">33</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>I would finally note that, but for exceptional
circumstances, the Crown is not entitled to put forward a different case on appeal
than the one it chose to advance at trial: see, for example, <I>R&nbsp;v
Suarez-Noa</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2017/2017onca627/2017onca627.html">2017 ONCA 627</A> at paras&nbsp;<A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2017/2017onca627/2017onca627.html#par30">30&ndash;36</A>, 350 CCC (3d) 267. This
principle applies equally in the context of a sentence appeal as in a
conviction appeal: <I>R v Poon</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/sk/skca/doc/2012/2012skca76/2012skca76.html">2012 SKCA 76</A>, 399 Sask R 89; Clayton Ruby, <I>Sentencing</I>,
10th ed (Toronto: LexisNexis, 2020) at &sect;4.79. In <I>R&nbsp;v&nbsp;Marks</I>
(1994), <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/nl/nlca/doc/1994/1994canlii9742/1994canlii9742.html">1994 CanLII 9742 (NL CA)</A>, 91 CCC (3d) 421 (NLCA) at&nbsp;429, the Court said this in the context
of a sentence appeal: </P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">There is no rule or
principle which precludes the Court from considering an appeal in which the
Crown takes a position different from that taken by Crown counsel at the trial
level and this is so whether or not there has been a joint submission as to
sentence. However, a court will only interfere with such a sentence in very
exceptional circumstances and where not to do so would result in an injustice.
In <I>Attorney General of Canada v Roy</I> (1972), CRNS&nbsp;89 (Que QB),
Hugessen J said at p 93:</P>

<P class="QuoteQuote" style="text-indent:0in">The Crown, like
any other litigant, ought not to be heard to repudiate before an appellate
court the position taken by its counsel in the trial court, except for the
gravest possible reasons. Such reasons might be where the sentence was an
illegal one, or where the Crown can demonstrate that its counsel had in some
way been misled or finally where it can be shown that the public interest in
the orderly administration of justice is outweighed by the gravity of the crime
and the gross insufficiency of the sentence.</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="34" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="34" data-viibes-start="33" data-viibes-end="32">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par34">34</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>I agree with this reasoning and note, by way of
example to the same effect, <I>R v Cromwell</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ns/nsca/doc/2021/2021nsca36/2021nsca36.html">2021 NSCA 36</A> at para <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ns/nsca/doc/2021/2021nsca36/2021nsca36.html#par68">68</A>; <I>Baptiste
c R</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/qc/qcca/doc/2021/2021qcca1064/2021qcca1064.html">2021 QCCA 1064</A> at paras <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/qc/qcca/doc/2021/2021qcca1064/2021qcca1064.html#par84">84&ndash;86</A>, 73 CR (7th) 321; and <I>R v H.S.</I>,
<A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2014/2014onca323/2014onca323.html">2014 ONCA 323</A> at paras <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2014/2014onca323/2014onca323.html#par59">59&ndash;62</A>, 308 CCC (3d) 27. </P></div>

<H1>V.<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>ISSUES</H1>

<H2>A.<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>Did the sentencing judge commit errors in
principle that affected the sentence? </H2>

<H3>1.<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN>Introduction</H3>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="35" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="35" data-viibes-start="34" data-viibes-end="33">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par35">35</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>The Crown alleges that the sentencing judge
committed a dizzying array of errors in principle. It submits that he ignored a
variety of aggravating factors that defined the gravity of the offences and Mr.
Mosquito&rsquo;s degree of responsibility in committing them. It contends that he
attached unreasonable weight to facts that did not mitigate the gravity of the
crimes or Mr.&nbsp;Mosquito&rsquo;s moral culpability. Indeed, it claims that the
sentencing judge failed to assess moral culpability at all and that he did not
take account of the fact that Mr. Nataucappo&rsquo;s death was the result of a home
invasion. The Crown strongly emphasizes that this was a violent home invasion,
of a home that was known to be occupied, that resulted in a death caused by a
prohibited weapon in the possession of an offender who was subject to a prohibition
order. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="36" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="36" data-viibes-start="35" data-viibes-end="34">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par36">36</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>In response, Mr. Mosquito asserts that the
sentencing judge did not err in principle. He contends that the sentencing
judge was alive to the arguments before him and considered the factors that
should be weighed in assessing his moral culpability. He also cautions that the
Crown has presented an unfair and slanted view of what occurred in the
sentencing hearing. On this note, he says the Crown has identified errors that
did not occur, misconstrued the submissions of the parties and the way they
were approached by the sentencing judge, and made expansive arguments based on
case law that Crown counsel did not refer to in their sentencing submissions. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="37" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="37" data-viibes-start="36" data-viibes-end="35">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par37">37</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>I agree with the substance of Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s position.
This was an oral sentencing decision, delivered by the sentencing judge
immediately after he heard the submissions of counsel, Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s comments
and the compelling victim impact statements. Although the decision is brief, it
is readily understandable when read in the context of the submissions of both
parties together with the pre-sentence report. It is apparent why the
sentencing judge failed to specifically mention certain aggravating factors and
emphasized the significance of certain mitigating factors. In the
circumstances, the failure by the sentencing judge to expressly advert to all aggravating
factors does not signal, let alone establish, a failure to consider them. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="38" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="38" data-viibes-start="37" data-viibes-end="36">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par38">38</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>My reasons for these conclusions follow. </P></div>

<H3>2.<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN>Analysis</H3>

<H4>a.<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN>Did the sentencing judge err by failing to
consider or to give sufficient weight to aggravating factors?</H4>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="39" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="39" data-viibes-start="38" data-viibes-end="37">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par39">39</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>To begin, the Crown submits that the sentencing
judge reasoned as if there was only one aggravating factor, that being Mr.
Mosquito&rsquo;s decision to take a loaded firearm to an occupied residence in
circumstances where violence was likely to occur. It identifies several other
aggravating factors that, in its view, were not considered. It suggests that these
included Mr.&nbsp;Mosquito&rsquo;s criminal record, which disclosed convictions for
more than two dozen crimes, among which were two previous robberies and an assault
with a weapon. It notes that he was awaiting trial on the last of these at the
time this manslaughter occurred. It argues that the sentencing judge failed to
attach any weight to these aggravating factors, signifying an error in the
proportionality inquiry that impacted the sentence imposed. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="40" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="40" data-viibes-start="39" data-viibes-end="38">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par40">40</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>Similarly, the Crown contends that the
sentencing judge failed to consider the firearms prohibition order to which Mr.
Mosquito was subject at the time of the offence. It says that this was a highly
aggravating factor as to both the gravity of the offence and his moral
culpability, citing <I>R v Omoragbon</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2020/2020onca336/2020onca336.html">2020 ONCA 336</A>. In that case, the
Ontario Court of Appeal concluded that breach of a weapons prohibition and
possession of cocaine for the purpose of trafficking were very serious
offences, each of which were very different delicts deserving of significant
consecutive penitentiary terms. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="41" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="41" data-viibes-start="40" data-viibes-end="39">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par41">41</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>The Crown also argues that the fact that the
firearm used by Mr. Mosquito was not simply any firearm but a prohibited weapon
was another highly aggravating factor that was not considered by the sentencing
judge. In this context, it notes that sawed-off firearms are inherently
dangerous and are used exclusively by criminals, as there is no legitimate
reason to possess them. Indeed, the Crown contends that Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s decision
to simply possess a loaded prohibited firearm without authorization in
contravention of a prohibition order called for a &ldquo;very lengthy penitentiary
sentence&rdquo; in and of itself. It relies on <I>R v Nur</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2015/2015scc15/2015scc15.html">2015 SCC 15</A>, [2015] 1
SCR 773, for this point, where the Supreme Court of Canada found that a
40-month sentence for unlawful possession of a loaded restricted firearm was
fit, despite the fact that Mr. Nur was a first-time offender and that there was
no evidence that he had used the firearm to commit a crime. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="42" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="42" data-viibes-start="41" data-viibes-end="40">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par42">42</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>I would agree that each of these factors was
aggravating. However, I am not persuaded that the sentencing judge failed to
consider them. Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s criminal record was referred to in the
pre-sentence report, which specifically adverted to the fact that he had twenty
convictions as a youth, including one for robbery and three for breaking and
entering. It also said this:</P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">In April 2017, Destin
was convicted of break and enter with intent and being unlawfully at large and
was given a choice to take a 24-month Federal sentence or 18 months Provincial.
Destin states he chose the Federal sentence option based on information he was
provided. His early release date was in October 2018.</P>

<P class="Quotes">Destin picked up a charge for assault with a
weapon just months before he was involved and charged with the index offence,
which occurred on September 21, 2019. He was remanded on September 22, 2019 and
has remained in custody since that date. On April 3, 2020, he was convicted on
the assault charge against his ex<SPAN style="color:#313035">-</SPAN>partner. </P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="43" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="43" data-viibes-start="42" data-viibes-end="41">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par43">43</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>The sentencing judge was well aware of the
contents of the pre-sentence report. He made that clear in the course of the s.&nbsp;606
inquiry, commenting as follows: </P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">As a -- as a first
starting point, I&rsquo;m sure both of you would agree, I want to thank the author of
the presentence report and I have it here on my big screen. The author of this
report, this was an extremely well-done report. As you will recall we were
concerned about <I>Gladue</I> factors and when you read the report it&rsquo;s obvious
why. What the author did in preparing this report was, first of all, a detailed
presentence report in general terms and then the -- the author very astutely
dealt with the <I>Gladue</I>, <I>Ipeelee</I> factors in relation to it. [&hellip;] But
in some fashion, Ms. Kujawa [Crown counsel], if I could ask you to convey to
her my strong appreciation for the presentence report and the ambit of it. And
it was -- it&rsquo;s one of the best ones I&rsquo;ve seen.</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="44" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="44" data-viibes-start="43" data-viibes-end="42">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par44">44</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>Similarly, the sentencing judge confirmed that
he had reviewed the brief of law that had been filed by Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s counsel.
As in the case of the pre&#8209;sentence report, he praised defence counsel,
noting that &ldquo;it wasn&rsquo;t simply a generic brief, I found that it was a brief very
much directed to a representation of Mr. Mosquito&rdquo;. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="45" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="45" data-viibes-start="44" data-viibes-end="43">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par45">45</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>Further, Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s criminal record was
referred to at the sentencing hearing. As noted above, Crown counsel began her
submissions by filing a copy of this record and specifically pointing out that
it included three convictions for violent crimes, being two robberies and
assault with a weapon, and two convictions for break and enter. Defence counsel
also adverted to his client&rsquo;s criminal record, noting that it revealed that he
was &ldquo;a young man who is clearly involved in criminal activity and &hellip; running
with the wrong crowd &hellip; from an early, early age&rdquo;. Further, the discussion of
the erroneous comparison of the criminal records of Mr. Sand and Mr.&nbsp;Mosquito
by Crown counsel flagged this aggravating factor. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="46" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="46" data-viibes-start="45" data-viibes-end="44">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par46">46</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>It is also important that the sentencing judge
said this in his decision, while discussing Mr.&nbsp;Mosquito&rsquo;s <I>Gladue</I>
factors:</P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">There is [<I>sic</I>] some
mitigating circumstances here that -- that I find must be addressed and dealt
with. First of all, I want to say that this is -- there has been provided to me
a particularly compelling <I>Gladue </I>picture of Mr. Mosquito. Mr. Mosquito,
I mean this very sincerely when I say to you, life hasn&rsquo;t always been fair to
you, it&rsquo;s been hard on you and you&rsquo;ve gone through a lot of experiences in your
life that no young man your age should have had to have gone through. But you
did and it&rsquo;s -- it&rsquo;s difficult to say to you, you shouldn&rsquo;t have done the
things that you&rsquo;ve done in relation to it, when you walk the walk you walk and
live the life you&rsquo;ve lived some of the decision[s] would have been hard to make
otherwise in that regard. And I&rsquo;m talking about earlier in your life decisions
that you&rsquo;ve made in that regard, substance abuse, things of that nature.</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="47" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="47" data-viibes-start="46" data-viibes-end="45">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par47">47</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>I understand this statement, brief as it is, as
confirming that Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s criminal history was in the mind of sentencing
judge when he made and delivered the sentencing decision. Once again, the
pre-sentence report &ndash; the source of the &ldquo;compelling <I>Gladue</I> picture&rdquo; &ndash; is
significant in this context, as it not only described Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s already
lengthy history of substance use but disclosed that he had admitted that drugs
and alcohol had contributed to his past involvement with the criminal justice
system. Indeed, it included a report that he had begun dealing drugs at the age
of 13 and had been beaten up by his fellow gang members at the age of 14, as a
result of having been robbed in the course of a drug transaction.</P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="48" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="48" data-viibes-start="47" data-viibes-end="46">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par48">48</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>I am equally confident that the sentencing judge
was aware that Mr. Mosquito was subject to a s.&nbsp;109 firearms prohibition
order and that the firearm he possessed was a prohibited weapon. That order was
listed in the criminal record. Crown counsel expressly advised the sentencing
judge in the course of her submissions of both of these facts. She had also told
him during the s.&nbsp;606 inquiry that the two firearms charges against Mr.
Mosquito would be stayed, but that the facts relating to them would nonetheless
be facts for the purpose of sentencing. The sentencing judge was accordingly aware
that those counts &ndash; which, as noted above, were on the same indictment as the
manslaughter and break, enter and commit &ndash; referred expressly to a sawed&#8209;off
shotgun that was a prohibited weapon and to a breach of the s.&nbsp;109 order,
as follows: </P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="margin-left:85.05pt;text-indent:-49.6pt">Count 3 &nbsp;&nbsp;  On or about the 21st day of September, 2019, at or near
Saskatoon, Saskatchewan, did possess a loaded prohibited firearm, to wit: a
sawed off shotgun, not being the holder of an authorization or license under
which he may possess the said firearm in that place, contrary to <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="95" ss="1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec95subsec1_smooth">section
95(1)</A>(a) of the <I><A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html">Criminal Code</A>; </I>and</P>

<P class="Quotes" style="margin-left:85.05pt;text-indent:-49.6pt">Count 4&nbsp;&nbsp;  On or about the 21st day of September, 2019, at or near
Saskatoon, Saskatchewan, did possess a prohibited firearm while he was
prohibited from doing so by reason of an order made pursuant to <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="109" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec109_smooth">section 109</A> of
the <I><A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html">Criminal Code</A>, </I>contrary to <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="117.01" ss="1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec117.01subsec1_smooth">section 117.01(1)</A> of the <I><A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html">Criminal
Code</A>.</I></P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="49" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="49" data-viibes-start="48" data-viibes-end="47">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par49">49</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>In the result, I am satisfied that the
sentencing judge was aware of and took account of these two aggravating factors
as well. Further, the fact that he did not expressly list them as such does not
signal otherwise, in the circumstances of this case. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="50" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="50" data-viibes-start="49" data-viibes-end="48">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par50">50</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>My conclusion in this respect is informed in
part by the fact that this was an oral decision delivered immediately after the
sentencing judge had conducted a careful s. 606 inquiry and counsel had made
their submissions, all in the course of a single afternoon session. The
reasoning in <I>R v Bear</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/sk/skca/doc/2022/2022skca7/2022skca7.html">2022 SKCA 7</A>, is helpful in this context. In <I>Bear</I>,
the trial judge, like the sentencing judge in this case, did not adjourn after
hearing counsel&rsquo;s arguments before delivering their oral decision. Justice
Schwann commented on the significance of this as follows: </P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">[27]&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; I must also
keep in mind that, while it is appropriate to consider the sentencing judge&rsquo;s
reasons in their entirety, the context and the parties&rsquo; submissions should also
be considered. In that vein, it is not without significance that this was an
extemporaneous oral decision rendered immediately following the submissions of
counsel and without any break in the proceedings. The sentencing judge knew
that the sentence for Mr. Bear was contested and that the Crown&rsquo;s submission
was capped at two years of imprisonment. As to denunciation and deterrence
being the primary objectives in this case, it is noteworthy that Crown counsel
had clearly spelled this out for the sentencing judge in her submissions. To
repeat, she told the sentencing judge that CL was Mr. Bear&rsquo;s domestic partner
and then followed that point by stating, &ldquo;which is again an aggravating factor&rdquo;.
Crown counsel went on to identify the significance this held in fashioning an
appropriate sentence: &ldquo;The Crown also points to the children in the residence, <I>and
the vulnerability of the individual who was the victim in this matter. [CL] is
an Aboriginal female woman, and -- and that also requires this court to
primarily considered denunciation and deterrence</I>&rdquo; &hellip;.</P>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">[28]&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; The Crown&rsquo;s
position on the matter at the time of sentencing could not have been clearer.
Given the speed with which the sentencing hearing progressed, I find it hard to
believe that the sentencing judge had forgotten Crown counsel&rsquo;s submissions,
was unaware of the applicable law or that he failed to appreciate the
significance of intimate partner violence in sentencing Mr. Bear. &hellip; </P>

<P class="QuoteReference" style="text-indent:0in">(Emphasis in
original) </P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="51" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="51" data-viibes-start="50" data-viibes-end="49">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par51">51</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>I am essentially of the same mind in this case.
The fact that reasons were delivered orally may or may not be of any
significance when determining whether they disclose an error. The issues on
appeal do not change, and the same standards of review apply, regardless of
whether reasons are delivered orally or in writing and, for that matter,
regardless of whether they are reported. Where the issue is the sufficiency of
reasons, for example, they must be adequate to serve the purposes described in <I>R
v Sheppard</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2002/2002scc26/2002scc26.html">2002 SCC 26</A> at para <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2002/2002scc26/2002scc26.html#par55">55</A>, [2002] 1 SCR 869. Where the issue is whether
the sentencing judge has applied the relevant principles of sentencing, such as
the duty to consider aggravating and mitigating factors, the reasons, when read
in context and as a whole in light of the live issues in the sentencing, must
be sufficient to show that has occurred. Further, the context of an oral
decision may vary because some oral reasons are delivered after a lengthy
adjournment, are read into the record by the trial judge, and are
indistinguishable in their form from written reasons. However, the fact that
reasons were delivered orally and without delay may, depending on the issues in
the sentencing and the content of the reasons, be a relevant consideration for
the purpose of the functional and contextual analysis described above. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="52" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="52" data-viibes-start="51" data-viibes-end="50">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par52">52</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>Here, the sentencing decision was pronounced by
a judge that had very recently reviewed the well&#8209;crafted written
submissions of defence counsel and a comprehensive pre-sentence report and had
just heard thorough submissions from counsel. He was also familiar with these
offences as a result of having sentenced Mr. Wesaquate. These circumstances can
properly be taken into account in the analysis of the sentencing decision,
including when considering the Crown&rsquo;s submission that the sentencing judge
failed to take account of all but one aggravating circumstance and, indeed,
failed to consider moral culpability at all. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="53" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="53" data-viibes-start="52" data-viibes-end="51">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par53">53</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>I would finally note that the Crown appears to
argue that the length of the sentence imposed on Mr. Mosquito demonstrates, in
and of itself, that the sentencing judge erred in principle by failing to take
account of the criminal record, prohibition order and the nature of the
firearm. This is demonstrated, for example, by the following passage from its
factum: </P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">38. The Crown will
demonstrate shortly that the sentences imposed in this case significantly
deviated from sentences imposed on very similar offenders with most if not all
of the same mitigating personal circumstances as the respondent (ie. youth, <I>Gladue</I><I>
</I>factors, remorse, guilty plea) but who, unlike the respondent, had little
or no<SPAN style="font-family:&quot;TimesNewRomanPSMT&quot;,serif"> </SPAN>violent criminal history. An obvious conclusion follows. The failure
of the trial judge to attach any weight to this aggravating factor cannot be
explained away as a valid instance of &ldquo;individualized&rdquo; sentencing. Rather, it
signifies an error in the trial judge&rsquo;s proportionality inquiry that had an
obvious impact on the sentence imposed. &hellip;</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="54" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="54" data-viibes-start="53" data-viibes-end="52">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par54">54</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>This reasoning appears to conflate the issue of
whether the sentencing judge erred in principle by failing to give these
aggravating factors any or sufficient weight (the first <I>Lacasse</I> ground)
with the question of whether the sentence was demonstrably unfit (the second <I>Lacasse</I>
ground). An error in principle may or may not impact the sentence. Where there
has been an error in principle, the fact that a sentence has been imposed that
appears to be outside the usual range may, depending on what is disclosed by
the reasons as a whole, be of assistance when deciding if the error has
impacted the sentence. However, that does not mean that the imposition of a
sentence that is lower than is generally imposed for such an offence can
demonstrate, in and of itself, that an error in principle had occurred to begin
with. Reasoning of that kind would present a readily apparent danger of
bootstrapping and would tend to erode the important conceptual difference
between the two grounds of appeal identified in <I>Lacasse</I> and <I>Friesen</I>.
</P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="55" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="55" data-viibes-start="54" data-viibes-end="53">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par55">55</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>The proper means of addressing the fact that a
sentence is a major deviation from the usual starting point or appears to be outside
the usual range is captured in the following statement by Karakatsanis J. (dissenting,
but not on this point) in <I>R v Parranto</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2021/2021scc46/2021scc46.html">2021 SCC 46</A>, 411 CCC (3d) 1 [<I>Parranto</I>],
in relation to departure from starting points: </P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">[236]&nbsp;&nbsp;  &hellip; Even though
the starting point reflects appellate guidance regarding the gravity of the
offence, where there is a significant deviation, appellate review must be
conducted in accordance with the ordinary sentencing standard of review. If,
for example, the deviation results from an unreasonably lenient view of the
gravity of the offence, or from unreasonably minimizing the offender&rsquo;s moral
blameworthiness, then the sentence will be an unreasonable departure from the
proportionality principle and will be demonstrably unfit. If, on the other
hand, the deviation stems from heavy but reasonable emphasis on mitigating
factors and rehabilitative principles, appellate intervention is not justified.</P>

<P class="Quotes">[237]&nbsp;&nbsp;  In other words, whether a sentence is
demonstrably unfit is a qualitative rather than a quantitative assessment. What
matters is whether the trial judge imposed a proportionate sentence by
reasonably appreciating the gravity of the offence and the degree of
responsibility of the offender in the specific circumstances of the case: <I>Lacasse</I>,
at paras.&nbsp;<A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2015/2015scc64/2015scc64.html#par58">58 and 67</A>; [<I>R v Nasogaluak</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2010/2010scc6/2010scc6.html">2010 SCC 6</A>, [2010] 1 SCR 206],
at para. 44; [<I>R v L.M.</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2008/2008scc31/2008scc31.html">2008&nbsp;SCC 31</A>, [2008] 2 SCR 163], at para. 36.
If the trial judge has done so then appellate intervention is not justified,
regardless of how far the sentence deviates from a range or starting point: <I>Lacasse</I>,
at paras. <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2015/2015scc64/2015scc64.html#par53">53 and 67</A>; Brown and Martin JJ.&rsquo;s reasons, at paras. 29-30 and 38.</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="56" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="56" data-viibes-start="55" data-viibes-end="54">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par56">56</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>The Crown also submits that, quite apart from
the fact that Mr. Mosquito chose to <I>arm</I> himself with a firearm, it was
aggravating that he decided to <I>use</I> the firearm when he entered the
Howell Avenue house for the purpose of assaulting the occupants. The Crown
bases this argument on submissions as to Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s state of mind. It contends
that Mr. Mosquito claimed the break and enter was accidental and not a
premeditated crime. The Crown characterizes that position as &ldquo;utter nonsense&rdquo;,
and &ldquo;merely another attempt to deny his high degree of culpability in the
offence&rdquo;. It also says that Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s claim that he did not intend to
shoot anyone cannot be squared with the undisputed facts, as he must have been
prepared to shoot someone if the need arose. It submits that &ldquo;a person who does
not intend to shoot anyone does not arm himself with a <I>loaded</I> sawed-off
shotgun before forcibly entering an occupied home&rdquo; (emphasis in original).
Further, it is the Crown&rsquo;s position that Mr. Mosquito effectively admitted
there was nothing accidental about the infliction of violence after the
forcible entry occurred, as that was the sole purpose of the home invasion. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="57" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="57" data-viibes-start="56" data-viibes-end="55">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par57">57</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>In my view, these submissions effectively misrepresent
both Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s position in relation to an important issue and the manner
in which that issue was addressed by the sentencing judge. Mr.&nbsp;Mosquito did
not take the position at any time that the <I>break and enter</I> offence was
&ldquo;accidental&rdquo;. In the course of his s. 606 inquiry, the sentencing judge was at
pains to ensure that Mr.&nbsp;Mosquito admitted the essential elements of the
crimes at issue. Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s counsel confirmed that he did. There was no
suggestion that the break, enter and assault on the occupants of the home was
&ldquo;accidental&rdquo;. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="58" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="58" data-viibes-start="57" data-viibes-end="56">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par58">58</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>Mr. Mosquito took a very different position in
relation to the death of Mr.&nbsp;Nataucappo. The pre&#8209;sentence report
disclosed that Mr. Mosquito said that the incident resulting in Mr.&nbsp;Nataucappo&rsquo;s
death was &ldquo;entirely accidental&rdquo; &ndash; that is, a shot being fired that caused the
death &ndash; and that he was &ldquo;adamant the death of the victim was completely
accidental&rdquo;. That, of course, is a very different thing than claiming that the
break, enter and commit, or, for that matter, his possession of a loaded
shotgun, was accidental. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="59" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="59" data-viibes-start="58" data-viibes-end="57">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par59">59</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>Further, the positions of both parties in
relation to Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s state of mind as to the use of the firearm were well
canvassed and entirely clear. In the course of his submissions to the
sentencing judge, Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s counsel had this to say: </P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">&hellip; During that melee,
Mr. -- Mr. Mosquito, who had the gun in his hand, it was accidentally
discharged. My learned friend and I are -- <I>are in agreement on that</I>.
There was no intention to discharge it or to discharge at anyone in -- in
particular. And in the process, tragically, Mr. Nataucappo lost his life and,
for that, Mr. Mosquito is extremely remorseful. &hellip;</P>

<P class="QuoteReference" style="text-indent:0in">(Emphasis
added)</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="60" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="60" data-viibes-start="59" data-viibes-end="58">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par60">60</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>Defence counsel reiterated these points shortly
thereafter, specifically connecting the dots between his client&rsquo;s intentions
and moral culpability and noting that Mr. Mosquito and the Crown were on the
same page as to his state of mind: </P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">The cases also
indicate -- that I filed indicate that, and Your Lordship&rsquo;s well aware of this,
that manslaughter, in particular, the sentencing judge must look at the moral
culpability or the blameworthiness of the individual that comes before you.
And, as I said earlier, this is a situation where this was not premediated.
This was not sophisticated. This was not planned. As my learned friend and I
both suggest, this was entirely accidental. That&rsquo;s not to suggest that going to
the house with a firearm is -- Mr. Mosquito is absolved of that, but it
certainly wasn&rsquo;t his intention to -- to shoot anyone, and certainly not Mr.
Nataucappo.</P>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">&hellip; This isn&rsquo;t a
situation where multiple shots were fired or, you know, shots were fired in --
in a revenge type of scenario. This was entirely accidental. That&rsquo;s really
important, I would suggest, My Lord.</P>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">A guilty plea has been
entered. He&rsquo;s accepting responsibility. I see my learned friend&rsquo;s point in pointing
out that there is some -- some ambiguity, if you will, in the presentence
report. Having said that, after going through Section 606 with Mr. Mosquito, as
you did, I would suggest that he is accepting complete responsibility for -- his
actions. </P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="61" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="61" data-viibes-start="60" data-viibes-end="59">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par61">61</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>These submissions accord not only with Mr.
Mosquito&rsquo;s statement to the court prior to sentencing but with the position
taken by Crown counsel. During the s. 606 inquiry, the sentencing judge said
this:</P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">Yeah. And, Mr.
Mitchell, I thank you and I want to add for his [i.e., Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s] benefit
in relation to it, that doesn&rsquo;t take away from the non&#8209;accidental part of
it, I mean, that&rsquo;s -- that&rsquo;s why this is manslaughter and it isn&rsquo;t murder. So I
recognize that. And what happened, from what I piece together in relation to
it, was that a situation got out of control and in essence the gun, what I will
call, largely accidentally discharges. It was -- this was not a matter of
intention, as I understand it and I want Mr. Mosquito to understand that, that
I understand that. And I know that that&rsquo;s not what Ms. Kujawa is suggesting.
That doesn&rsquo;t take away from culpability as a result of unlawful act
manslaughter, but it does put a certain perspective on it. But what you have
done for me is that you&rsquo;ve alleviated my concern about what Mr. Mosquito is or
isn&rsquo;t prepared to acknowledge, okay?</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="62" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="62" data-viibes-start="61" data-viibes-end="60">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par62">62</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>When Crown counsel was then asked whether she
had any further comment about this, she stated that she did not, that everybody
was &ldquo;on the same page&rdquo;, and that the Crown &ldquo;is certainly not alleging that
there was an intentional discharge &hellip; of the firearm&rdquo;. She later reiterated that
&ldquo;the Crown is certainly not alleging that there was an intention to kill Kevin
Nataucappo. It was an unfortunate result of this terrible decision made by
these individuals at the time&rdquo;. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="63" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="63" data-viibes-start="62" data-viibes-end="61">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par63">63</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>Finally, it is entirely clear that the
sentencing judge reached the same conclusion as to Mr.&nbsp;Mosquito&rsquo;s state of
mind and that he sentenced Mr. Mosquito on the basis of his admission that he
had taken the loaded shotgun to the crime scene and that it was in his
possession when the fatal shot was fired. As noted above, he said this in the
sentencing decision: </P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">The aggravating
circumstance is taking a gun to a function where or a gathering where violence
could well have erupted. That&rsquo;s a very, very aggravating circumstance. I accept
without reservation that, Mr. Mosquito, you did not intend to harm anyone, but
the taking of the firearm, a loaded firearm, to a gathering where there could
be a violent occurrence is &hellip; an extremely serious matter.</P>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">So it&rsquo;s &hellip; an
aggravating circumstance, significant one, to take a firearm to that group of
people where violence was likely to occur. &hellip; The mistake, the fundamental
mistake Mr.&nbsp;Mosquito made was to go along and support Mr. Al-Zawahreh. &hellip;</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="64" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="64" data-viibes-start="63" data-viibes-end="62">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par64">64</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>In light of all of this, it is very difficult to
understand the Crown&rsquo;s position on this point in this appeal. To reiterate, the
issue was fully and directly addressed at the sentencing hearing. The stance
taken there by Crown counsel was clear, was the same as that taken by Mr.
Mosquito, and was reflected in the findings of fact made by the sentencing
judge. The sentencing judge took account of the fact that the shooting was
unintentional, but that the lack of intention did not take away from the
&ldquo;non-accidental&rdquo; aspect of this manslaughter, being the intentional act of
breaking and entering of an occupied house and committing therein the
indictable offence of assault with a weapon that was a prohibited firearm. All
of that accords with his characterization of the circumstances of this offence
as falling in the middle of the spectrum. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="65" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="65" data-viibes-start="64" data-viibes-end="63">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par65">65</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>I see nothing in any of this to suggest that, as
the Crown claims, the sentencing judge&rsquo;s analysis of the fact that the shotgun
was used, and of Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s state of mind, reflects error. Nor am I able to
conclude, based on the guidance provided by <I>Lacasse</I> and <I>Parranto</I>,
that he dealt with these facts in assessing the severity of the crime or Mr.
Mosquito&rsquo;s personal responsibility and moral culpability in a manner that
constituted or contributed to an error in the proportionality inquiry. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="66" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="66" data-viibes-start="65" data-viibes-end="64">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par66">66</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>Before leaving this point, I would finally note
that having advanced this argument that the intentional choice to actually <I>use</I>
the firearm to assault the occupants of the residence was a separate
aggravating factor, the Crown submitted &ndash; all in the context of its allegation
that there had been an error in principle &ndash; that this aggravating factor
warranted a sentence at the high end of the seven to ten year sentence range,
regardless of whether Mr. Nataucappo had been killed. In that context, it
referred to the fact that the maximum sentence for a dwelling house break and
enter is life in prison demonstrates Parliament&rsquo;s view as to the seriousness of
the offence; that <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="348.1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec348.1_smooth">s.&nbsp;348.1</A> of the <I><A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html">Criminal Code</A></I> provides that
breaking and entering into a home which is known to be occupied at the time is
aggravating; and that the use of violence while committing a break and enter is
also aggravating. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="67" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="67" data-viibes-start="66" data-viibes-end="65">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par67">67</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>Having identified these factors, the Crown
reviewed case law which has stressed the serious nature of home invasions and
the need to emphasize denunciation, deterrence and public protection when
sentencing for this offence. Those cases included <I>R v Pelly</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/sk/skca/doc/2006/2006skca60/2006skca60.html">2006 SKCA
60</A>, 210&nbsp;CCC (3d) 416; <I>R v Matwiy</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ab/abca/doc/1996/1996abca63/1996abca63.html">1996 ABCA 63</A>, 105 CCC (3d) 251; <I>R
v Ross</I> (1999), <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/mb/mbca/doc/1999/1999canlii4397/1999canlii4397.html">1999 CanLII 4397 (MB CA)</A>, 138 Man R (2d) 75 (CA); and <I>R v A.J.C.</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/bc/bcca/doc/2004/2004bcca268/2004bcca268.html">2004 BCCA
268</A>, 186 CCC (3d) 227. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="68" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="68" data-viibes-start="67" data-viibes-end="66">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par68">68</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>These submissions as to the sentencing range for
a home invasion are certainly relevant to the Crown&rsquo;s argument that the
sentence was demonstrably unfit. And, to be sure, the failure to consider
aggravating factors could result in an error in the proportionality analysis
and, in turn, in the imposition of an unfit sentence. However, it would not be
appropriate to conclude that the fact the sentence was seven years, rather than
the nine years that the Crown proposed, demonstrates that the sentencing judge
erred in principle by failing to take account of Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s state of mind.
By arguing in this fashion, the Crown once again invites the Court to conflate
the two <I>Lacasse</I> grounds. </P></div>

<H4>b.<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN>Did the sentencing judge unreasonably emphasize
mitigating factors? </H4>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="69" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="69" data-viibes-start="68" data-viibes-end="67">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par69">69</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>The Crown also submits that the sentencing judge
placed an unreasonable emphasis on several mitigating factors. In substance, I
see this aspect of the Crown&rsquo;s argument &ndash; and indeed, the gravamen of the
Crown&rsquo;s submissions as a whole &ndash; as an invitation to this Court to reweigh and
rebalance the relevant factors and to substitute our discretion for that of the
sentencing judge. That is not permitted by the standard of review. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="70" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="70" data-viibes-start="69" data-viibes-end="68">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par70">70</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>However, the Crown has identified specific
arguments as to various mitigating factors. I&nbsp;will accordingly deal with
the substance of each of those arguments. As in relation to the alleged errors
as to aggravating factors, I will take account of the submissions made by Crown
counsel at the sentencing hearing and the live issues in light of the evidence.
</P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="71" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="71" data-viibes-start="70" data-viibes-end="69">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par71">71</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>First, while the Crown agrees that Mr.
Mosquito&rsquo;s guilty pleas are mitigating, it contends that the sentencing judge
erred by failing to take account of contextual considerations that reduce their
significance. More specifically, it emphasizes that Mr. Mosquito did not plead
guilty early, but instead waited 18 months before he accepted responsibility,
and then only on the condition that the Crown would not recommend a sentence
that is usually imposed on similar offenders who commit similar crimes. On a
related note, the Crown alleges that Mr. Mosquito attempted to avoid taking responsibility
by telling the author of the pre-sentence report that he was not actually in
possession of the firearm when he entered the Howell Avenue house and was not
the person who shot Mr. Nataucappo.</P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="72" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="72" data-viibes-start="71" data-viibes-end="70">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par72">72</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>In my view, Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s evasive explanations
to the author of the pre-sentence report as to what occurred when the shotgun
was discharged are of little or no significance for the purpose of determining
the mitigating effect of the guilty plea. The fact is that Mr. Mosquito pled
guilty to both offences. As I have explained in detail above, he admitted that
he took the loaded shotgun into the home and that it was in his possession when
the shooting occurred. He also acknowledged his responsibility to Mr.&nbsp;Nataucappo&rsquo;s
family when he spoke at the sentencing hearing. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="73" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="73" data-viibes-start="72" data-viibes-end="71">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par73">73</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>Further, the sentencing judge was aware of this
portion of the pre-sentence report and that the Crown had contended that it signalled
a failure to accept responsibility, which Crown counsel connected to Mr.
Mosquito&rsquo;s immaturity. That was a key reason for his careful s. 606 inquiry as
to what Mr. Mosquito was admitting. I am unable to conclude that he did not
consider these facts, or that he committed an error within the meaning of the
standard of review, when he determined the weight to be assigned to them, in
the context of all relevant factors. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="74" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="74" data-viibes-start="73" data-viibes-end="72">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par74">74</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>Nor do I agree that the sentencing judge erred
by according too much weight to Mr.&nbsp;Mosquito&rsquo;s guilty pleas because those
pleas were juxtaposed with the Crown&rsquo;s submission that nine years would be a
fit sentence. Initially, the Crown took the position on this appeal that the Court
should discount the mitigating effect of the guilty pleas, as they were the
result of a plea bargain. Defence counsel vigorously and understandably
objected to that proposition. He correctly pointed out that, even if the
sentencing judge could have concluded that there was a plea bargain, he could
not have known why the bargain had been struck, as those negotiations were
privileged. As he also noted, the Crown&rsquo;s position would tend to undermine the
very foundation of plea bargains, which are based on a quid pro quo.
Fortunately, Crown counsel abandoned this line of argument at the hearing of
the appeal. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="75" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="75" data-viibes-start="74" data-viibes-end="73">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par75">75</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>Further, it is inconsistent for the Crown to have
submitted at the sentencing hearing that nine years in custody is a fit
sentence and that the guilty pleas are mitigating and yet, on appeal, contend
that the mitigating effect of the guilty pleas should be discounted because the
Crown took that position as to sentence. Once again, this was an attempt by the
Crown to distance itself from the positions Crown counsel had taken at the
sentencing hearing. In substance, it seeks to make a new argument on appeal. I
note that, at the sentencing hearing, Crown counsel addressed the effect of
what it considered to be the key mitigating factors as follows: </P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">He does have some
mitigating factors. He&rsquo;s got his guilty plea. He also has the fact that this
wasn&rsquo;t his plan, he -- he merely went along with other people. Unfortunately,
young people are prone to do that sort of thing. And his youth is on his side,
as well, the <I>Gladue</I><I> </I>factors. I would echo everyone&rsquo;s comments today
about the well-written report and those factors have to be taken into account
in this situation.</P>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">As well, the fact that
he has been in custody during a pandemic is also a factor I would suggest has
to be considered in this equation. The Crown and defence had factored that in
with Mr. Wesaquate. I believe it has to also be factored in this case as well,
especially given that Mr. Mosquito has contracted COVID. Fortunately he -- he
survived it, so all of those things come into account. </P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="76" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="76" data-viibes-start="75" data-viibes-end="74">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par76">76</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>Crown counsel later summarized her position on
the aggravating and mitigating factors in this sentencing as follows: </P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">&hellip; The Crown has taken
into account totality; the Crown has taken into account the guilty plea, the <I>Gladue</I><I>
</I>factors and the COVID factors and are suggesting that he receive the same
sentence that Mr. Sand received. I would suggest that that is not out of line,
in fact, it&rsquo;s perfectly in line when you look at the positive and the negative
factors that existed in -- in Mr. Sand&rsquo;s case.</P>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">With all due respect
to my learned friend, he suggests five to six years. I don&rsquo;t think we should be
putting Mr. Mosquito in the same level as Mr. Wesaquate, who received five
years. And I don&rsquo;t think his -- the fact that he gets out of the car, he
participates in a home invasion and he does so with a weapon can only raise
this to the level of one-year greater than the one person who remained in the
vehicle. So that&rsquo;s why the Crown takes the position that it does for the
nine-year sentence. And takes into all -- into account all of those factors
I&rsquo;ve enumerated. <A name="_Hlk126754210">I think COVID has done a number on all
of us and it has to be reflected in sentences at this time, My Lord, because I
think that it makes being in custody far more risky, probably far more
terrifying and I don&rsquo;t want it to be suggested that the Crown is not mindful of
this kind of factor. </A>So all of these go into the equation to suggest rather
than perhaps a 12-year sentence that nine years would be far more reflective of
the mitigating factor of his guilty plea, as I&rsquo;ve indicated it wasn&rsquo;t his plan,
there&rsquo;s many <I>Gladue</I> factors going on and the COVID crisis.</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="77" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="77" data-viibes-start="76" data-viibes-end="75">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par77">77</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>The sentencing judge was entitled to rely on
these submissions. So too were Mr.&nbsp;Mosquito and his defence counsel. The
submissions of counsel are often a key factor in defining the live issues and,
thus, in determining whether the reasons given by the sentencing judge disclose
errors in principle that affected the sentence. That is certainly true in this
case. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="78" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="78" data-viibes-start="77" data-viibes-end="76">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par78">78</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>As to the fact that Mr. Mosquito pled guilty 18
months after his initial arrest, it is true that a guilty plea is sometimes
accorded greater weight when it occurs earlier in the process. However, the
extent that this is so depends on the circumstances of each case. The
sentencing judge was well aware that 18 months had passed. I am unable to conclude
that he did not take this into account as part of the circumstances as a whole.
It must be kept in mind that the criminal justice system was significantly
disrupted in Saskatchewan by Covid protocols at the relevant time; that these
were very serious offences; that a direct indictment was preferred 11&nbsp;months
before the pleas were entered; and that pleas were taken and sentences imposed
on two of Mr.&nbsp;Mosquito&rsquo;s co-accused in the period leading up to his guilty
pleas. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="79" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="79" data-viibes-start="78" data-viibes-end="77">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par79">79</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>Further, even if I had concluded that the
sentencing judge did not consider the delay in entering the plea, I would not
have found that it would have impacted the sentence. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="80" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="80" data-viibes-start="79" data-viibes-end="78">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par80">80</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>The Crown also takes the position that the
sentencing judge erred in his treatment of <I>Gladue</I> factors. In particular,
it submits that the sentencing judge erred in principle by failing to undertake
the analysis mandated by <I>R v Chanalquay</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/sk/skca/doc/2015/2015skca141/2015skca141.html">2015 SKCA 141</A>, 472 Sask R 110;
that is, it says that Mr.&nbsp;Mosquito failed to explain how his unique
circumstances impacted his culpability and, indeed, argues that there was no
evidence to support any such link. It contends that both the sentencing judge
and Mr. Mosquito reasoned that the mere presence of <I>Gladue</I> factors
warranted a sentence discount and that this was an error in principle that had
an obvious impact on the sentence.</P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="81" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="81" data-viibes-start="80" data-viibes-end="79">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par81">81</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>Once again, the Crown has changed its tune and
seeks to make a divergent argument in relation to this issue on appeal. It asks
that this Court find that the sentencing judge erred by taking account of <I>Gladue</I>
factors despite the submissions by Crown counsel at the sentencing hearing advising
that he should do so. The Crown&rsquo;s sentencing submissions in relation to
mitigating factors was very clear. Crown counsel told the sentencing judge that
the readily apparent <I>Gladue</I> factors, together with the guilty pleas and
the impact of Covid, were the reason that the sentence should be nine years,
rather than the twelve it would have sought in the absence of those factors.
Those submissions can only be taken to mean that the Crown agreed with defence
counsel that the <I>Gladue</I> and Covid factors were important mitigating
factors that meaningfully affected the duration of a fit sentence. The
sentencing decision confirms that the sentencing judge was of the same mind.
That conclusion was well grounded in the evidence and did not result in the
unreasonable exercise of his discretion. Accordingly, given the standard of
review, there was no error of the kind identified by the Crown. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="82" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="82" data-viibes-start="81" data-viibes-end="80">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par82">82</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>Finally, the Crown submits that the sentencing
judge erred by treating Covid as a mitigating factor. Indeed, it contends that
this aspect of his reasoning was &ldquo;unreasonable and unsupported by the evidence&rdquo;
and &ldquo;does not deserve or warrant deference&rdquo;. The Crown put the matter this way
in its factum: </P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">98. The trial judge
reasoned as if the Covid pandemic was a significant mitigating factor. Even if
the trial judge was correct not to question [Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s] questionable claim
that he was infected with the virus twice within a 60 day period (in December
2020 and again in January 2021) despite what is commonly known about the
biological processes relating to natural immunity, it is difficult to see why
this was treated as a mitigating factor at all.</P>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">&hellip;</P>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">100. More importantly,
[Mr. Mosquito] was in pre-sentence custody because he had a lengthy criminal
record, unlawfully killed a man while awaiting trial on a charge of assault
with a weapon and refused to accept responsibility for that crime until he was assured
he would not be sentenced like similar offenders who commit similar crimes. He
was subject to the lock downs on the remand unit and was unable to participate in
programs that are available to sentenced offenders by reason of his voluntary criminal
acts and the choices he made while awaiting trial. To reason as if he was entitled
to credit for the consequences of those acts and choices was illogical and unreasonable.</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="83" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="83" data-viibes-start="82" data-viibes-end="81">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par83">83</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>I am troubled by many aspects of this
submission. It directly contradicts the position taken by Crown counsel at the
sentencing hearing. Crown counsel did not suggest Mr. Mosquito&rsquo;s claim that he
had contracted Covid twice was questionable. To the contrary, she specifically
said that he had contracted Covid. There is no doubt but that the sentencing
judge was entitled to and did accept that as a fact. The suggestion that this
finding did not make sense because of what is &ldquo;commonly known about the
biological processes relating to natural immunity&rdquo; is of no assistance to the
Crown. Quite apart from the fact that the Crown did not advance that position
at the sentencing hearing, this was not a fact in relation to which judicial
notice could be taken. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="84" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="84" data-viibes-start="83" data-viibes-end="82">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par84">84</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>Further, Crown counsel submitted at the
sentencing hearing that the sentencing judge was not only entitled to but should
take the impact of Covid into account. To reiterate, she said this: </P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">I think COVID has done
a number on all of us and it has to be reflected in sentences at this time, My
Lord, because I think that it makes being in custody far more risky, probably
far more terrifying and I don&rsquo;t want it to be suggested that the Crown is not
mindful of this kind of factor. </P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="85" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="85" data-viibes-start="84" data-viibes-end="83">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par85">85</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>There is nothing ambiguous about this position. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="86" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="86" data-viibes-start="85" data-viibes-end="84">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par86">86</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>All of this being so, the only remaining question
in relation to the Covid issue is whether the sentencing judge was entitled to
take it into account as a factor that directly impacted the sentence, rather
than as remand credit. At the hearing of this appeal, this Court invited
counsel to provide supplementary written submissions on this point. Both the
Crown and Mr. Mosquito did so, contending that there are circumstances in which
a sentencing judge should take account of the effect of Covid on the offender
when determining a proportionate sentence. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="87" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="87" data-viibes-start="86" data-viibes-end="85">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par87">87</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>I agree. Decisions from this Court have affirmed
that where an offender submits that the impact of Covid should be taken into
account when determining a fit sentence, evidence will be required as to the
effect of the Covid pandemic on that offender: <I>R v AB</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/sk/skca/doc/2021/2021skca119/2021skca119.html">2021 SKCA 119</A> at
paras&nbsp;<A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/sk/skca/doc/2021/2021skca119/2021skca119.html#par40">40&ndash;42</A>; see also, <I>R v Mehari</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/sk/skca/doc/2021/2021skca139/2021skca139.html">2021 SKCA 139</A> at paras <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/sk/skca/doc/2021/2021skca139/2021skca139.html#par53">53&ndash;57</A>
(Jackson J.A., in Chambers). Courts of Appeal in other provinces have generally
agreed with this requirement. Such evidence could, for example, relate to the
impact of protocols on the conditions of incarceration in a particular
institution or part thereof, both in general and in relation to the offender at
issue. It could also relate to the risk of infection or the potential impact of
infection for the offender, including their unique vulnerabilities. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="88" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="88" data-viibes-start="87" data-viibes-end="86">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par88">88</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>That is not to suggest that judicial notice
could not be taken of some relevant facts, providing that the conditions that
must be met before judicial notice can be taken have been met. However, a
cautious approach is in order. This Court canvassed this issue in <I>O.M.S. v
E.J.S.</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/sk/skca/doc/2023/2023skca8/2023skca8.html">2023&nbsp;SKCA 8</A>, which related to judicial notice as to the safety
and efficacy of a Covid vaccine in a family law context: </P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">[33]&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; Judicial
notice &ldquo;dispenses with the need for proof of facts that are clearly
uncontroversial or beyond reasonable dispute&rdquo; ([<I>R v Find</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2001/2001scc32/2001scc32.html">2001 SCC 32</A>,
[2001] 1 SCR 863] at para 48; and [<A name="_Hlk119065891"><SPAN style="">David M. Paciocco, Palma Paciocco &amp; Lee Stuesser, <I>The
Law of Evidence</I>, 8th ed (Toronto: Irwin Law, 2020)</SPAN></A><SPAN style="">]</SPAN> at 575). As such, it applies to two kinds of
facts: those that are &ldquo;(1) so notorious or generally accepted as not to be the
subject of debate among reasonable persons; or (2) capable of immediate and
accurate demonstration by resort to readily accessible sources of indisputable
accuracy&rdquo; (<I>Find</I> at para <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2001/2001scc32/2001scc32.html#par48">48</A>). These are often referred to as the &ldquo;Morgan
criteria&rdquo;, from the article by Professor E. M. Morgan, &ldquo;Judicial Notice&rdquo; (1944)
57 Harv L Rev 269. See also: <I>R v Mabior</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2012/2012scc47/2012scc47.html">2012 SCC 47</A> at para&nbsp;<A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2012/2012scc47/2012scc47.html#par71">71</A>,
[2012] 2 SCR 584; <I>Reference Re Alberta Statutes</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/1938/1938canlii1/1938canlii1.html">1938 CanLII 1 (SCC)</A>, [1938] SCR 100 at 128; <I>Quebec
(Attorney General) v A</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2013/2013scc5/2013scc5.html">2013 SCC 5</A> at para <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2013/2013scc5/2013scc5.html#par238">238</A>, [2013] 1 SCR 61; <I>Saskatchewan
v Good Spirit School Division No. 204</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/sk/skca/doc/2020/2020skca34/2020skca34.html">2020 SKCA 34</A> at para <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/sk/skca/doc/2020/2020skca34/2020skca34.html#par107">107</A>, 445 DLR
(4th) 179; and [<SPAN style="">Sidney N. Lederman, Michelle K.
Fuerst &amp; Hamish C. Stewart,&nbsp;<I>Sopinka, Lederman &amp; Bryant: The Law
of Evidence in Canada</I>, 6th ed (Toronto: LexisNexis, 2022)]</SPAN> at para&nbsp;19.25.</P>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">&hellip;</P>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">[35]&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; Matters that
are the proper subject of expert evidence are, by definition, &ldquo;neither
notorious nor capable of immediate and accurate demonstration&rdquo; (<I>Find</I> at
para <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2001/2001scc32/2001scc32.html#par49">49</A>. See also: [<I>R v J.M.</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2021/2021onca150/2021onca150.html">2021 ONCA 150</A>, 154 OR (3d) 401] at para
35; and Paciocco at 577). That said, recourse to a scientific treatise or
textbook may be permissible to prove a fact that is not notorious, but which is
capable of immediate and accurate demonstration, if the court can be certain
that there will be no conflict between different sources of that kind. See:
Sopinka at para 19.54; and <I>J.M.</I> at para <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2021/2021onca150/2021onca150.html#par31">31</A>. However, the exception to
the general rule that judicial notice cannot be taken of matters that require
reference to an expert&rsquo;s guidance must be applied only where it is clear that
this condition has been met; that is, only where there is well-established, and
clearly applicable consensus.</P>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">&hellip;</P>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">[37]&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; In any
event, precedent in matters involving judicial notice must be approached
cautiously in a context where there is continuing development regarding what
facts are notorious and which sources are indisputably accurate. This is
nowhere more so the case than with the information surrounding the Covid-19
pandemic, where important aspects of what &ldquo;everybody knows&rdquo; today have come to
be perceived, at least by some, as suspect or incorrect tomorrow.</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="89" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="89" data-viibes-start="88" data-viibes-end="87">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par89">89</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>This Court has not yet addressed the question of
the appropriate legal framework to apply when determining whether the pandemic
should impact a sentence. Should the effect of the Covid pandemic be dealt with
as a collateral consequence? Should it be considered as a mitigating
circumstance? When, if ever, should it be dealt with by providing remand credit
in excess of the 1.5:1 ratio specified in <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="719" ss="3" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec719subsec3_smooth">s.&nbsp;719(3)</A> of the <I><A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html">Criminal
Code</A></I>? What of potential <I>Charter </I>remedies? </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="90" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="90" data-viibes-start="89" data-viibes-end="88">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par90">90</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>All of these approaches have found support in
the case law. The remand credit approach has been repeatedly applied by Ontario
courts, through the provision of &ldquo;<I>Duncan</I> credits&rdquo; &ndash;so&#8209;called
because enhanced credit was first awarded for harsh remand conditions in
Ontario in <I>R&nbsp;v&nbsp;Duncan</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2016/2016onca754/2016onca754.html">2016 ONCA 754</A>. See, for example: <I>R v
Rajmoolie</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2020/2020onca791/2020onca791.html">2020 ONCA 791</A>, 155 OR (3d) 64; <I>R&nbsp;v Brown</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2020/2020onca196/2020onca196.html">2020 ONCA
196</A>; and <I>R v Green</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2021/2021onca932/2021onca932.html">2021 ONCA 932</A>. In <I>R v Marshall</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2021/2021onca344/2021onca344.html">2021 ONCA
344</A>, Doherty&nbsp;J.A. explored the difference between the statutory credit
that is capped at 1.5:1 by s.&nbsp;719(3) the <I><A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html">Criminal Code</A></I> and <I>Duncan</I>
credits. Additional remand credit would, of course, make sense only if the
offender was adversely affected while in remand, whether as a result of the
heightened risk of or actually contracting Covid in a congregate setting or by
the impact of protocols intended to mitigate that risk. Ontario courts have
also accepted that the collateral consequence approach may apply in some
circumstances: <I>R v Kanthasamy</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2021/2021onca32/2021onca32.html">2021 ONCA 32</A>; <I>R&nbsp;v&nbsp;Johnston</I>,
<A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2021/2021onca331/2021onca331.html">2021 ONCA 331</A>; and <I>R v Morgan</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/on/onca/doc/2020/2020onca279/2020onca279.html">2020 ONCA 279</A>. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="91" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="91" data-viibes-start="90" data-viibes-end="89">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par91">91</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>There are also many decisions of interest in
other provinces. In <I>R v McDonald</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ab/abca/doc/2021/2021abca262/2021abca262.html">2021 ABCA 262</A>, the Court concluded
that remand credit in excess of the 1.5:1 ratio cannot be granted absent a <I>Charter</I>
breach of a sort that would enable the court to consider a remedy under s.&nbsp;24(1)
of the <I>Charter</I>. In <I>R v St. Constantine</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/bc/bcca/doc/2022/2022bcca6/2022bcca6.html">2022 BCCA 6</A>, the Court
found that the effect of the pandemic on offenders should be addressed as a
collateral consequence unique to each offender. <I>R v Demeter</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/bc/bcca/doc/2022/2022bcca115/2022bcca115.html">2022 BCCA
115</A>, affirmed <I>St. Constantine</I>. In <I>R v Caribou</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/mb/mbca/doc/2022/2022mbca95/2022mbca95.html">2022&nbsp;MBCA 95</A>,
Spivak J.A., having canvassed the various approaches that had been taken by
other courts, summarized the Manitoba Court of Appeal&rsquo;s justification for
adopting the collateral consequence route as follows: </P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">[45]&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; In my view,
the preferable method to allow for the impact of the COVID&#8209;19 pandemic in
determining a fit sentence, one that is doctrinally sound and consistent with sections
719(3) and 719(3.1) of the <I>Code</I>, is to treat this as a collateral
consequence, rather than an enhanced credit. I would, therefore, endorse the
approach taken in <I>Morgan</I>. As highlighted in [<I>R v Suter</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2018/2018scc34/2018scc34.html">2018 SCC
34</A>, [2018] 2 SCR 496] and <I>Morgan</I>, the question is whether the collateral
consequences are such that a particular sentence would have a more significant
impact on an offender because of their circumstances. It is analytically
fitting to assess the potential impact of the COVID-19 pandemic on an offender
in this context. In saying this, I reiterate what has been emphasized in the
jurisprudence&mdash;that collateral consequences cannot be used to reduce a sentence
to the point where it is no longer proportionate (see <I>Kolola</I> at para 18;
and <I>St Constantine</I> at para 43).</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="92" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="92" data-viibes-start="91" data-viibes-end="90">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par92">92</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>These comments mirror the following statement by
the Nunavut Court of Appeal in its <I>per curiam</I> decision in <I>R v Kolola</I>,
<A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/nu/nuca/doc/2021/2021nuca11/2021nuca11.html">2021 NUCA 11</A>: </P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">[18]&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; The COVID-19
pandemic may be considered a consequence which might have a more significant
impact on an offender because of his unique and individual
circumstances.&nbsp;It is within a judge's discretion to consider the impact of
the COVID-19 pandemic on an offender as part of the sentencing process.
Consideration of a collateral consequence may result in a more lenient sentence;
however, it cannot be used to reduce a sentence to the point that it becomes
disproportionate to the gravity of the offence or the moral blameworthiness of
the offender: <I>R v Suter</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2018/2018scc34/2018scc34.html">2018 SCC 34</A> at paras <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2018/2018scc34/2018scc34.html#par48"><A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2018/2018scc34/2018scc34.html#par48">48</A>, 56</SPAN>.</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="93" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="93" data-viibes-start="92" data-viibes-end="91">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par93">93</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>As a general proposition, I agree with <I>St. Constantine</I>,
<I>Caribou </I>and <I>Kolola </I>that the effects of the Covid pandemic are
properly dealt with as collateral consequences. That approach maintains the
distinction between mitigating factors, which reduce the gravity of the offence
or the moral blameworthiness of the offender, and factors that result from the
extraordinary conditions experienced by an offender in serving their sentence or
from the exceptional effect on the offender that do not. This requires that the
general and specific impacts of Covid be taken into account in a manner that
respects the principles of sentencing, including the fundamental principle that
a sentence must be proportionate to the gravity of the offence and the degree
of responsibility of the offender. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="94" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="94" data-viibes-start="93" data-viibes-end="92">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par94">94</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>Having affirmed the propriety of this approach,
it is not necessary to decide whether there are circumstances in which Covid
could relate to the circumstances of the offence or the offender in a manner
that is either aggravating or mitigating. Here, the sentencing judge was
entitled to take account of the effect of the Covid pandemic on Mr. Mosquito as
a collateral consequence. The submissions of counsel on this point were
grounded in the facts. Mr. Mosquito had had two bouts of Covid and had been impacted
by protocol driven restrictions on his freedom of movement within the
institution and the corresponding ability to interact with others. The weight
to be given to this factor was, subject to the principles of sentencing as a
whole, for the sentencing judge to determine. I am unable to say that he erred
in his decision on this point. </P></div>

<H2>B.<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; 
</SPAN>Did the sentencing judge err by imposing a
sentence that was demonstrably unfit? </H2>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="95" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="95" data-viibes-start="94" data-viibes-end="93">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par95">95</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>The Crown also alleges that the sentence imposed
on Mr. Mosquito was demonstrably unfit. As is noted above, the focus of the
inquiry under this ground of appeal is on the fundamental principle of
proportionality stated in <A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" s="718.1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html#sec718.1_smooth">s.&nbsp;718.1</A> of the <I><A class="reflex2-link" concatenated-id="12792-current-1" href="/en/ca/laws/stat/rsc-1985-c-c-46/latest/rsc-1985-c-c-46.html">Criminal Code</A></I>, which
requires that a sentence must be &ldquo;proportionate to the gravity of the offence
and the degree of responsibility of the offender&rdquo;. A sentence will therefore be
demonstrably unfit if it constitutes an &ldquo;unreasonable departure from this
principle&rdquo; (<I>Lacasse</I><I> </I>at para <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2015/2015scc64/2015scc64.html#par53">53</A>). The fact that this test sets a high
threshold is evident in the phrases that have been used to describe it, such as
&ldquo;clearly unreasonable&rdquo;, &ldquo;clearly or manifestly excessive&rdquo;, &ldquo;clearly excessive
or inadequate&rdquo;, or representing a &ldquo;substantial and marked departure&rdquo; (at para
52). </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="96" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="96" data-viibes-start="95" data-viibes-end="94">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par96">96</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>The Crown&rsquo;s submissions in relation to this
ground place a good deal of emphasis on the usual range of sentences for the
offences in question. It contends that for serious home invasions committed by
similar offenders &ndash; which it describes as offenders who are young, remorseful
and have &ldquo;unfortunate&rdquo; backgrounds &ndash; the range is seven to ten years. Indeed,
it argues that a sentence at the upper end of this range would have been
appropriate even if Mr. Mosquito had not caused the death of Mr.&nbsp;Nataucappo.
As indicated above, it also correctly emphasizes that courts have stressed the
importance of denunciation, deterrence and public protection in home invasion
cases. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="97" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in;page-break-after:avoid" data-viibes-parag="97" data-viibes-start="96" data-viibes-end="95">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par97">97</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>The Crown further alleges that the sentencing
judge did not respect the secondary principle of parity. The significance of
starting points and ranges, and their relationship to the principles of
proportionality and parity, was addressed in <I>Parranto</I>. In <I>R v Hilbach</I>,
<A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2023/2023scc3/2023scc3.html">2023 SCC 3</A>, Martin&nbsp;J. summarized this relationship as follows: </P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">[67]&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; Parity requires
that a sentence &ldquo;be similar to sentences imposed on similar offenders for
similar offences committed in similar circumstances&rdquo; (s. 718.2(b)). Sentencing
ranges and starting points are useful tools in assessing proportionality and
parity as they reflect judicial consensus on an offence's gravity and help
reduce substantial disparities in sentencing (<I>R. v. Parranto</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2021/2021scc46/2021scc46.html">2021 SCC 46</A>,
at para. <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2021/2021scc46/2021scc46.html#par20">20</A>, citing <I>R. v. Lacasse</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2015/2015scc64/2015scc64.html">2015 SCC 64</A>, [2015] 3 S.C.R. 1089, at
para. <A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2015/2015scc64/2015scc64.html#par2">2</A>). Mandatory minimum sentences, as well as the range of sentences set
out by Parliament, for similar offences may also assist in this inquiry.</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="98" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="98" data-viibes-start="97" data-viibes-end="96">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par98">98</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>The fact that ranges and starting points are
useful tools, however, does not mean that they trump the principles that
constrain appellate review of sentencing decisions, which reflect both the
nature of the sentencing process and the deference accorded to sentencing
judges. As Wagner&nbsp;J. emphasized in <I>Lacasse</I>: </P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">[11]&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; This Court
has on many occasions noted the importance of giving wide latitude to
sentencing judges. [&hellip;] The fact that a judge deviates from the proper
sentencing range does not in itself justify appellate intervention. Ultimately,
except where a sentencing judge makes an error of law or an error in principle
that has an impact on the sentence, an appellate court may not vary the
sentence unless it is demonstrably unfit.</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="99" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="99" data-viibes-start="98" data-viibes-end="97">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par99">99</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;
</SPAN>As he later confirmed:</P></div>

<P class="Quotes" style="text-indent:0in">[58]&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; There will
always be situations that call for a sentence outside a particular range:
although ensuring parity in sentencing is in itself a desirable objective, the
fact that each crime is committed in unique circumstances by an offender with a
unique profile cannot be disregarded. The determination of a just and
appropriate sentence is a highly individualized exercise that goes beyond a
purely mathematical calculation. It involves a variety of factors that are
difficult to define with precision. This is why it may happen that a sentence
that, on its face, falls outside a particular range, and that may never have
been imposed in the past for a similar crime, is not demonstrably unfit. Once
again, everything depends on the gravity of the offence, the offender&rsquo;s degree
of responsibility and the specific circumstances of each case. LeBel J.
commented as follows on this subject [<I>R v Nasogaluak</I>, <A class="reflex3-caselaw" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2010/2010scc6/2010scc6.html">2010 SCC 6</A> at para
<A class="reflex-parag" href="/en/ca/scc/doc/2010/2010scc6/2010scc6.html#par44">44</A>, [2010] 1 SCR 206]:</P>

<P class="QuoteQuote" style="text-indent:0in">A judge can order
a sentence outside that range as long as it is in accordance with the
principles and objectives of sentencing. Thus, a sentence falling outside the
regular range of appropriate sentences is not necessarily unfit. Regard must be
had to all the circumstances of the offence and the offender, and to the needs
of the community in which the offence occurred.</P>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="100" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="100" data-viibes-start="99" data-viibes-end="98">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par100">100</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;  </SPAN>Having considered the Crown&rsquo;s position as to the sentence imposed on
Mr. Mosquito in light of these principles, I am unable to conclude that a
seven-year sentence is demonstrably unfit. The Crown&rsquo;s argument is closely
linked to the alleged errors in principle dealt with above. It submits that the
sentencing judge did not impose a proportionate sentence because he failed to
consider material aggravating factors, overemphasized mitigating factors and
ignored the principle of parity. For the reasons explained above, I do not
agree that the sentencing judge committed errors in principle in relation to
aggravating and mitigating factors. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="101" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="101" data-viibes-start="100" data-viibes-end="99">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par101">101</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;  </SPAN>As to the principle of parity, I would agree with Mr. Mosquito that
the sentences that were imposed on his co-accused are significant comparators.
As he notes, Crown counsel suggested at the sentencing hearing that Mr.
Al-Zawahreh &ndash; who was sentenced to eight years in custody &ndash; was arguably more
culpable. The driver, Mr. Wesaquate, was clearly less culpable, and he was
sentenced to five years, while the individual who stood next to Mr. Mosquito at
the time of the shooting received a seven&#8209;year sentence. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="102" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="102" data-viibes-start="101" data-viibes-end="100">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par102">102</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;  </SPAN>Mr. Mosquito contends that the decisions relied on by the Crown as
demonstrating that the sentence was unfit &ndash; many of which are from Ontario and
British Columbia &ndash; are distinguishable. In my view, there are both sufficient
similarities to make them of interest and sufficient differences that they can
be distinguished. As he also points out, this Court has accepted that the
sentencing range for unlawful act manslaughter is from four to twelve years. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="103" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="103" data-viibes-start="102" data-viibes-end="101">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par103">103</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;  </SPAN>As to the <I>Sand</I><I> </I>decision in particular, I would agree with Mr.
Mosquito that there are significant distinguishing aggravating factors,
including that Mr. Sand had planned the home invasion; had pled guilty but did
not otherwise accept responsibility; had over 100 prior convictions; had no
plans to change his life or behaviour; and had acted in a manner that was
considered aggravating while on remand. In light of these factors, the sentence
imposed in <I>Sand</I><I> </I>is entirely consistent with that imposed on Mr.
Mosquito. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="104" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="104" data-viibes-start="103" data-viibes-end="102">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par104">104</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;  </SPAN>It is also my view that, even if this sentence could be seen as
having deviated from the usual range in similar circumstances, it is clear why
the sentencing judge settled on a seven-year term of incarceration. He
identified the mitigating factors that he considered to be relevant, with particular
emphasis on the impacts of <I>Gladue</I> and Covid. He believed that Mr.
Mosquito was remorseful and had concluded that there were reasonable prospects
for the rehabilitation of this young man. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="105" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="105" data-viibes-start="104" data-viibes-end="103">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par105">105</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;  </SPAN>I do not understand the Crown to suggest that these findings of fact
were not open to the sentencing judge. Notably, the Crown, despite having a
different take on the question of whether Mr. Mosquito had accepted
responsibility, had also concluded that the mitigating factors were sufficient
to justify a recommendation that the sentence imposed should be reduced by
three years from what it otherwise would have sought. </P></div>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="106" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="106" data-viibes-start="105" data-viibes-end="104">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par106">106</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;  </SPAN>I am simply unable to conclude, based on all of this, that the
sentence was demonstrably unfit. </P></div>

<H1>VI.<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;
</SPAN><SPAN style="text-transform:none">CONCLUSION</SPAN></H1>

<div class="paragWrapper"><div class="bootstrap unselectable viibes-marker-toolbox" data-with-parag="107" title="Paragraph tools"><div class="dropdown dropleft"><i data-toggle="dropdown" class="fa-thin fa-pen-to-square"></i><div class="dropdown-menu"></div></div></div><P class="Body" style="margin-left:0in;text-indent:0in" data-viibes-parag="107" data-viibes-start="106" data-viibes-end="105">[<A class="reflex-paragAnchor" name="par107">107</A>]<SPAN style="font:7.0pt &quot;Times New Roman&quot;">&nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp;  </SPAN>For these reasons, I would grant leave for the Crown to appeal Mr.
Mosquito&rsquo;s sentence but would dismiss the appeal. </P></div>

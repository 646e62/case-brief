CRIMINAL LAW – Appeals – Indictable offences – Powers of Court of Appeal – Legal assistance for appellant

 

The Court ordered the assignment of counsel for the appellant pursuant to s. 684 of the Criminal Code, finding that it was in the interests of justice.

 

Authorities Cited:

 

CASES CONSIDERED:  Buckingham v. R., 2004 PESCAD 21; R. v. Bernardo, 1997 CanLII 2240 (ON CA), [1997] O.J. No. 5091 (ONCA); R. v. Keats, 2017 NSCA 7.

 

 

STATUTES CONSIDERED:  Criminal Code of Canada, R.S.C. 1985, C-46, s-ss. 684(1), (2) and (3).

 

Reasons for judgment:

 

MURPHY J.A.:

 

[1]                The appellant was convicted of sexual assault by a Provincial Court judge and sentenced to 48 months less time served in pre-sentence custody.  He was represented at trial by three different lawyers, and self-represented at the sentencing hearing.  He filed a notice of appeal as a prisoner appeal on May 14, 2021.  On July 26, 2022, he filed a motion seeking an appointment of counsel.

[2]               In his notice of appeal, the appellant submits that the Crown witnesses did not tell the truth and in the circumstances their evidence was unreliable, he had witnesses whom his lawyer failed to call who will verify his whereabouts, and the lack of DNA evidence would exonerate him.  The arguments really assert:

i)                    ineffective assistance of counsel;

                        ii)         unreasonable verdict;

iii)       Charter violation.

 

He also appeals from sentence.  He argues he was not represented at the sentencing hearing and did not have the ability to present any case law that might assist him; and as a result, the hearing was one-sided.  He asserts the sentence was too long.

[3]               The appellant asked legal aid for counsel to represent him on the appeal.  However, the Director of Legal Aid denied his request, as the Director had obtained an opinion from outside counsel that the appellant’s chance of success on appeal from conviction was “very slim.” 

 

[4]              The Crown and the Attorney General appeared on this application. Both concede that the appellant does not have sufficient means to fund an appeal.  Both concede the appellant could not effectively represent himself on an appeal.  Even with the appellant’s extensive experience in dealing with court matters, his limited education and reading and writing skills along with the complexity of an appeal advise he could not effectively represent himself.  Crown and Attorney General object to the appointment of counsel solely on the basis that the appellant has not demonstrated that arguable grounds for the appeal exist. 

Application pursuant to s.684 of the Criminal Code

 

[5]               Section 684(1) of the Criminal Code provides:

             

            A court of appeal or a judge of that court may, at any time, assign counsel to act on behalf of an accused who is a party to an appeal or to proceedings preliminary or incidental to an appeal where, in the opinion of the court or judge, it appears desirable, in the interests of justice that the accused should have legal assistance and where it appears that the accused has not sufficient means to obtain that assistance.

 

[6]              Sub-section 684(2) provides that where the court assigns counsel as in s-s.(1) and legal aid is not granted to the accused pursuant to a provincial legal aid program, the fees and disbursements shall be paid by the Attorney General. 

 

[7]               The Court’s consideration of the appellant’s request for legal assistance pursuant to s. 684 is not however a review of the decision of legal aid to refuse funding (Buckingham v. R., 2004 PESCAD 21, at para. 11).

 

[8]              Since the requirement to show insufficient financial means is conceded, to obtain an order for the appointment counsel under s. 684, the appellant must satisfy the remaining criterion:

 

i)                    it appears desirable in the interests of justice that the appellant should have legal assistance.

 

 

            It appears desirable in the interests of justice to appoint counsel

 

[9]              The meaning of “desirable in the interests of justice” has been considered in many cases, most notably in R. v. Bernardo, 1997 CanLII 2240 (ON CA), [1997] O.J. No. 5091 (ONCA).  To satisfy this criterion an applicant must show:

 

a)                 the appeal is “arguable;” and

 

b)                 it is “necessary” that counsel be appointed having regard to

 

i.                    whether the applicant is capable of effectively advancing his grounds of appeal without a lawyer; and

 

ii.                  whether the court will be able to properly decide the appeal without the assistance of defence counsel.

 

[10]           The first inquiry that needs to be considered is the merits of the appeal.  The merits inquiry is a determination only of whether the appeal is arguable – that is a low threshold.  An appeal which is not frivolous and will have at least an opportunity to succeed is one that is arguable (Buckingham, supra, at para. 16; R. v. Keats, 2017 NSCA 7).

 

[11]            This case requires a cautious approach.  I have difficulty discerning whether the applicant can pass muster on the merits inquiry because it is difficult for him in his circumstances of having little education, no lawyer and being incarcerated in prison to properly enunciate and advance his grounds of appeal.

 

[12]            The following factors are present: the appellant is incarcerated, and has limited access to resources that could assist him in adequately presenting this motion;  he has a limited education and has demonstrated in his notice of appeal, participation at trial, and on presentation of this appeal motion, difficulty in weighing and processing information.  All of this impedes his ability to effectively advance argument on this motion and to marshall arguments on an appeal.

 

[13]            The appellant’s grounds of appeal from conviction relate to procedural fairness, inconsistencies and discrepancies in the evidence, and lack of credibility and unreliability of the witnesses’ testimony.  I am mindful of the applicable standard of review on these proposed grounds.  These assertions suggest unreasonable verdict and miscarriage of justice.  I am not making any conclusions on the outcome of the appeal; however, when all the circumstances are considered cumulatively, and given the remedial purpose of s. 684, in my opinion it is desirable in the interests of justice to err on the side of caution and appoint counsel for the appellant on the appeal.   

 

[14]            As for the sentence appeal, I hesitate to enter into the fray or delve too deeply into the potential merits for fear of creating any suggestion of prejudgment of this issue.  Bearing in mind the low threshold for appointment of counsel for the appeal and reflecting on the appellant’s primary ground that he was self-represented at the sentencing hearing and as a result the sentence was too long, I am satisfied regarding the sentencing appeal that this ground meets the low threshold of being arguable.  

 

Conclusion

 

[15]            The following considerations militate in favour of the appointment of counsel:

 

i)          the appellant has been convicted of sexual assault, a very serious offence that carries a lengthy prison sentence, and stigma in society.

 

ii)         the grounds of appeal meet the low threshold argument of an arguable appeal. 

 

iii)       it would be difficult for the court to properly decide the case without the assistance of counsel.

 

[16]           These words of Doherty J.A. in the Bernardo case appropriately explain the importance of appellate review for accused persons and the public interest:

 

[19]         Appellate review as provided for by Part XXI of the Criminal Code is not an indulgence to be doled out to those who are somehow seen as deserving of the opportunity to challenge their conviction. The salutary purposes underlying broad appellate review on appeals from convictions are engaged and must be served no matter how heinous the crime or despicable the accused. Detached and reflective appellate review of the trial process is perhaps most important in notorious, emotion-charged cases involving the least deserving accused. It is in those cases that the public eye is most closely focused on the process and the mettle of the criminal justice system undergoes its severest test. By giving the most repugnant appellant full recourse to meaningful appellate review, and by subjecting the apparently most deserving convictions to careful appellate scrutiny the integrity of the process is maintained and a commitment to the unbending application of the rule of law is affirmed. [Emphasis mine.]

 

[17]            In my view it appears desirable, in the interests of justice, that counsel be appointed, to effectively advance the grounds of appeal and also assist the court in properly deciding the appeal.  An order pursuant to s. 684 of the Criminal Code for the assignment of counsel should issue.

 

[18]           The Attorney General is required to fund counsel in accordance with the provisions of s-ss. 684(2) and (3) of the Criminal Code.

 

[19]           The appellant mentioned two counsel and indicated he would attempt to contact them to represent him.  If these counsel are not prepared to represent him, I remind him that he must be reasonable in accepting counsel who might be prepared to represent him.  It would be helpful if Legal Aid assisted the appellant in obtaining counsel, provided they are prepared to do so.  The appellant must also be reasonable in accepting counsel’s advice to him.   If he is unreasonable, he may find himself unrepresented and in a difficult position for appointment of counsel.

 

[20]         The appellant’s new counsel should have opportunity after review of the transcript and the notice of appeal to make any amendment to the notice of appeal counsel and the accused consider advisable.  This should be completed in a timely manner, by March 1, 2023.

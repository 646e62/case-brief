DARVILLE y. THE QUEEN
Supreme Court of Canada, Taschereau, Locke, Cartwright, Fauteux and Nolan JJ. October 24,•-1956.
J. G. O'Driscoll, for appellant.
W. M. Martin, Q.C., and D. H. Christie, for respondent.
TASCHEREAU J.:—On September 22, 1955, the appellant was charged as follows : "That Marcel Oscar Darville and Frederick Chibok, at the City of Toronto, in the County of York, on or about the 9th day of December, 1954, unlawfully did have in their possession a drug, to wit, diacetylmorphine for the purpose of trafficking, contrarÿ to Section 4(3) (b) of the Opium and Narcotic Act, R.S.C. 1952, chapter 201, and amendments there to."
The appellant was found guilty and the conviction was unani mously confirmed by the Court of Appeal of Ontario. He ob tained leave to appeal to this Court on the following ground : "Was the appellant deprived of a trial according to law by the - refusal of the learned trial Judge to grant the appellant's ap plication for an adjournement of the trial?"
The accused was arrested at the City of Toronto on December 9, 1954, and was committed for trial on January 20, 1955, and was represented by counsel at the preliminary hearing. He has continuously been in custody from the moment of his arrest on December 9, 1954. He had also the assistance of counsel until September, 1955. However, at the trial held on September 20, 1955, before a jury and His Honour Judge Forsyth, he was not represented by counsel. He pleaded not guilty, and the Crown prosecutor explained what were the challenges accorded to him by law. He was assisted by Mr. Murphy, who was acting _ for the co-accused Chibok in the selection of the jury, and the lat ter having been sworn retired temporarily.
It was then represented to the Court by Mr. Murphy that on September 2, 1955, Darville, while in custody, wrote to the Sheriff asking him to subpoena three witnesses. On the 14th of the same month, the Sheriff wrote to Darville that his letter had been shown to the Senior County Court Judge, and that he un derstood that Mr. Fred Thompson of the Globe and Mail was checking with the suggested witnesses as to the type of evidence they could give, and how it could help the accused in his trial.
Mr. Thompson was present in Court, consulted with Mr. Murphy, Q.C., and the Court was adjourned for a short recess in order to allow the appellant, Mr. Thompson and Mr. Mur- phy to confer together. After the recess, the accused-appellant addressed the Court saying that he had been in jail for many months, that he was never given the facilities to contact wit nesses, that he had written to the Sheriff, that he had no lawyer to represent him, and that he felt that he could not proceed with the case without having these witnesses. The Judge refused the application and the Court ordered the trial to proceed.
On October 11, 1955, the appellant personally served a notice of application for leave to appeal to the Court of Appeal of Ontario, against his conviction, in which he says that he has been denied his right to justice, as he remained nine and a half months in custody in the Toronto Jail, and could not prepare an adequate defence. He also states in his application that he was unable to subpoena several witnesses to prove that Detec tives Pilkington and Stewart were never at the Home Grill where the alleged transaction was supposed to have taken place, and that there was another witness, a cab-driver, who drove him at the R.C.M.P. Barracks on Beverley St. on December 7, 1954, where he intended to deliver the drug that he had in his pos session.
I do not feel that it is essential in the present case to deter mine if the trial Judge exercised his discretion in refusing the postponement of the trial in order to allow the appellant to subpoena his witnesses. If it were a proper exercise of dis cretion, I am satisfied that this exercise of discretion would not be reviewable by this Court (Mulvihill v. The King (1914), 23 Can. C.C. 194, 18 D.L.R. 217, 49 S.C.R. 587), as it would be without jurisdiction, the question being a question of fact. If the discretion of the learned trial Judge was not exercised in a judicial way, then this Court would have jurisdiction, as it would be dealing with a question of law, but in any alternative, I am of opinion that the appeal fails. I have no doubt that the appellant had all the necessary opportunities to have these al leged witnesses before the Court. Nine and a half months elapsed between the time he was incarcerated and the day of his trial. While in jail, he had been in communication with a Mr. Fred Thompson of the Globe and Mail, who inquired about
these witnesses, and who also had an interview with the Sheriff some time before the trial. At the opening of the trial, after the jury had been sworn in, the accused, Mr. Thompson and Mr. Murphy, counsel of the co-accused Chibok, conferred to gether, and nothing was shown to establish in a judicial way that the witnesses were material to the case, that there had been , no neglect in omitting to endeavour to procure their attend ance, and that there was reasonable expectation that they would be present at a future date if a postponement were granted.
Moreover, under the provisions of s. 589 (1) (b) of the Cr. Code, the Court of Appeal may, where it considered it in the interest of justice, order any witness who would have been a compellable witness at the trial, whether or not he was called at the trial, to attend and be examined before the Court of Ap peal, or to be examined in the manner provided by Rules of Court, or by any other person appointed by the Court of Ap peal for that purpose. Before the Court of Appeal, the appel lant was represented by counsel, and no application of this kind was made.
Chief Justice Pickup was therefore right when, giving the unanimous judgment of the Court, he said that there was noth ing before the Court that could indicate that there were any witnesses who could give any material evidence and were not heard at the trial.
The appeal should be dismissed.
LocKE J. concurs with TASCHEREAU J.
CARTWRIGHT J. :—The charge on which the appellant was con victed, the ground on which leave to appeal was granted, and the relevant facts, are set out in the reasons of my brother Taschereau.
In my opinion, when it appeared that the appellant, who had been in custody for some months and was without counsel at the trial, had written to the Sheriff asking that the attendance of witnesses be secured, and when the appellant stated that he could not proceed without such witnesses, it was the duty of the learned trial Judge to inform the appellant of the matters that had to be shown to entitle him to an adjournment of the trial and to give him an opportunity to be sworn in the absence of the jury and to establish the existence of these matters if he could.
There was no disagreement before us as to what conditions must ordinarily be established by affidavit in order to entitle a party to an adjournment on the ground of the absence of wit nesses, these being as follows :
(a) that the absent witnesses are material witnesses in the case;
(b) that the party applying has been guilty of no laches or neglect in omitting to endeavour to procure the attendance of these witnesses;
(c) that there is a reasonable expectation that the witnesses can be procured at the future time to which it is sought to put off the trial.
In my respectful view, it was error in law on the part of the learned trial Judge to refuse an adjournment without having given the appellant an opportunity to show, if he could, that these conditions existed.'
However, as I read the reasons of the learned Chief Justice of Ontario, one of the grounds on which the appeal was dis missed by the Court of Appeal was the failure of the appellant, who was then represented by counsel, to make any attempt to show to that Court the existence of the three conditions men tioned, as might have been done under the provisions of s. 589 of the Code, and particularly s-s. (1) (d) thereof. I am un able to say that the Court of Appeal were wrong in so deciding and on this ground I agree that the appeal should be dismissed.
FAUTEUX and NOLAN JJ. concur with TASCHEREAU J.
Appeal dismissed.

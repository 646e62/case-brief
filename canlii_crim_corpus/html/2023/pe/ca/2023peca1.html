PRACTICE AND PROCEDURE – Appeals – Time limits – Extending time for commencement of appeal

 

Application for extension of time granted.  The applications judge concluded that an extension of time to file the Notice of Appeal should be granted as the applicant satisfied the factors to be considered for granting an extension of time and the justice of the case requires that an extension be given.  The delay did not cause prejudice to the respondent.  It is desirable to have appeals decided on their merits.

 

Authorities Cited:

CASES CONSIDERED:  Bryant v. Fenton, 1998 CanLII 5372 (PESCAD); Thomas v. Thomas Estates, 2016 PECA 13; Rizzi v. Marvros, 2007 ONCA 350, (2007) 85 O.R. (3d) 401; Bratti v. Wabco Standard Trane Inc., 1994 CanLII 1261 (Ont. C.A.); Charlottetown v. Prince Edward Island (IRAC), 2012 PECA 16.

 

RULES CONSIDERED:  Prince Edward Island Rules of Civil Procedure, Rules 3.02, 61.03(1).

 

Reasons for judgment:

 

MURPHY J.A.:

 

[1]               The applicant has filed an application pursuant to Rule 3.02 of the Rules of Civil Procedure requesting this Court to extend the time to file a Notice of Appeal from an order of the Supreme Court filed September 23, 2022.  The Order ensued following a motion held on June 20, 2022.  The Order provided for matters relating to communication, exchanges of the children, and child support for three children of the applicant and respondent.

[2]               The grounds for the application are:

i)          the applicant was not able to file within the prescribed time as P.E.I. was hit by Hurricane Fiona on September 24, 2022.  This caused him to lose power and therefore did not have access to Wi-Fi to allow him to research and prepare the materials needed on appeal.

ii)         the applicant was mentally stressed and also has a medical condition which is acerbated by stress and was thus physically unable to prepare the appeal documents.

iii)       the respondent’s interests are not prejudiced at this stage of the appeal process.

[3]               The respondent’s position is:

i)         the appeal does not have merit.  The respondent asserts that trial judges make orders for child support on a regular basis.  The decision was very routine requiring an analysis of the incomes of the parties and no error was made here.

 

ii)         the applicant did not have a reasonable excuse for the delay.  The respondent suggests the applicant was beyond the time limit for filing the appeal by three weeks.  The respondent admits Hurricane Fiona and the aftermath of having no power, and the courthouse not being operational caused a 10-day delay.  The respondent asserts the applicant’s medical problem should not be considered as a reasonable excuse for the delay, and the medical evidence does not assist him as he was advancing other litigation with the respondent around this time.

 

iii)        the respondent suggests the better course for the applicant would be to seek a variation of the judge’s order rather than proceeding with an appeal of the judge’s order.

 

iv)        the respondent concedes that the applicant had a bona fide intention to appeal early on.

 

v)         the respondent admits that no real prejudice has occurred to the detriment of the respondent as a result of the delay.

 

Issue:  Should this Court grant an extension of time to allow the applicant to file a notice of appeal?

 

            The Law

[4]              The Rules of Civil Procedure provide in Rule 61.03(1) that an appeal to the Court of Appeal shall be commenced by properly serving a notice of appeal within thirty days after the filing of the order appealed from unless a statute or the rules provide otherwise. 

 

[5]               Compliance with the aforesaid time limit is a critical requirement.  Time limits are generally construed to be mandatory.  Accordingly, unless there is a provision for extension of a time limit, non-compliance with the time limit will preclude an appellate court from entertaining an appeal.

 

[6]              Rule 3.02 permits the Court to grant an extension of time on such terms as are just.

 

[7]               In exercising discretion to grant relief from compliance with the time limit for commencing an appeal, appeal courts attempt to reconcile the competing interests of finality and certainty for the successful party to the proceeding and the effect for the unsuccessful party of failing to comply with the time limit.  One competing interest approaches the issue from the premise that a successful party has a vested right once the time for appeal has expired.  The other competing interest is that “it is desirable to have appeals decided on their merits,” unless a respondent has been prejudiced by the delay (Bryant v. Fenton, 1998 CanLII 5372 (PESCAD)).  Courts have attempted to reconcile these competing interests by considering several factors while maintaining an overriding power to ensure that the “interests of justice” in each individual case should govern (Thomas v. Thomas Estates, 2016 PECA 13, paras. 4-7).

 

[8]              This Court in previous decisions, including Thomas at para. 7, has cited the applicable factors to be considered in deciding whether to grant an extension of time to be:

 

1.      Was there a bona fide intention to appeal?

 

2.      Does the appeal have merit?

 

3.      Is there a reasonable excuse for a delay in not filing within the prescribed time?

 

4.      Are there exceptional or special circumstances justifying the extension of time?

 

[9]              The Ontario Court of Appeal treats the question of whether the “justice of the case” requires that an extension be given as the governing principle, and employs all of the mentioned factors, as well as prejudice to the other party as factors (Rizzi v. Marvros, 2007 ONCA 350, (2007) 85 O.R. (3d) 401; following Bratti v. Wabco Standard Trane Inc., 1994 CanLII 1261 (Ont. C.A.)).

 

Assessment of the Application for an Extension of Time

 

(i)                 Was there a bona fide intention to appeal?

 

[10]           The respondent concedes that the applicant had a bona fide intention to appeal the motion judge’s decision.  The applicant submitted that when the Order was being settled and his requested changes were not included in the final order, he decided to appeal.  Hurricane Fiona landed on our Province on September 23, 2022, the day after the Order was signed.  Normal operations resumed at the Supreme Court on October 3, 2022.  On October 5, 2022, the applicant attended at the Supreme Court to pick up a CD of the hearing.  His evidence is when he reviewed the CD on the weekend following its receipt to work on the appeal his work was averted as he did not have an external CD player to play the CD and had to search for someone who had a computer with this capacity.  As well, the CD did not contain the judge’s decision so he had to return to the Supreme Court to obtain the reasons which were provided to him on a thumb drive.

 

[11]            It would be reasonable to conclude that the applicant had a bona fide intention to appeal, in early October when he sought to obtain the judge’s reasons.

 

        (ii)               Does the appeal have merit?

 

[12]            In the draft Notice of Appeal filed by the applicant he asserts that the motions judge denied him procedural fairness in the conduct of the hearing by interrupting him and discontinuing his cross-examination.  Allegations of bias are also made.  He also submits that the judge erred in calculating the child support payable as the calculation did not include all sources of income of the respondent.

 

[13]            In his supplementary affidavit filed he transcribes a portion of the transcript.  I acknowledge the transcript portions are not verified but taken together with the grounds in the draft Notice of Appeal, it is fair to say that the grounds of appeal are arguable.  The threshold concerning the merits of an appeal is not high – all that needs to be shown is that the appeal is arguable (Charlottetown v. Prince Edward Island (IRAC), 2012 PECA 16, para. 12).

 

[14]            The respondent submits that the calculation of child support is a pretty routine and regular occurrence in the Supreme Court.  I am not pre-judging the appeal.  However, if all sources of income were not included in the calculation for child support, an error could have occurred.  This ground of appeal could be considered arguable.

 

           (iii)      Is there a reasonable excuse for a delay in not filing within the prescribed time?

 

[15]            The applicant filed an affidavit in support of his application.  He states he made best efforts to create the required documents but was hindered in his efforts by the hurricane that struck our province.  For a period of time the Supreme Court was not operational and for a short period of time, a few days, he was without power and could not conduct research and prepare his notice of appeal.  He occasioned further delay in obtaining the transcript and reasons of the trial judge.  I am of the view that the combination of factors provides a reasonable excuse for not filing the Notice of Appeal within the prescribed time.

 

         (iv)          Are there exceptional or special circumstances justifying the extension of time?

 

[16]           With the notice of application for an extension of time the applicant filed a one-page document entitled “Master Patient Index Report dated November 29, 2022.”  It contained scant information – only the dates, times, and short references, i.e., “Nursing/Physician” to the medical service provided.  In the case management conference I had with the parties, I requested that the applicant file more detailed medical information.  In his supplementary affidavit he provided his attending physicians report and emergency documentation from the Queen Elizabeth Hospital.  His family physician indicated he has an anxiety and panic disorder that causes difficulty concentrating, communicating and difficulty with cognitive tasks which is brought on by the stress of the court matters he is dealing with.  At the hearing of the application, he filed a letter from his Gastroenterology Nurse Practitioner.  This medical evidence discloses that the applicant has Crohn’s disease which is being managed aggressively requiring IV iron therapy and blood transfusions. 

 

[17]            I am satisfied based on the medical evidence that the applicant’s medical conditions constitute special circumstances that justify granting an extension of time to file an appeal.

 

Conclusion

 

[18]           When an assessment of the applicable factors for granting an extension of time to appeal is made, the applicant has demonstrated that the justice of the case requires that an extension be provided.

 

[19]           In this case the desirability of deciding appeals on their merits outweighs the competing interest of finality as the respondent has not shown any significant prejudice from the delay.  As a result, I would grant the applicant a short extension up to and including February 1, 2023, to file his Notice of appeal.

 

[20]         I would order that costs shall be in the cause.

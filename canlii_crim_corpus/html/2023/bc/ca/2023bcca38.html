Summary:

The appellant appeals his conviction on a number of firearm offences. During a Motor Vehicle Act stop, the police noted that the appellant appeared startled when the police drove past him in a high crime area, his car was registered to an individual much older than him, he drove off rapidly in the opposite direction, and he appeared agitated and nervous when he was pulled over. The police decided to conduct a pat‑down search because of safety concerns arising from the appellant’s behaviour, and when the officer attempted to do this, she noticed the appellant tried turning one side of his body away from her where he had a bag hanging across his body. The police observed what they believed to be drug paraphernalia and detained the appellant for a drug investigation. The police then advised the appellant of his right to counsel. Upon continuing the pat‑down search, the police felt a gun in the appellant’s bag and arrested him, and further advised him of his Charter rights. The appellant’s access to counsel was not facilitated until he was transported to the Vancouver jail. On appeal, the appellant alleges that the trial judge erred in finding that his ss. 8 and 10(b) Charter rights were not infringed.

Held: Appeal dismissed. With respect to the s. 8 issue, the trial judge did not err by applying the wrong legal test for justifying the police’s safety search incidental to investigative detention. The law does not require an imminent threat for such a search to be lawful, and doing so would set too high a bar and prevent the police from taking immediate steps to address risks to their safety and that of the public. Rather, the threshold standard is whether the officer had reasonable suspicion to believe that the detainee may pose a threat to the officer or the public.

With respect to the s. 10(b) issue, the trial judge did not err by finding that the failure of police to provide access to counsel at the roadside when the appellant was arrested violated his s. 10(b) rights. The right to have access to counsel “without delay” does not mean the duty to facilitate access must be near‑immediate. It was reasonable for the police to consider the surrounding circumstances and conclude that it would not be practical or safe to provide the appellant with access to counsel roadside.

Reasons for Judgment of the Honourable Justice Skolrood:

Introduction

[1]         On January 29, 2021, the appellant, Devinder Singh Dhillon, was found guilty in the Provincial Court of British Columbia of a number of firearm offences. The charges stemmed from an incident that occurred on July 12, 2019, when he was found to be in possession of a loaded handgun. The weapon was discovered when Mr. Dhillon was searched by police officers following a traffic stop that occurred in the Downtown Eastside of Vancouver.

[2]         At trial, Mr. Dhillon alleged that his rights under ss. 8, 9, 10(a), and 10(b) of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms had been infringed and he sought to exclude evidence of the loaded handgun pursuant to s. 24(2) of the Charter. A voir dire was held to consider the alleged breaches. The judge held that Mr. Dhillon’s Charter rights had not been infringed. He dismissed the application under s. 24(2) and convicted Mr. Dhillon.

[3]         On appeal, Mr. Dhillon submits that the judge erred in failing to find that his rights under ss. 8 and 10(b) were infringed. With respect to s. 8, Mr. Dhillon submits that the judge applied the wrong test in finding that the police officers were justified in subjecting him to a pat‑down safety search as part of an investigative detention. Specifically, Mr. Dhillon submits that there must be an imminent threat to an officer or to public safety in order for a safety search to be lawful and that such a threat was absent here.

[4]         With respect to s. 10(b), Mr. Dhillon submits that the judge erred in finding that the police had not infringed his right to instruct counsel without delay when they failed to facilitate access to counsel roadside upon him being detained, but rather waited until he was transported to the Vancouver jail.

Background

[5]         As Mr. Dhillon’s Charter arguments turn largely on the interactions between him and the two police officers involved in the traffic stop, it is necessary to review the background circumstances in some detail.

[6]         On July 12, 2019, Constables Waller and Battle of the Vancouver Police Department were on general patrol in a marked police vehicle. Cst. Battle was driving and Cst. Waller was in the passenger seat. At approximately 7:30 pm, they drove into an open parking lot in the 100 block of Keefer Street in Vancouver where they were aware that stolen vehicles had previously been located. Cst. Waller testified that she was searching licence plates of vehicles in the lot to determine if the vehicles had been reported stolen.

[7]         While doing so, Cst. Waller observed a silver BMW in the lot with the engine running and parked in an adjacent laneway at an odd angle. She thought the vehicle looked out of place so decided to query the licence plate. Cst. Battle drove out of the parking lot and into the laneway, past the BMW. According to Cst. Waller, when they passed the BMW, she made eye contact with the driver, a South Asian male in his early 20s, who appeared surprised and alarmed at seeing the police. It was an admitted fact at trial that the driver of the BMW was Mr. Dhillon.

[8]         Cst. Battle stopped the police car further up the laneway and Cst. Waller queried the licence plate of the BMW by accessing a database available in the police car. She determined that the registered owner of the BMW was 68 years old and that the vehicle was associated with multiple police files, including a “take auto without consent” matter, and a recent file regarding assault with a weapon. Cst. Waller decided to check the vehicle but before she could do so, she heard a “peel out” sound and saw the BMW driving down the laneway away from the police car at a speed that she estimated to be 10 to 15 kilometres per hour above the laneway speed limit of 20 kilometres per hour.

[9]         According to Cst. Waller, this action along with Mr. Dhillon’s facial expression, caused her to believe that he was attempting to avoid the police. She testified that at that point, she was intending to stop the vehicle to check the driver’s licence, confirm proper registration and permission for the driver to have possession, and to ticket the driver for speeding.

[10]      Cst. Battle gave similar evidence about intending to stop the vehicle in order to ensure the driver had a proper licence and permission to use the vehicle. Cst. Battle said that she also formed the opinion that the driver of the vehicle was intending to evade the police.

[11]      The police officers followed the BMW for a short period, then pulled it over in the 300 block of East Hastings Street. Both officers got out of the police car and approached the BMW. Cst. Battle went to the driver‑side window and Cst. Waller to the passenger side.

[12]      Cst. Battle said that she identified herself and asked Mr. Dhillon to produce his driver’s licence and insurance registrations documents. She said that he appeared nervous; there were beads of sweat on his forehead and his hands were shaking to the point that he had difficulty removing the insurance registration document from its plastic sleeve.

[13]      According to Cst. Battle, she wanted to check the rear seat area to see if it was occupied, but the rear window had a black mesh screen that made it impossible for her to see into the rear passenger compartment. She therefore asked Mr. Dhillon to roll down the rear windows but he refused, telling her she needed a warrant. He subsequently did comply with the request to roll the windows down.

[14]      Cst. Waller testified that while she could not hear all of the discussion between Cst. Battle and Mr. Dhillon, she did hear him ask repeatedly why he had been stopped. She described Mr. Dhillon as agitated and verbally confrontational.

[15]      Mr. Dhillon produced his driver’s licence and vehicle registration to Cst. Battle. During this interaction, Cst. Battle described Mr. Dhillon as one of the most nervous people she had encountered in her albeit short career as a police officer doing traffic stops.

[16]      Cst. Waller also observed Mr. Dhillon’s behaviour. She said he was shaking so badly that he could barely keep a lit cigarette in his hand. She said his eyes were darting and he was looking towards the road. She said she interpreted his behaviour to suggest that he was either contemplating fleeing or becoming assaultive. She decided that they needed to get Mr. Dhillon out of the car “before something bad was going to happen”. Cst. Waller therefore instructed Cst. Battle to remove Mr. Dhillon from the car.

[17]      Cst. Battle directed Mr. Dhillon to open the door and get out of the car. She said he challenged her authority to require him to do so but nonetheless complied. She said he continued to exhibit nervous behaviour.

[18]      Cst. Battle then returned to the police car to run Mr. Dhillon’s name through the police database. She left him on the sidewalk with Cst. Waller, who also described him as continuing to be highly agitated and nervous. Cst. Waller in fact asked Mr. Dhillon why he was so nervous and whether he had had police interactions in the past. He said he had but he denied being under any court‑imposed conditions.

[19]      Cst. Waller said that during this interaction, Mr. Dhillon was compliant but still agitated and asking why he had been stopped. She told him that he had been speeding in the laneway, the location of his vehicle was suspicious, and he did not match the description of the registered owner.

[20]      Cst. Waller then advised Mr. Dhillon that she was going to conduct a safety pat‑down of his person. She said she started to do so but he continued to move away from her and was “blading”, or turning one side of his body away where he had what was described as a “man purse” hanging.

[21]      Cst. Waller testified that she decided to do the pat‑down search because of safety concerns arising from Mr. Dhillon’s behaviour. She believed it was possible that he had something that could harm her. When he started blading the side of his body away from her to shield the bag, she became concerned that there was something in the bag that could harm her.

[22]      Mr. Dhillon’s behaviour, including the fact that he was moving away from her which made it difficult to conduct the pat‑down search, caused her to decide to place him in handcuffs. She therefore gestured for Cst. Battle to come and assist her. When asked why she placed Mr. Dhillon in handcuffs at this point, Cst. Waller said she was “extremely fearful” that there was something in his bag that could harm her.

[23]      Cst. Waller testified that as she understood the proper scope of a safety search, she could search for weapons or other things that could harm her or others, but that she could not search for evidence.

[24]      After Mr. Dhillon was placed in handcuffs, Cst. Waller observed multiple monetary bills and what she erroneously believed to be drug paraphernalia protruding from his bag. These items, along with the location of the vehicle when first observed, which was an area known for drug trafficking, led her to suspect that Mr. Dhillon was involved in drug activity. I note that the item that appeared to be drug paraphernalia turned out to be a vape pen.

[25]      At that point, Cst. Waller advised Mr. Dhillon that he was being detained for a drug investigation and she read him his Charter rights. Cst. Waller asked Mr. Dhillon if he wanted to call a lawyer and he replied “yes”. I will return to the facts relating to the s. 10(b) Charter issue shortly.

[26]      Cst. Waller continued with the pat‑down of Mr. Dhillon, but she realized that even in handcuffs, he could still access his bag. She, therefore, had Cst. Battle pin one of his arms against his vehicle so that she could continue with her search. In the course of doing so, Cst. Waller observed that the bag appeared to be weighted down and it had a bulge in it. She put her hand on the bag and recognized the bulge to be a gun. She immediately advised Cst. Battle and other officers who had arrived that she believed there to be a gun in the bag.

[27]      Cst. Waller unzipped the bag and saw a handgun. She then cut the strap of the bag and removed it from Mr. Dhillon. Mr. Dhillon was arrested and again advised of his Charter rights.

[28]      The sequence and timing of events relevant to the s. 10(b) access to counsel issue are set out in a set of admissions filed on the Charter voir dire as follows:

2.   Mr. Dhillon was pulled over by police in his vehicle at 7:39 p.m.

3.   Mr. Dhillon was detained for a drug investigation and read his s. 10(a) and (b) Charter rights at 7:44 p.m.

4.   Mr. Dhillon was arrested for possession of a firearm and possession of a dangerous weapon and again read his s. 10(a) and (b) Charter rights at 7:47 p.m.

5.   The police requested a VPD police wagon to transport Mr. Dhillon from the location of his arrest to Vancouver jail (the “jail”) at 7:57 p.m.

6.   Mr. Dhillon was placed in a VPD police wagon for transport to the jail at 8:11 p.m.

7.   Mr. Dhillon arrived at the jail at 8:37 p.m.

8.   Mr. Dhillon was on the phone with a legal aid lawyer at 8:48 p.m.

9.   The time period between 8:11 p.m. and 8:48 p.m. was the time required for police and jail guards to transport Mr. Dhillon from the scene of his arrest to the jail and partially complete the jail booking procedure for prisoners, which is required for reasons of officer and prisoner safety. As part of the booking-in process, prisoners are subjected to a search to ensure that they are not placed alone in a private room to phone legal counsel while being in possession of weapons that they could use to harm themselves or others. Aside from partially completing the booking procedure, nothing else occurred between 8:37 p.m. and 8:48 p.m.

10.  The jail guards acted expeditiously to complete Mr. Dhillon’s booking procedure once he had arrived at the jail and acted promptly to facilitate his access to counsel in a private phone booth after Mr. Dhillon had undergone an initial safety search.

11.  No police office or jail guard attempted to elicit evidence from Mr. Dhillon nor received any information from him between 8:11 p.m. and the time at which he spoke to a lawyer at 8:48 p.m.

[29]      In addition to these agreed facts, Cst. Waller testified that she did not facilitate Mr. Dhillon’s access to counsel when he was arrested and requested to call a lawyer because she only had her personal cell phone with her and she was unwilling to allow him to use it. She thought it would be unsafe to allow Mr. Dhillon to use his own phone to contact counsel and she could not afford him privacy where they were standing in the 300 block of East Hastings Street. She said that the police have a policy or practice of not placing suspects in police patrol vehicles so she requested a transport vehicle to come and take Mr. Dhillon to the Vancouver jail where there are procedures in place to allow private phone calls with counsel.

Ruling on the Charter Voir Dire

[30]      The judge held that while the police officers likely formed an initial suspicion that Mr. Dhillon was involved in the drug trade or that he was in possession of a stolen vehicle, there was no good reason to disbelieve the officers’ evidence as to why they first decided to perform a vehicle stop. The judge accepted both officers’ evidence that they intended to check Mr. Dhillon’s status as a driver, the insurance status of the vehicle, and his permission to have possession of the vehicle. However, before that vehicle stop could be effected, Mr. Dhillon sped away.

[31]      The judge noted that despite rigorous cross‑examination of both police officers, he did not find that they had either jointly or severally contrived a plan to pull Mr. Dhillon over under the guise of a legitimate Motor Vehicle Act stop on nothing more than a hunch that he might be involved in criminal activity. Rather, the judge was satisfied that the vehicle stop was initiated for valid Motor Vehicle Act purposes.

[32]      Having found that the detention of Mr. Dhillon was valid, the judge then considered whether Cst. Waller was justified in conducting the pat‑down search. The judge cited R. v. Mann, 2004 SCC 52, where it was held that a police officer may conduct a pat‑down search incident to a lawful detention if the officer believes on reasonable grounds that his or her own safety or the safety of others is at risk.

[33]      The judge cited a number of authorities from this Court including R. v. Thibodeau, 2007 BCCA 489, leave to appeal to SCC ref’d, 32377 (28 February 2008), R. v. Sheck, 2015 BCCA 471, leave to appeal to SCC ref’d 36735 (12 May 2016), R. v. Webber, 2019 BCCA 208, and R. v. Patrick, 2017 BCCA 57, leave to appeal to SCC ref’d 37514 (03 October 2017), which address the test for and scope of safety searches. The judge noted that in Patrick, this Court held that an officer’s subjective perception of a risk to safety must be reasonable in the circumstances.

[34]      The judge then set out a number of circumstances identified by Cst. Waller that, in the judge’s view, gave rise to a reasonable suspicion of a safety risk and that accordingly justified the pat‑down search:

a)   Mr. Dhillon’s startled look when the police drove past him in a high crime area;

b)   the database information associating the BMW to an assault file and a “take auto without consent” file;

c)   the fact that Mr. Dhillon drove off rapidly in the opposite direction;

d)   Mr. Dhillon’s confrontational or agitated behaviour when he was pulled over and being dealt with;

e)   the physical signs of nervousness that Mr. Dhillon exhibited both inside and outside his vehicle, primarily the beads of sweat on his forehead, his shaking hands, and his darting eye movements; and

f)     Mr. Dhillon’s body movements to adjust the position of his bag away from Cst. Waller when she told him she was going to pat him down for safety.

[35]      With respect to s. 10(b), the judge held that there was no obligation on the officers to advise Mr. Dhillon of his right to retain and instruct counsel when he was initially detained for Motor Vehicle Act purposes. That obligation arose when Mr. Dhillon was detained for suspected drug trafficking and at that point, Cst. Waller advised him of his rights.

[36]      In terms of facilitating access to counsel, the judge found that it was not reasonable to expect that police could provide secure and private access to counsel at the roadside, particularly in the Downtown Eastside of Vancouver. The judge found that it was reasonable to delay access to counsel until Mr. Dhillon was booked into the jail. While there was some delay in waiting for the transport vehicle and while Mr. Dhillon was being processed at the jail, that delay was not unreasonable. The judge noted in his reasons that the principal ground relied on in support of the s. 10(b) argument was the failure of the police to provide a cell phone at the scene, which again the judge found not to be unreasonable.

Analysis

[37]      As noted at the outset, Mr. Dhillon alleges that the judge erred in finding that his rights under ss. 8 and 10(b) of the Charter were not breached. I will address each of these grounds of appeal in turn.

Section 8: Unreasonable Search

[38]      The central issue on this ground of appeal is whether the judge applied the correct test in finding that Cst. Waller was justified in conducting the pat‑down safety search of Mr. Dhillon that led to discovery of the handgun.

[39]      Mr. Dhillon alleges that in order for a police officer to conduct a safety search incidental to an investigative detention, the officer must have objectively reasonable grounds to believe that there is an “imminent threat” to their safety or the safety of others. He submits that the evidence here fell short of the “imminent threat” standard and that accordingly, the judge erred in finding that the pat‑down search of Mr. Dhillon was not unreasonable and in breach of s. 8 of the Charter.

[40]      The Crown submits that there is no legal requirement of an imminent threat in order to justify a protective safety search. Rather, the officer must have a reasonable suspicion that a protective search is necessary to ensure officer and/or public safety. The Crown submits further that an imminent threat test would impose a dangerously high standard on police officers and is not required by the relevant authorities.

Relevant Case Law

Supreme Court of Canada

[41]      Both parties acknowledge that the starting point for considering the test for a valid protective or safety search is Mann. In Mann, police officers received a radio call reporting a break and enter in progress and providing a description of the suspect. As the officers approached the scene, they observed an individual, the accused, who matched the description they had been given. The officers stopped the accused and asked him to identify himself. He did so and also complied with a pat‑down search for concealed weapons. During the search, the officer felt a soft object in the accused’s pocket. The officer reached into the pocket and found a plastic bag containing marijuana. Further searches of the accused’s pockets revealed additional drugs. The accused was arrested and charged with possession of marijuana for the purpose of trafficking.

[42]      The trial judge held that the search of the accused’s pockets was unreasonable and he excluded the evidence of the marijuana, resulting in an acquittal. The Manitoba Court of Appeal set aside the acquittal and ordered a new trial. On further appeal to the Supreme Court of Canada, the acquittal was restored.

[43]      Justice Iacobucci for the majority reviewed the development of the common law governing investigative detention and searches incidental thereto. He noted that the courts have recognized the importance of ensuring officer safety and that a protective or safety search is a relatively non‑intrusive procedure (at para. 43, citing Cloutier v. Langlois, 1990 CanLII 122 (SCC), [1990] 1 S.C.R. 158 at 185).

[44]      Justice Iacobucci summarized the governing principles:

[45]      To summarize, as discussed above, police officers may detain an individual for investigative purposes if there are reasonable grounds to suspect in all the circumstances that the individual is connected to a particular crime and that such a detention is necessary. In addition, where a police officer has reasonable grounds to believe that his or her safety or that of others is at risk, the officer may engage in a protective pat-down search of the detained individual. Both the detention and the pat-down search must be conducted in a reasonable manner. In this connection, I note that the investigative detention should be brief in duration and does not impose an obligation on the detained individual to answer questions posed by the police. The investigative detention and protective search power are to be distinguished from an arrest and the incidental power to search on arrest, which do not arise in this case.

[Emphasis added.]

[45]      Applying these principles, Iacobucci J. found that there were reasonable grounds to conduct a protective search of the accused. However, there was no reasonable basis for the officer to search the accused’s pockets and that in doing so, the officer violated the accused’s rights under s. 8 of the Charter.

[46]      In R. v. Chehil, 2013 SCC 49, the Supreme Court of Canada confirmed reasonable suspicion as the threshold test for justifying a protective search incident to investigative detention (at para. 23 and fn. 1).

[47]      As I will return to when considering decisions of this Court, Mann has long been cited as the leading decision governing protective or safety searches incidental to an investigative detention. However, Mr. Dhillon submits that the Supreme Court of Canada effectively changed the test and established an “imminent threat” requirement for such searches in R. v. MacDonald, 2014 SCC 3.

[48]      In that case, police responded to a noise complaint at the accused’s condominium. An officer attended the unit, knocked on the door, and asked the accused to turn his music down. The accused swore at the officer and slammed the door.

[49]      A second officer arrived and the two officers attempted to get the accused to open his door. After some time, the accused opened the door partially—enough to permit one officer to observe the right side of the accused’s body and his face. The officer noticed something black and shiny in the accused’s hand that was partially hidden. The officer believed it might be a knife. He asked the accused what he was holding but got no response. The officer then pushed the door open further and saw a handgun. The officer pushed his way in, struggled with the accused, and disarmed him. The accused was subsequently charged with a number of firearm offences.

[50]      Both the trial judge and the Nova Scotia Court of Appeal held that the accused’s s. 8 rights were not infringed by the police officer’s actions in pushing the door open. The Supreme Court of Canada came to a similar conclusion, although the Court split 4–3 on the test to be applied for a justifiable officer safety search.

[51]      Mr. Dhillon relies heavily on the majority reasons written by Justice LeBel. Justice LeBel noted that the framework for scrutinizing warrantless searches for Charter compliance had been set out in Mann and said:

[32]        A search that is reasonably necessary to eliminate threats to the safety of the public or the police — which I will term a “safety search” — will generally be conducted by the police as a reactionary measure. In other words, although such searches may arise in a wide variety of contexts, they will generally be unplanned, as they will be carried out in response to dangerous situations created by individuals, to which the police must react “on the sudden”. Binnie J.’s observation in [R. v. A.M., 2008 SCC 19] in relation to sniffer-dog searches that “the police are generally required to take quick action guided by on-the-spot observations” (at para. 90) is equally applicable to safety searches. Thus, safety searches will typically be warrantless, as the police will generally not have sufficient time to obtain prior judicial authorization for them. In a sense, such searches are driven by exigent circumstances. Even if exigent circumstances exist, however, “safety searches” must be authorized by law.

[52]      Justice LeBel then reviewed previous jurisprudence from the Court dealing with police officers’ common law authority to conduct safety searches and came to the following conclusions, which are central to Mr. Dhillon’s position on this appeal:

[40]        On balancing these factors, I am convinced that the duty of police officers to protect life and safety may justify the power to conduct a safety search in certain circumstances. At the very least, where a search is reasonably necessary to eliminate an imminent threat to the safety of the public or the police, the police should have the power to conduct the search.

[41]        But although I acknowledge the importance of safety searches, I must repeat that the power to carry one out is not unbridled. In my view, the principles laid down in Mann and reaffirmed in [R. v. Clayton, 2007 SCC 32] require the existence of circumstances establishing the necessity of safety searches, reasonably and objectively considered, to address an imminent threat to the safety of the public or the police. Given the high privacy interests at stake in such searches, the search will be authorized by law only if the police officer believes on reasonable grounds that his or her safety is at stake and that, as a result, it is necessary to conduct a search (Mann, at para. 40; see also para. 45). The legality of the search therefore turns on its reasonable, objectively verifiable necessity in the circumstances of the matter (see R. v. Tse, 2012 SCC 16, [2012] 1 S.C.R. 531, at para. 33). As the Court stated in Mann, a search cannot be justified on the basis of a vague concern for safety. Rather, for a safety search to be lawful, the officer must act on “reasonable and specific inferences drawn from the known facts of the situation” (Mann, at para. 41).

[Emphasis added.]

[53]      Justice LeBel then reiterated:

[43]      … it is only when police officers have reasonable grounds to believe that there is an imminent threat to their safety that it will be reasonably necessary to conduct such a search. This limit guarantees that the lawful police power is not excessively broad. In so doing, it ensures that the law itself is reasonable and can be reasonably delineated.

[Emphasis added.]

[54]      The minority (Moldaver and Wagner JJ., as he then was, writing), came to the same conclusion on the result as the majority, but took issue with the majority’s formulation of the test to justify an officer safety search. The point of departure is succinctly summarized below:

[66]      In the instant case, while we agree with the majority on all three issues in this case, including Mr. MacDonald’s claim under s. 8 of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms, we part company with our colleagues on the proper interpretation of Mann. Our colleagues assert, relying on Mann, that officers are only empowered to conduct “safety searches” where they have reasonable grounds to believe an individual is armed and dangerous (paras. 39 and 44). With respect, we do not agree with that conclusion. In our view, Mann decided that officers may conduct safety searches when they have reasonable grounds to suspect an individual is armed and dangerous. The balance of these reasons will focus on explaining why. 

[55]      As can be seen, in the opinion of the minority, the majority requirement of “reasonable grounds to believe” in a threat to safety is an unjustified modification of the “reasonable grounds to suspect” test established by Mann and sets a dangerously high standard.

[56]      While I will return to MacDonald, it is useful to note at this point that the focus of the decision, as reflected in both the majority and minority reasons, is on the threshold test for justifying a safety search (reasonable grounds to believe versus reasonable suspicion) of a threat to safety, and not on the nature of the perceived threat, i.e., whether it need be “imminent”.

[57]      The issue of warrantless searches has been addressed by the Supreme Court of Canada in two more recent decisions. While not directly on point, they are instructive nonetheless.

[58]      In R. v. Stairs, 2022 SCC 11, a 9‑1‑1 call reported a man repeatedly hitting a woman in a car parked in the driveway of a house. Police attended the house, knocked on the front door, and announced their attendance. When no one answered, they entered the house, fearing for the safety of the woman. A woman with injuries to her face came up the stairs from the basement and the police observed the accused run by the bottom of the stairs. He then barricaded himself in a room in the basement. Police managed to extract him from the room and he was arrested. After the arrest, the police conducted a visual search of the area which revealed containers of methamphetamine and which resulted in the accused being charged with a drug offence. At trial, the accused challenged the search as being a breach of his s. 8 rights.

[59]      The accused was unsuccessful on this point before the trial judge and the Court of Appeal for Ontario. A majority of the Supreme Court of Canada (Moldaver and Jamal JJ. writing) dismissed his further appeal.

[60]      The central issue before the Court was the test for determining whether a search of an arrested person’s home conducted incident to arrest is unreasonable and thus a breach of s. 8 of the Charter.

[61]      The majority began its analysis by noting the common law standard for search incident to arrest which, generally speaking, “permits the police to search a lawfully arrested person and to seize anything in their possession or the surrounding area of the arrest to guarantee the safety of the police and arrested person, prevent the person’s escape, or provide evidence against them (at para. 34, citing Cloutier at pp. 180–181).

[62]      The majority then held that the common law standard must be modified where an arrest and search incident thereto takes place in a person’s home, given the heightened privacy interest people have in their homes. In doing so, they rejected both the Crown’s position that the existing common law standard for searches incident to arrest provides sufficient protection, and the accused’s position that a search is only justified if there is a reasonable belief of imminent harm. Rather, the majority adopted a standard falling between those positions (at para. 55).

[63]      It is not necessary to review the majority reasons in detail. For present purposes, the important point to note is the finding that in order for police to lawfully search areas of a home outside of the arrested person’s physical control, “the police must have reason to suspect that there is a safety risk to the police, the accused, or the public which would be addressed by a search” (at para. 56).

[64]      The majority described the “reasonable suspicion” standard in these terms:

[66]      Like the common law test, the purpose of the search must be subjectively connected to the arrest, and the officer’s belief that the purpose will be served by the search must be objectively reasonable. However, the objective requirement is stricter. To meet this stricter standard, the Crown must establish “objective facts [that] rise to the level of reasonable suspicion, such that a reasonable person, standing in the shoes of the police officer, would have held a reasonable suspicion” (R. v. Chehil, 2013 SCC 49, [2013] 3 S.C.R. 220, at para. 45).

…

[68]      … to establish reasonable suspicion, the police require a constellation of objectively discernible facts assessed against the totality of the circumstances giving rise to the suspicion of the risk. This assessment must be “fact‑based, flexible, and grounded in common sense and practical, everyday experience” (Chehil, at para. 29). In addition, the police must have reason to suspect that the search will address the risk. However, reasonable suspicion is a lower standard than reasonable and probable grounds because it is based on a possibility rather than a probability (Chehil, at para. 32).

[65]      The majority went on to explain why it rejected an “imminent harm” standard and, in doing so, it distinguished MacDonald. Specifically, the majority noted that in MacDonald, the accused was not under arrest when police forced their way into the home, thus he retained a strong expectation of privacy which required heightened grounds on the part of the police to justify entry, i.e., a reasonable belief in imminent harm (at para. 76).

[66]      Lastly, the majority identified independent reasons for rejecting the imminent harm standard:

[77]      ... First, because a search incident to arrest typically occurs at the early stages of an investigation, the police will often be unable to show reasonable and probable grounds. Setting the bar too high will prevent the police from acting promptly, taking immediate steps to address risks to their safety and the safety of others, including innocent bystanders. Second, an imminence requirement would practically proscribe the search incident to arrest power, as it would simply restate the exigency exception. If there were exigent circumstances, the police could act solely on that basis. There would be no need for the power to search incident to arrest.

[67]      The other recent Supreme Court of Canada decision is R. v. Tim, 2022 SCC 12, where police were investigating the accused for a traffic accident in which he struck a roadside sign and kept driving until his vehicle stopped working. An officer found the accused standing on the roadside by his damaged vehicle. The officer asked the accused for his licence, vehicle registration, and proof of insurance. When the accused returned to his vehicle to get the documents, the officer observed him trying to hide a pill. The officer mistakenly believed the pill to be a controlled substance and he therefore arrested the accused. The officer then conducted a pat‑down search of the accused and a search of the vehicle incident to arrest. The search of the accused person found ammunition and drugs as did the search of the vehicle. A second search of the accused’s person was conducted, which revealed a loaded handgun.

[68]      The accused argued that he was unlawfully detained in breach of s. 9 of the Charter and that the various searches breached his s. 8 rights. On a voir dire, the trial judge held that the accused’s Charter rights had not been violated and the accused then pleaded guilty to drug and firearm offences. A majority of the Alberta Court of Appeal came to the same conclusion. A majority of the Supreme Court of Canada (Jamal J. writing) further dismissed the appeal.

[69]      Again, it is not necessary to review the majority reasons in detail. The relevant aspect of the decision is Jamal J.’s discussion of the law governing searches incident to investigative detention, where he cited Mann with approval:

[53]      This Court in Mann recognized that the police have a common law power to search incident to investigative detention under certain circumstances. Speaking for the majority, Iacobucci J. stated that “police officers may detain an individual for investigative purposes if there are reasonable grounds to suspect in all the circumstances that the individual is connected to a particular crime and that such a detention is necessary” (para. 45). He added that a police officer “may engage in a protective pat-down search of the detained individual” when the officer “has reasonable grounds to believe that his or her safety or that of others is at risk” (para. 45). In addition, both the investigative detention and the pat-down search “must be conducted in a reasonable manner” (para. 45; see also R. v. Clayton, 2007 SCC 32, [2007] 2 S.C.R. 725, at paras. 20 and 29-31).

[70]      Justice Jamal found that the accused was lawfully detained as part of a traffic collision investigation (at para. 59). The issue then became “whether the arresting officer had reasonable grounds to believe that his safety or the safety of others was at risk” (at para. 60, citing Mann at paras. 40, 45, as well as Thibodeau at para. 10). Justice Jamal found that the officer expressed “subjective concerns about safety, even if only implicitly, and that those concerns were objectively reasonable in the circumstances” (at para. 64). The search therefore did not breach the accused’s s. 8 Charter rights.

Court of Appeal for British Columbia

[71]      There are a number of decisions of this Court addressing the test for conducting a safety search incidental to investigative detention.

[72]      In R. v. Crocker, 2009 BCCA 388, leave to appeal to SCC ref’d, 33411 (28 January 2010), officers detained two occupants of a vehicle in connection with possible criminal activity. One of the officers asked the driver to roll down his window and to open his fist. According to the officer, he was concerned that the driver might be concealing a weapon in his hand. The driver was in fact concealing a bag of marijuana.

[73]      On a voir dire to consider various Charter breaches alleged by the accused, the trial judge found that the officer’s demand that the accused open his fist constituted a search and that the search was unreasonable. This Court disagreed. Justice D. Smith writing for the Court held that it was a valid protective search incident to investigative detention. In support of her conclusion, she relied on the test set out in Mann:

[63]        Mann recognized the need for police to ensure their short-term safety by non-intrusive means. As the Court noted at para. 43, “[t]he importance of ensuring officer safety has been recognized in obiter by this Court in R. v. Mellenthin, 1992 CanLII 50 (SCC), [1992] 3 S.C.R. 615. Police officers face any number of risks everyday in the carrying out of their policing function, and are entitled to go about their work secure in the knowledge that risks are minimized to the greatest extent possible.” Moreover, the potentially dangerous or even lethal risk to police officers in approaching a stationary, occupied vehicle, even when that risk is not readily observable, is well established: R. v. Miller and Cockriell (1975), 1975 CanLII 927 (BC CA), 24 C.C.C. (2d) 401 (B.C.C.A.); R. v. Romeo (1989), 1989 CanLII 7122 (NB CA), 47 C.C.C. (3d) 113 (N.B.C.A.).

…

[70]        The test in Mann requires an assessment of all of the known facts and circumstances of a situation, including any reasonable inferences that might be drawn from those known facts or circumstances, in determining if the Crown has established reasonable grounds, both subjectively and objectively, to support an officer’s decision to conduct a protective safety search. In my view, the trial judge failed to consider the totality of the circumstances, and the reasonable inferences that could be drawn from them, in determining if reasonable grounds existed for the search.

[71]        In summary, police have a duty to investigate crime. Courts should be reluctant to second guess an officer’s testimony as to his or her honest belief in the need for a protective safety search where he or she can articulate reasonably clear and focused reasons that inform their risk assessment. This is particularly so where the objective grounds for the search closely mirror those factors that informed the officer’s subjective belief.

[72]        In today’s world, weapons may extend to more than conventional guns or knives, and include other forms of weaponry of microscopic size. A lawful protective safety search to address these potential risks, particularly in a dynamic and potentially evolving street encounter with an occupant of a vehicle, need only meet the legal standard of reasonable suspicions. Here, the trial judge, in my view, applied a higher standard for establishing reasonable grounds for a protective safety search than that required by Mann. Had he correctly applied the legal standard for a search incidental to an investigative detention, I am unable to conclude that his ruling on this issue would necessarily have been the same.

[74]      Patrick involved a Crown appeal from the acquittal of the accused on several firearm‑related offences. The accused had been pulled over by a police officer while driving in Kamloops. In addition to the accused, there were three passengers in the vehicle, all of whom were known to the officer to be involved in criminal activities and capable of violence. After pulling the vehicle over, the officer asked the accused to get out. When he did, the officer noticed an oddly shaped bulge in the area of the accused’s right shoulder under his jacket. The officer asked the accused if he had anything on him and he replied that he had a shotgun. A loaded, sawed‑off shotgun was seized and the accused was arrested.

[75]      The accused was charged with eight firearm‑related offences, three of which related to the sawed‑off shotgun. At trial, the judge found that the roadside search of the accused, particularly the questions asked by the officer of the accused, constituted an unreasonable search in breach of s. 8 of the Charter and excluded all the evidence seized at the roadside, including the shotgun. This Court allowed the appeal on these three counts.

[76]      Justice Fitch for the Court framed the issue this way:

[10]      The question of law that lies at the heart of this Crown appeal reduces to this: Does a police officer have the power to ask a detainee questions preliminary to or in the course of conducting a permissible pat-down search incident to a lawful investigative detention? If so, what can properly be asked of a detainee at the investigative detention stage?

[77]      This issue engaged the central question of the scope of the power to search incidental to investigative detention. In addressing this issue, Fitch J.A. noted the split in the Supreme Court of Canada in MacDonald on whether the threshold standard was “reasonable grounds to suspect” or “reasonable grounds to believe”; however, he held it was unnecessary to deal with that point in order to resolve the appeal (at para. 59).

[78]      Justice Fitch cited Mann in support of his description of the underlying rationale of protective searches incident to investigative detention, that being the protection of police officers and others from harm that could have been avoided through a minimally invasive search. As Fitch J.A. observed, this rationale serves to circumscribe the scope of the search power (at para. 60). Justice Fitch also cited, at para. 61, one of the seminal passages from Mann where Iacobucci J. said:

[43]        … Police officers face any number of risks everyday in the carrying out of their policing function, and are entitled to go about their work secure in the knowledge that risks are minimized to the greatest extent possible. As noted by L’Heureux-Dubé J. in Cloutier, supra, at p. 185, a frisk search is a “relatively non-intrusive procedure”, the duration of which is “only a few seconds”. Where an officer has reasonable grounds to believe that his or her safety is at risk, the officer may engage in a protective pat-down search of the detained individual. The search must be grounded in objectively discernible facts to prevent “fishing expeditions” on the basis of irrelevant or discriminatory factors.

[79]      Justice Fitch held that protective searches conducted in accordance with the Mann test need not be confined to physical searches and that, depending on the circumstances, it may be necessary for an officer to make inquiries of a person before proceeding with a physical pat‑down search (at para. 98).

[80]      While Fitch J.A. did not consider it necessary to address head on the “reasonable suspicion” versus “reasonable grounds to believe” debate arising out of MacDonald, this Court subsequently did so in Webber. In that case, police officers pulled over the accused’s vehicle for traffic violations. Drugs were found in the centre console of the accused’s vehicle during a warrantless search conducted for safety purposes. At trial, the accused alleged that the search was unreasonable and breached his s. 8 rights. The trial judge rejected this argument.

[81]      On appeal, the accused argued that the trial judge erred by applying the “reasonable suspicion” standard rather than “reasonable belief”. In his submission, the Supreme Court of Canada in MacDonald had “recalibrated” the requisite legal standard from that established in Mann.

[82]      Justice Savage writing for the Court disagreed. He reviewed the relevant case law subsequent to Mann, including numerous cases in which the Mann “reasonable suspicion” test was endorsed (e.g., Chehil, Crocker, and R. v. Atkins, 2013 ONCA 586 at para. 15). This review led Savage J.A. to say:

[58]      … the Supreme Court of Canada, this Court and the Ontario Court of Appeal have all interpreted Mann as applying the reasonable suspicion standard as authorizing a safety search in certain contexts, where privacy interests are reduced.

[83]      Justice Savage cited with approval the decision of Justice Campbell in R. v. Le, 2014 ONSC 2033 at paras. 99–100, aff’d 2018 ONCA 56, rev’d on other grounds 2019 SCC 34. In Le, Campbell J. reviewed the historical and jurisprudential underpinnings of the “reasonable suspicion” standard and said:

[100]   In R. v. Mann and its progeny the courts have confirmed the existence of the police power to detain individuals for investigative purposes and, where the police have “reasonable grounds” to suspect the detainee is armed and dangerous, to conduct a brief frisk or pat-down search to ensure their own safety and the safety of the public as they conduct such investigations. In my view, R. v. MacDonald is but an application of that well-established warrantless search power in a particular factual context, namely, where the search involves police entry of the confines of a private residence, where there is an increased expectation of privacy. See: R. v. Zargar, 2014 ONSC 1415, at paras. 29-32. Indeed, in R. v. MacDonald, the Supreme Court expressly purports to apply R. v. Mann in this factual context – not overrule it (or the many subsequent judgments that have clarified and applied it). The confusion in relation to this legal threshold has arisen, it seems to me, from the use of the phrase “reasonable grounds” to describe the threshold of “reasonable suspicion” or “articulable cause,” as this same terminology is also used to describe the higher threshold of “reasonable and probable grounds.” See: R. v. Mann, at paras. 33-35, 40-45, 63-64. Accordingly, it is important to recall that, in this particular context, the term “reasonable grounds” is used to describe a threshold of reasonable suspicion, not a threshold of reasonable probability.

[84]      Justice Savage concluded his analysis on this point by stating:

[65]      … the Supreme Court of Canada did not recalibrate the test for lawful police safety searches from the traditional “reasonable suspicion” standard.

Supreme Court of British Columbia

[85]      The various decisions of this Court referred to above focus on the required state of mind of a police officer conducting a safety search; again, the reasonable suspicion versus reasonable grounds to believe standard. These decisions do not deal expressly with the nature of the perceived safety threat, in particular whether it need be imminent in order to justify a safety search.

[86]      Mr. Dhillon submits that there are, however, numerous decisions in the Supreme Court of British Columbia that have endorsed the imminent threat standard. He cites, by way of example: R. v. Speck, 2022 BCSC 486 at paras. 79, 81; R. v. Hendrickson, 2018 BCSC 288 at para. 32, R. v. Howell, 2020 BCSC 1731 at paras. 39, 42; R. v. French, 2018 BCSC 825 at paras. 123, 136; and R. v. Hynes, 2019 BCSC 890 at para. 98, among others.

[87]      It is not necessary to review these decisions in any detail other than to note that they uniformly cite LeBel J.’s majority reasons in MacDonald that contain the “imminent threat” reference (see paras. 51–53 above).

[88]      It is, however, useful to consider one other Supreme Court decision, R. v. A.R., 2021 BCSC 1539, cited by Mr. Dhillon, in which Justice Crossin went beyond simply citing the language of MacDonald and engaged in a detailed analysis of whether the law requires an imminent threat in order to justify a safety search. That case involved a summary conviction appeal from a decision of the Provincial Court of British Columbia convicting the accused of one count of possession of a prohibited weapon. The first ground of appeal advanced before Crossin J. was that the judge had erred in finding that a pat‑down safety search of the accused did not violate s. 8 of the Charter, despite the absence of evidence establishing an imminent threat to safety.

[89]      Justice Crossin began his analysis with Mann and the framework established by Iacobucci J. for determining when a warrantless search incident to detention is authorized (see paras. 43–44 above). Justice Crossin then addressed the majority reasons of LeBel J. in MacDonald, noting that it is here that the language of “imminent threat” appears (at paras. 66, 68). Before Crossin J., the Crown contended that the use of the phrase “imminent threat” in MacDonald should be limited to the facts of that case, which did not involve a search incident to investigative detention.

[90]      Justice Crossin considered a number of decisions of this Court, including Webber and Patrick, as well as R. v. Aikman, 2019 BCCA 312, in which Justice Stromberg‑Stein distinguished MacDonald and Mann on the basis that in MacDonald, the safety search was not conducted incident to investigative detention but was in the nature of a stand‑alone search intended to eliminate risks to the officers and the public (at para. 69).

[91]      Based upon his review of the case law, Crossin J. reached the following conclusion:

[93]      While it might reasonably be argued the Supreme Court of Canada has not spoken definitively on this point, the task of this Court is to apply the law, as I understand the law to be, to the facts of this case, in relation to this offender. I am satisfied that the law as it stands in B.C. supports the requirement that there be an “imminent threat” concerning searches incident to investigative detention. In my view importing such a requirement accords with the principles in Mann and MacDonald that police safety searches be constrained by “objectively verifiable necessity”: MacDonald at para. 31, citing Mann at para. 26. The requirement of necessity follows as a matter of logic from an imminent threat.

[92]      Justice Crossin then considered what the requirement for an imminent threat would actually mean for police officers conducting safety searches. He agreed with the Crown’s submission that it should not demand a requirement of immediacy, such that a safety search would only be justified in cases of repelling an immediate attack. Justice Crossin said:

[95]      … It makes little sense to demand a requirement that the threat be immediate. This would make a lawful safety search authorized only in situations necessary to repel a threat that was thought to be occurring instantaneously. The “imminent threat” in MacDonald was the observance of a black and shiny item behind Mr. MacDonald’s case. There was no indication that an attack would immediately follow the officer’s observation of that item.

[96]        In my view, the jurisprudence simply demands the imminent threat or harm must be more than a vague and general concern for safety. The police must act, as was stated in Mann “on reasonable and specific inferences drawn from the known facts of the situation”. This also recognizes that policing is not a static exercise and circumstances in any given situation are dynamic.

Discussion

[93]      It is well established that the standard of review applicable to a trial judge’s determination of the proper legal test for assessing an alleged Charter breach is correctness: R. v. Clarke, 2017 BCCA 453 at para. 24, leave to appeal to SCC ref’d, 37977 (31 January 2019).

[94]      For the reasons that follow, I am of the view that the judge did not err by applying the wrong legal test for justifying a safety search incidental to investigative detention. Specifically, it is my view that the law does not require an imminent threat in order for such a search to be lawful.

[95]      This Court in Webber held that the Supreme Court of Canada in MacDonald did not modify or “recalibrate” the test for conducting a safety search incident to investigative detention from the traditional threshold standard of “reasonable suspicion”. While Webber is silent on the issue of whether an imminent threat is required, I am similarly of the view that MacDonald cannot be read as modifying Mann by incorporating an imminent threat requirement.

[96]      I arrive at this conclusion for a number of reasons. First, as I noted above at para. 56, both the majority and minority reasons in MacDonald focused on the threshold requirement of reasonable suspicion versus reasonable grounds to believe. There is no discussion in either set of reasons of an intention to depart from, modify, or “recalibrate” the test from Mann, particularly as it relates to the nature of the perceived threat. Indeed, both the majority and minority in MacDonald purport to apply the Mann test.

[97]      Further, the particular facts of MacDonald must be kept in mind. At the time the police officer forced entry into the accused’s home, the accused had not been arrested or detained. Rather, the officer believed that he had seen a weapon in the accused’s possession and he acted to address a threat to safety that, in the circumstances, was clearly imminent.

[98]      Justice Jamal for the majority in Stairs distinguished MacDonald on this basis, noting that the accused in MacDonald retained a strong expectation of privacy in his home (at para. 76). I also agree with Campbell J.’s comments in Le (see para. 83 above) that MacDonald is properly viewed as an application of the Mann test in the particular context of a warrantless search of a private residence (Le at para. 100).

[99]      Justice Jamal in Stairs also specifically rejected an imminent threat requirement for safety searches incident to arrest on the basis that police already have the power to act to address exigent circumstances, and therefore, adding an imminence requirement to the power to search incident to arrest would effectively render that power redundant. He also noted that “setting the bar too high”, by requiring an imminent threat, would prevent police officers from taking immediate steps to address risks to their safety and the safety of the public (at para. 77). The same concerns apply to the power to conduct a safety search incident to investigative detention.

[100]   In my view, the incorporation of an “imminent threat” requirement to the test for justifying a protective safety search incident to investigative detention adds little to the test and, in fact, is more likely to create confusion. Justice Crossin noted in A.R. that “imminent” cannot be interpreted to mean “immediate”. Yet, use of the term imminent implies a degree of immediacy. For example, “imminent” is defined in the Oxford Canadian Dictionary to mean “impending; about to happen”.

[101]   Ultimately, the test for justifying a safety search incident to investigative detention must balance the privacy interests of the detained individual with the interests of the police officers in maintaining their safety and the safety of the public. That balance is maintained by permitting an officer to engage in a protective or safety pat‑down search of a detained individual when the officer has reasonable grounds to suspect that there is a risk to their safety, or the safety of others, which would be addressed by an immediate search. The reasonable suspicion must be based on “objectively discernible facts”, rather than on a hunch or vague concern for safety, and must be conducted in a reasonable and minimally intrusive manner: see MacDonald at paras. 72, 76.

[102]   Applying this test here, I am satisfied that the judge did not err in finding that Cst. Waller had a reasonable suspicion that Mr. Dhillon may have posed a threat to her or to the public and therefore was justified in performing the pat‑down search. I agree with Mr. Dhillon that, beyond listing the factors identified by Cst. Waller that gave rise to her suspicion (see para. 34 above), the judge did not set out a detailed analysis of whether that suspicion was objectively reasonable. However, it is apparent from the judge’s reasons that he considered the totality of the circumstances and was satisfied that Cst. Waller’s suspicion was in fact reasonable. Again, I am not satisfied that the judge erred in reaching this conclusion. In this regard, I note D. Smith J.A.’s admonition in Crocker that “[c]ourts should be reluctant to second guess an officer’s testimony as to his or her honest belief in the need for a protective safety search where he or she can articulate reasonably clear and focused reasons that inform their risk assessment” (at para. 73 above, citing Crocker at para. 71).

[103]   I therefore find that the judge did not err in finding that Mr. Dhillon’s rights under s. 8 of the Charter were not breached.

Section 10(b): Access to Counsel

[104]   Mr. Dhillon submits that the judge erred in finding that the failure of police to provide access to counsel at the roadside when he was arrested did not violate his s. 10(b) right to counsel.

[105]   In support of his position, Mr. Dhillon relies on R. v. Flintroy, 2019 BCSC 78, particularly Justice Williams’ observation below:

[46]      In the current environment in which we live, cell phones are ubiquitous. While the decided authorities appear to accept that there is no obligation upon police officers to provide their own personal cell phones to detainees, it is surprising indeed that arrangements could not have been made in this case for the officers who were to deal with and transport the applicants to jail to have available a cellphone which would have permitted them to make their telephone calls to counsel at roadside. I do not know of any practical considerations that would have made that impossible or unreasonable.

[106]   In Flintroy, two accused were arrested as part of a large‑scale drug trafficking investigation that involved the execution of numerous search warrants. Both accused were arrested at their homes when the police attended pursuant to a warrant and both were transported to the Vancouver jail. Neither was given the opportunity to speak to counsel until some time after they arrived and were processed at the jail.

[107]   Justice Williams noted that it was part of the police’s operational plan to afford the accused access to counsel sometime subsequent to their arrival at the jail (at para. 37). He found that this plan failed to “recognize the importance and urgency of the constitutional obligation on police to provide that access without delay” (at para. 45). This led him to make the observations set out above. Justice Williams concluded that the accused’s s. 10(b) rights had been breached.

[108]   Flintroy is distinguishable on its facts. As noted, the accused individuals in that case were arrested as part of a large‑scale investigation and the delay in providing access to counsel was a deliberate decision made as part of the operational plan. In contrast, in the case at bar, Mr. Dhillon was arrested as a result of circumstances that arose and evolved quickly following an impromptu traffic stop. I note that in R. v. McPake, 2019 BCSC 751, Justice DeWitt‑Van Oosten (as she then was) distinguished Flintroy on similar grounds.

[109]   In Patrick, this Court confirmed that the requirement that an individual be given access to counsel “without delay” does not mean that the duty to facilitate access is near‑immediate or near‑absolute (at para. 111; see also R. v. Ashby, 2013 BCCA 334 at para. 72). Rather, the practicalities of the situation, including issues of officer and public safety as well as any other exigent circumstances, must be taken into account (Patrick at paras. 112–113).

[110]   Cst. Waller explained in her testimony why it was not practical or safe to provide Mr. Dhillon with cell phone access to counsel roadside and why the decision was made to first transport him to the jail. These concerns have been recognized by the courts as valid: Ashby at paras. 71–73; R. v. Taylor, 2014 SCC 50 at para. 27, and R. v. Montgomery, 2009 BCCA 41 at paras. 34–36.

[111]   The judge considered Cst. Waller’s explanation as well as the surrounding circumstances and concluded that the actions of the police were reasonable. Mr. Dhillon has not demonstrated that the judge erred in coming to this conclusion. I therefore find that the judge did not err in finding that Mr. Dhillon’s. 10(b) Charter rights were not breached.

Conclusion

[112]   Accordingly, I would dismiss the appeal.

Summary:

Sentence appeal by the Crown from a declaration that s. 244.2(3)(a)(i), which establishes a five-year mandatory minimum sentence for an offence under s. 244.2(1)(b), is unconstitutional as it infringes s. 12 of the Charter. The respondent was convicted of intentionally discharging a firearm while being reckless as to the life or safety of another person as a result of her involvement as an aider or abettor in a drive-by shooting. In concluding that the mandatory minimum sentence was unconstitutional, the judge found that he was not bound by the decision of this Court in R. v. Oud, 2016 BCCA 332. Further, he concluded that the decision in R. v. Cardinal, 2018 NWTSC 12, which posited a new hypothetical for the generalized inquiry under s. 12, represented a significant change in the reasonably foreseeable application of the law subsequent to the decision in Oud. Held: Appeal allowed. The declaration of unconstitutionality is set aside. The four-and-a-half-year sentence imposed for the offence is set aside and replaced with a five-year sentence. The judge erred in failing to treat this Court’s decision in Oud as binding on him in its application to the mandatory minimum sentence for the s. 244.2(1)(b) offence. Further, the judge erred in concluding that the Cardinal hypothetical met the threshold for reconsideration of a previous higher court decision upholding the constitutionality of a mandatory minimum sentence. Applying the test in Canada (Attorney General) v. Bedford, 2013 SCC 72 and R. v. Nur, 2015 SCC 15, the Cardinal hypothetical does not represent a significant development in the law sufficient to fundamentally shift the parameters of the legal debate regarding the constitutionality of the mandatory minimum sentence established in s. 244.2(3)(a)(i).

Reasons for Judgment of the Honourable Mr. Justice Butler:

Background

[1]         This appeal arises from a challenge to the mandatory minimum sentence in s. 244.2(3) of the Criminal Code, R.S.C. 1985, c. C-46. It raises a difficult question of stare decisis: when should a court reconsider the constitutionality of a mandatory minimum sentence that has been upheld by this Court?

[2]         The respondent, Ms. Dingwall, was convicted of eight offences relating to a drive-by shooting that occurred in 2016 at approximately 4:00 a.m. A truck stopped in a residential neighbourhood in Mackenzie, British Columbia, and one of the occupants of the truck fired four bullets towards a house from a semi-automatic handgun. At the time of the shooting, three people were outside the house on the front lawn, one of whom was shot in the leg. Ms. Dingwall was not present at the shooting but was convicted as an aider or abettor.

[3]         Ms. Dingwall was convicted of the intentional discharge of a firearm while being reckless as to the life and safety of another person, contrary to s. 244.2(1)(b) of the Criminal Code. The mandatory minimum sentence for the s. 244.2(1)(b) offence is the subject of this appeal. She was also convicted of the intentional discharge of a firearm with the intent to wound, maim or disfigure another person, contrary to s. 244(1).

[4]         At sentencing, the judge held that the decision of this Court in R. v. Oud, 2016 BCCA 332, which upheld the constitutionality of the mandatory minimum sentence in s. 244.2(3)(b), was not binding on him. I will refer to this provision as the former s. 244.2(3)(b), as the mandatory minimum sentence in s. 244.2(3)(b) has since been repealed: An Act to amend the Criminal Code and the Controlled Drugs and Substances Act, S.C. 2022, c. 15. The judge found that the mandatory minimum sentence for the s. 244(1) offence did not infringe s. 12 of the Charter. However, he concluded that the mandatory minimum sentence in s. 244.2(3)(a)(i) for the s. 244.2(1)(b) offence infringed s. 12 when applied to the reasonable hypothetical scenario described in R. v. Cardinal, 2018 NWTSC 12.

[5]         The Crown appeals the judge’s declaration that s. 244.2(3)(a)(i) constitutes cruel and unusual punishment and offends s. 12 of the Charter. The Crown argues that the judge erred in finding that Oud was not binding on him and in concluding that the Cardinal hypothetical represented “a significant change in the reasonably foreseeable applications of the law”.

[6]         For the reasons that follow, I am of the view that the judge erred in concluding that Oud was not binding on him. Although it was open to the judge to consider the Cardinal hypothetical, I am also of the view that he erred in concluding that it represented a change in the foreseeable applications of the law or that it gives rise to a s. 12 Charter violation.

Sentencing Reasons

[7]         The sentencing judge reviewed the framework for challenges to mandatory minimum sentences under s. 12 of the Charter, noting that a sentence offends s. 12 if it is grossly disproportionate for the individual before the court, or if its reasonably foreseeable application in hypothetical circumstances would impose grossly disproportionate sentences on others: at para. 22.

[8]         Beginning with the “particularized inquiry”, the judge first reviewed the aggravating and mitigating factors. He acknowledged Ms. Dingwall’s role as an aider or abettor, and observed that this may be a relevant sentencing consideration. However, based on his review of authorities, he was of the view that the extent to which it may be a mitigating factor is highly contextual and, at most, a minor element of the analysis. Further, in this case, he did not consider it to be a mitigating factor: at paras. 50, 57. Similarly, he acknowledged that willful blindness could be a mitigating factor in some cases, but was not in these circumstances: at paras. 59, 66.

[9]         The judge then reviewed Gladue factors. He found that in Ms. Dingwall’s case, they played a minor role in determining a fit sentence for the firearms offences (ss. 244(1) and 244.2(1)(b)) that formed the basis of her constitutional challenges to the mandatory minimum sentences.

[10]      The judge ultimately concluded the particularized inquiry by finding that the upper range of a fit sentence for Ms. Dingwall, considering her personal circumstances and the gravity of the offences, would be a term of imprisonment of five years. He then determined that a fit sentence would be four years and six months, to be served concurrently, for the convictions under ss. 244(1) and 244.2(1)(b). As a result, the judge reasoned that the mandatory minimum did not constitute a grossly disproportionate sentence for either of the two firearms offences as applied specifically to Ms. Dingwall.

[11]      Turning to the generalized inquiry, the judge examined two hypotheticals advanced by counsel. The first, referred to as the “Dingwall Hypothetical”, was modeled closely on Ms. Dingwall’s circumstances. The second, set out at para. 81, is described as the “Cardinal Hypothetical” and based on that decision:

A young Aboriginal person, suffering from lived and inter-generational trauma, is intoxicated and distressed and attempts to take his own life. He takes a [restricted or prohibited firearm] from his stepfather’s gun closet. The individual puts the gun to his own chin and fires, but in the moment of pulling the trigger the gun is pushed away by a friend or family member in the room. No one is harmed by the bullet, which exits through an exterior wall in the home.

[12]      The judge began his consideration of the hypotheticals by noting the Crown’s submissions that he was bound by this Court’s decision in Oud. He found that Oud, while persuasive, was not binding on him because of “noteworthy differences” between the two cases: at para. 85. First, he distinguished the relevant sentencing provisions, stating that while Oud considered the constitutionality of the mandatory minimum prescribed by the former s. 244.2(3)(b) (where the offence is committed with a non-restricted firearm), this case concerns the mandatory minimum prescribed by s. 244.2(3)(a) (where the offence is committed with a restricted or prohibited firearm), a more serious offence: at para. 86. Second, he distinguished the underlying offence in Oud based on its mens rea requirement: at para. 87. Oud dealt with s. 244.2(1)(a), which applies to an offender “who intentionally discharges a firearm into or at a place, knowing that or being reckless as to whether another person is present in the place”. This case concerns s. 244.2(1)(b), which applies to an offender “who intentionally discharges a firearm while being reckless as to the life or safety of another person.” The judge nonetheless recognized the reasoning in Oud as persuasive, engaging similar considerations regarding Parliament’s intention and the gravity of the offences as the present case: at para. 88.

[13]      The judge held that, in any case, the recent Cardinal decision, in which Justice Charbonneau found the mandatory minimum sentence prescribed by s. 244.2(3)(a)(i) to be unconstitutional in the context of a charge under s. 244.2(1)(b), meant that there had been a “significant change in the reasonably foreseeable applications of the law” as discussed in R. v. Nur, 2015 SCC 15 at para. 71. This, combined with the fact that Oud considered different Criminal Code provisions, justified a consideration of the constitutionality of the mandatory minimum prescribed by s. 244.2(3)(a)(i).

[14]      The judge turned to consider s. 244(2)(a)(i), the mandatory minimum sentence applicable to the s. 244(1) offence of discharging a firearm with intent to wound. He concluded that the Dingwall hypothetical did not raise particular circumstances that would render the five-year mandatory minimum in s. 244(2)(a)(i) grossly disproportionate, “particularly in light of the seriousness of the offence”: at para. 98. He did not consider the Cardinal hypothetical as it was not reasonable in relation to the s. 244(1) offence.

[15]      The judge then considered the s. 244.2(3)(a)(i) mandatory minimum in relation to the Cardinal hypothetical. He noted the double mens rea requirement of the corresponding s. 244.2(1)(b) offence and that the gravity of the offence is very high: at paras. 110–111. He found, quoting from Cardinal, that the hypothetical was reasonably foreseeable and that the circumstances described satisfied the essential elements of the underlying offence: at paras. 116–117.

[16]      The judge then considered the relevant factors outlined in R. v. Morrisey, 2000 SCC 39, to determine whether the mandatory minimum would result in a grossly disproportionate sentence. He found that a five-year sentence was grossly disproportionate given the hypothetical Aboriginal offender’s young age and lack of a criminal record. He considered the penological goals and sentencing principles underlying the mandatory minimum, and found that the hypothetical circumstances of the offender militated against a sentence that emphasized denunciation and deterrence so strongly.

[17]      The trial judge concluded that a fit sentence for the offender in the Cardinal hypothetical would be significantly lower than the five-year mandatory minimum sentence under s. 244.2(3)(a)(i) such that the mandatory minimum was grossly disproportionate and would offend s. 12 of the Charter as applied to offences under s. 244.2(1)(b).

On Appeal

Positions of the parties

[18]      The appellant argues that the judge erred in two ways in concluding he was not bound by this Court’s decision in Oud. First, he failed to appreciate the scope of this Court’s ruling in Oud. Second, he erred in concluding that the hypothetical posed in Cardinal amounted to a “significant change in the reasonably foreseeable applications of the law” (Nur at para. 71) such that he could reconsider the decision in Oud.

[19]      With regard to the first alleged error, the appellant argues that the decision in Oud was based on the nature, scope and breadth of conduct captured by the s. 244.2 offence without reference to differing mens rea requirements between subsections (1)(a) and (1)(b). The appellant submits that subsections 1(a) and 1(b) simply describe two different ways that the single offence of intentional and reckless discharge of firearms under s. 244.2 can be established. The Crown submits that the gravity of the offence is identical whether the Crown proves recklessness as to the life or safety of persons, or recklessness is presumed through proof of knowledge or recklessness as to the presence of people in the place into which the firearm was discharged.

[20]      Further, the appellant argues that the judge erred in distinguishing the sentencing provision in Oud on the basis that it dealt with the four-year mandatory minimum under the former s. 244.2(3)(b), rather than s. 244.2(3)(a)(i). The appellant says that a five-year minimum sentence for the commission of any s. 244.2 offence committed with a restricted or prohibited firearm cannot be grossly disproportionate when the four-year minimum sentence involving the same criminality committed with a non-restricted firearm was found compliant with s. 12 of the Charter in Oud.

[21]      With regard to the second alleged error, the Crown submits that it was only open to the sentencing judge to revisit Oud if there was “a significant change in the reasonably foreseeable applications of the law”, citing Nur at para. 71. The Crown argues that the Cardinal hypothetical does not amount to a change in circumstances or evidence that could shift the parameters of the debate about the constitutionality of the mandatory minimum.

[22]      The Crown says the Cardinal hypothetical is not reasonable because, if taken as stated, the offender’s sole objective was a self-inflicted gunshot to his head, which does not contravene s. 244.2(1)(b). There is nothing in the hypothetical to suggest that the offender was aware of, or recklessness about, the risk to the lives or safety of others. However, if it is inferred that he intended to discharge the firearm with sufficient knowledge of circumstances making the discharge reckless as to the life or safety of others, the mandatory minimum would not be grossly disproportionate.

[23]      The respondent argues that the judge correctly concluded that he was not bound to follow Oud because of the distinguishing features of this case. Namely, the offender in Oud was the principal offender while Ms. Dingwall was a party to the offence; s. 244.2 creates two related, but distinct, offences and the inquiry in Oud considered only s. 244.2(1)(a); Oud dealt with the mandatory minimum sentence prescribed by the former s. 244.2(3)(b), not s. 244.2(3)(a)(i); and Oud did not consider Gladue principles.

[24]      Alternatively, the respondent submits that even if Oud was binding on the judge, it was open to him to revisit the outcome. Citing Carter v. Canada (Attorney General), 2015 SCC 5 at para. 44, she argues that her case raised new legal issues and presented a change in circumstances or evidence that fundamentally shifted the parameters of the debate. She argues that the Cardinal hypothetical presents new circumstances that might reasonably arise and demonstrates that the mandatory minimum is grossly disproportionate in its application to some offenders convicted of the s. 233.2(1)(b) offence.

[25]      In her factum, the respondent also argued that the judge erred in concluding that the mandatory minimum was not grossly disproportionate to the sentencing range applicable to her. She argued that the mandatory minimum therefore contravenes s. 12 of the Charter. She withdrew that argument at the hearing of the appeal and I need not consider it.

Analysis

Legislation

[26]      As of the date of the trial judgment, the relevant provisions of s. 244.2 of the Criminal Code were:

244.2 (1) Every person commits an offence

(a) who intentionally discharges a firearm into or at a place, knowing that or being reckless as to whether another person is present in the place; or

(b) who intentionally discharges a firearm while being reckless as to the life or safety of another person.

…

(3) Every person who commits an offence under subsection (1) is guilty of an indictable offence and

(a) if a restricted firearm or prohibited firearm is used in the commission of the offence or if the offence is committed for the benefit of, at the direction of or in association with a criminal organization, is liable to imprisonment for a term of not more than 14 years and to a minimum punishment of imprisonment for a term of

(i) five years, in the case of a first offence, and

(ii) seven years, in the case of a second or subsequent offence; and

(b) in any other case, is liable to imprisonment for a term of not more than 14 years and to a minimum punishment of imprisonment for a term of four years.

[27]      As a consequence of the amendment to the Criminal Code, which came into force on November 17, 2022, s. 244.2(3)(b) has been replaced by the following:

(b) in any other case, is liable to imprisonment for a term of not more than 14 years.

[28]      Section 12 of the Charter provides:

12 Everyone has the right not to be subjected to any cruel and unusual treatment or punishment.

Law

[29]      The analytical framework to be applied when considering a s. 12 challenge to the constitutionality of a mandatory minimum sentence is not controversial. As Justice Cory noted in Steele v. Mountain Institution, 1990 CanLII 50 (SCC), [1990] 2 S.C.R. 1385 at 1417:

It will only be on rare and unique occasions that a court will find a sentence so grossly disproportionate that it violates the provisions of s. 12 of the Charter. The test for determining whether a sentence is disproportionately long is very properly stringent and demanding. A lesser test would tend to trivialize the Charter.

[30]      A court should be reluctant to interfere with the considered views of Parliament and will do so only in the clearest of cases: R. v. Smith, 1987 CanLII 64 (SCC), [1987] 1 S.C.R. 1045 at 1070. The sentence must be “so excessive as to outrage standards of decency” and be disproportionate to the extent that Canadians “would find the punishment abhorrent or intolerable”: R. v. Ferguson, 2008 SCC 6 at para. 14; Nur at para. 39.

[31]      The general rule regarding the doctrine of stare decisis is easily stated as requiring that judges follow the rulings of higher courts but, in practice, it can be difficult to determine what exactly is binding on lower courts. The traditional view was that the ratio decidendi is the only aspect of a decision that binds lower courts. However, the doctrine of stare decisis has expanded to include broader legal analysis and sometimes even obiter dicta: R. v. Henry, 2005 SCC 76 at paras. 52–57. Ultimately, the question to be answered is “what did the case decide?” Beyond the ratio decidendi, the legal question decided may be narrow or broad, depending on the case. The weight given to the obiter “decreases as one moves from the dispositive ratio decidendi to a wider circle of analysis which is obviously intended for guidance and which should be accepted as authoritative”: Henry at para. 57.

[32]      Lower courts are required to follow the precedent set by higher courts. However, certain circumstances may arise that permit them to revisit a matter already decided by a higher court. Chief Justice McLachlin, writing for the Court in Canada (Attorney General) v. Bedford, 2013 SCC 72, discussed these circumstances:

[42]      In my view, a trial judge can consider and decide arguments based on Charter provisions that were not raised in the earlier case; this constitutes a new legal issue. Similarly, the matter may be revisited if new legal issues are raised as a consequence of significant developments in the law, or if there is a change in the circumstances or evidence that fundamentally shifts the parameters of the debate.

[33]      The principle of stare decisis applies to the analysis of whether a mandatory minimum sentence violates s. 12 of the Charter, particularly with respect to reasonable hypotheticals. This point was addressed in Nur. First, the Court held that where a court has previously ruled that a mandatory minimum does not violate s. 12, a particular future offender can still argue that it violates s. 12 because it imposes cruel and unusual punishment on them specifically, if they can establish new circumstances or evidence. Second, the Court also held at para. 71 that:

… A court’s conclusion based on its review of the provision’s reasonably foreseeable applications does not foreclose consideration in future of different reasonable applications: Morrisey, at para. 89, per Arbour J. That said, the threshold for revisiting the constitutionality of a mandatory minimum is high and requires a significant change in the reasonably foreseeable applications of the law.

[34]      In Nur, the Court also discussed the extent to which the personal characteristics of a hypothetical offender could be considered when analyzing a reasonably foreseeable application of a mandatory minimum. The Court rejected the suggestion that personal characteristics of a hypothetical offender should be entirely excluded, but cautioned that the exercise has to be grounded in common sense and experience. It is not appropriate to use personal features in a hypothetical to construct the most innocent and sympathetic case: Nur at paras. 73–76.

[35]      Finally, I note that a question was raised at the hearing of this appeal as to whether the standard for revisiting a previous decision as set out in Bedford is the same as that set out in Nur in the context of s. 12 challenges. In R. v. Al-Isawi, 2017 BCCA 163, Justice Stromberg-Stein considered them together in determining whether the reconsideration of a past s. 12 decision was merited in the case before her. I agree with that approach. It is my view that the Court in Nur applied the Bedford test to the particular circumstances that arise when a court determines whether it is bound by stare decisis in relation to a mandatory minimum sentence that was previously found compliant with s. 12 of the Charter.

Issues

[36]      This appeal raises two issues:

1)   Did the sentencing judge err in failing to treat this Court’s decision in Oud as binding on him in its application to the mandatory minimum sentence for the s. 244.2(1)(b) offence; and

2)   Did the sentencing judge err in concluding that the Cardinal hypothetical amounted to a significant change in the reasonably foreseeable application of the law and in finding that the mandatory minimum sentence gives rise to a s. 12 violation.

Is Oud binding regarding the s. 244.2(3)(a)(i) mandatory minimum for the s. 244.2(1)(b) offence?

[37]      The judge found that he was not bound to follow the decision in Oud. He held that the present case concerns the s. 244.2(1)(b) offence and the s. 244.2(3)(a)(i) mandatory minimum sentence, neither of which were directly in issue in Oud. It is my view that these distinctions are not material to the legal conclusion reached in Oud. The Court intended to provide guidance on the constitutionality of the mandatory minimum sentence for the offence of the reckless intentional discharge of a firearm under s. 244.2, which encompasses both subsections 244.2(1)(a) and (b). Further, as per Oud, the former four-year mandatory minimum sentence for that offence, if committed with a non-restricted or prohibited firearm, was not grossly disproportionate. Consequently, a five-year mandatory minimum sentence for such an offence committed with a restricted or prohibited firearm, as in this case, cannot offend s. 12 of the Charter.

Are the underlying offences in Oud and this case distinguishable?

[38]      The appellant argues, and I agree, that the Court’s decision in Oud was based on the nature, scope and breadth of the conduct captured by both ss. 244.2(1)(a) and (b). I note that the mandatory minimum sentence established by s. 244.2(3)(a) does not distinguish between the two ways in which the reckless discharge of firearms is criminalized. The same minimum applies whether the Crown has proved that the offence was committed by the intentional discharge of a firearm into a place, knowing or being reckless as to whether another person was in that place, or by the intentional discharge of a firearm while being reckless as to the life or safety of another person.

[39]      The Crown submits that it is difficult to discern any difference in the degree of moral responsibility between the two modes of commission of the offence (Appellant Factum at para. 36):

Both involve an intentional discharge of a firearm a) knowing or being reckless as to whether the place into which the firearm is discharged is occupied, or b) being reckless as to the life or safety of another person. The gravity of the (b) offence involves recklessness as to the actual life or safety of persons whereas the (a) offence involves knowledge or recklessness as to presence of another person in the place at which the firearm is discharged. In this latter situation, the risk to life or safety is simply presumed by knowledge or recklessness as to the presence of people in the place. It represents one specific, yet common, way in which the offence described s. 244.2(1)(b) is committed. Consequently, the gravity of the crimes described in ss. 244.2(1)(a) and (b) is identical.

[40]      I would agree with this submission. The respondent has not offered any explanation based in logic or principle for treating the two subsections differently for sentencing purposes, let alone in applying a mandatory minimum sentence. There is no difference in the degree of moral responsibility between the modes of commission of the offence and no difference in the gravity of the offence as between subsections 244.2(1)(a) and (b).

[41]      The judge did not provide an explanation as to why the two subsections should be treated differently for mandatory minimum sentencing purposes, other than his reference to the new hypothetical from Cardinal. As I discuss below, it was open to the judge to consider whether the Cardinal hypothetical amounted to new evidence or circumstances that reflected a “significant change in the reasonably foreseeable applications of the law” applying the principles set out in Bedford and Nur. However, that consideration is distinct from and does not assist in answering the prior question of whether the decision in Oud was, subject to such new evidence or circumstances, binding on the sentencing judge.

[42]      The Court’s analysis of legislative intent in Oud supports the view that the decision was intended to apply to the s. 244.2(1) offence, not only to s. 244.2(1)(a). The Court referred to the comments made by the Minister of Justice when the legislation creating s. 244.2 was introduced in the House of Commons. The Minister noted that the proposed offence was intended to fill a gap in the Criminal Code and would “target drive-by and other intentional shootings involving reckless disregard for the life and safety of others”: Oud at para. 35 (emphasis in original). The Minister described the s. 244.2 offence as requiring “proof that the accused specifically turned his or her mind to the fact that discharging his or her firearm would jeopardize the life and safety of another person, and appreciating this fact, that the accused still went ahead.” He stated that the offence was intended to provide “a powerful new tool to target not only drive-by shootings but any shooting which involves consciously reckless behavior”: Oud at para. 34 (emphasis in original).

[43]      Justice Saunders emphasized that the offence targets “all intentional discharges of firearms in situations highly dangerous to others, with knowledge or recklessness”: Oud at para. 35. She observed that “there is no small offence encompassed by the double mens rea of s. 244.2”, given that the “central premise of the offence … is the intentional discharge of a firearm, knowing or being reckless whether others are in the place targeted”, which is “highly dangerous behavior that will attract a stern response”: Oud at para. 38. The legal conclusion reached in Oud is cast at a level of generality that, in my view, means it cannot be limited in its application to s. 244.2(1)(a): 

[49]      In all, I do not consider the hypothetical circumstances newly advanced before us to stretch the range of potential offences realistically captured by s. 244.2 so broadly as to render the mandatory minimum sentence cruel and unusual punishment.

[50]      The question really is whether we can conceive of an offence under s. 244.2 in which a four-year sentence would be grossly disproportionate. This is not to ask whether a four-year sentence would never be considered unfit or demonstrably unfit for purposes of a sentence appeal, but whether a four-year sentence would be so unfit as to rise to the level of gross disproportionality. Some guidance in this may be taken from Nur itself. In Nur, a 40-month sentence for a 19-year-old first offender who merely possessed a loaded firearm in a public place for a short period of time and did not discharge it or use it in a threatening manner, was not demonstrably unfit. Remembering the modern approach to gun offences, I cannot conceive of an offence under s. 244.2 for which the mandatory minimum penalty would be said to be cruel and unusual punishment.

[51]      I conclude, for these reasons, that s. 244.2(3)(b) is a mandatory minimum sentence that is within Parliament’s competence. The offence is narrowly cast and the gravity of the offence puts this mandatory minimum sentence within the range of proportionate sentences for every reasonable foreseeable hypothetical. In my view, it is not grossly disproportionate; it is not cruel and unusual punishment.

[Emphasis added.]

[44]      Although the concluding paragraph refers only to the former s. 244.2(3)(b), that does not take away from the generality of the principle. This is because the former s. 244.2(3)(b), like s. 244.2(3)(a)(i), applied to any offence committed under s. 244.2(1), whether by intentional discharge of a firearm into a place knowing or being reckless as to whether another person is present in the place or by intentional discharge of a firearm while being reckless as to the life or safety of others. I am of the view that Oud decided that the mandatory minimum sentence established in the former s. 244.2(3)(b) as it applied to any offence under s. 244.2(1) was not grossly disproportionate and did not amount to cruel and unusual punishment.

Did Oud only consider the constitutionality of the mandatory minimum prescribed by the former s. 244.2(3)(b)?

[45]      The judge relied on the fact that Oud considered only the former s. 244.2(3)(b) mandatory minimum, which applied where the offence was committed with a firearm that is neither prohibited nor restricted. The constitutionality of the specific mandatory minimum sentence provision at issue in this case—s. 244.2(3)(a)(i)—was not part of the ratio decidendi in Oud. However, it is my view that the particular circumstances before the court required consideration of the broader approach to stare decisis discussed in Henry. Here, that meant considering whether the decision in Oud is binding in relation to both subsections 244.2(3)(a)(i) and the former s. 244.2(3)(b). And if so, whether the decision in Oud on the mandatory minimum sentence fell within the wider circle of analysis that was “obviously intended for guidance and which should be accepted as authoritative”: Henry at para. 57. To decide that question, the judge should have considered whether there is any difference between subsections 244.2(3)(a)(i) and the former s. 244.2(3)(b) that is material to the s. 12 challenge.

[46]      The judge did not undertake that analysis. He did not consider whether, accepting the decision in Oud that the four-year mandatory minimum sentence for a s. 244.2(1) offence committed using a non-restricted or prohibited firearm withstands constitutional scrutiny, there was any possibility that a five-year sentence for the same criminal behavior with a restricted or prohibited firearm would not withstand constitutional scrutiny. In fairness, it would appear that this issue was not directly argued by counsel for either party. Instead, they simply took opposing positions on whether Oud was binding.

[47]      Counsel have not referred us to any cases that have considered whether a decision upholding a mandatory minimum sentence for an offence committed with a non-restricted firearm is binding in a subsequent case where the Criminal Code also provides for a longer mandatory minimum sentence when the offence is committed with a prohibited or restricted firearm. There are, however, numerous other Criminal Code provisions establishing a more onerous mandatory minimum sentence where the offence is committed with a restricted or prohibited firearm: s. 239(1)(a) (attempt to commit murder); s. 273(2)(a) (aggravated sexual assault); s. 272(2)(a) (sexual assault with a weapon, threats to a third party or causing bodily harm); s. 344(1)(a) (robbery); s. 246(1.1)(a) (extortion); s. 279.1(2)(a) (hostage‑taking); and s. 279(1.1)(a) (kidnapping). Each provision provides for a mandatory minimum that is a year longer where a first offence is committed with a restricted or prohibited firearm.

[48]      The question is whether, accepting the decision in Oud on the constitutionality of the former s. 244.2(3)(b), it is possible to conclude that the mandatory minimum sentence prescribed by s. 244.2(3)(a)(i) could amount to cruel and unusual punishment. The respondent offers no logical explanation as to why the mandatory minimum sentence ruling in Oud would not bind a sentencing judge where the offence was committed with a restricted or prohibited firearm. The respondent acknowledges, as did the judge, that the use of a restricted or prohibited firearm increases the gravity of the offence. The Court in Oud could not conceive of any offence under s. 244.2 committed with a non-restricted or prohibited firearm, for which the former mandatory minimum penalty would be cruel and unusual punishment. Consequently, how could the mandatory minimum penalty for any offence under that section committed with a prohibited or restricted firearm offend s. 12?       

[49]      Just as there is a material difference between the possession and use of firearms which is reflected in the mandatory minimum sentence provisions in the Criminal Code, there is a material difference between use of a non-restricted or prohibited firearm and use of a restricted or prohibited firearm. The gravity of an offence committed with the latter is much greater.

[50]      As noted in Oud, considerable legislative attention has been directed to firearms offences as a result of the increase in gun violence. The categorization and regulation of firearms was central to the decision in Nur, in which the Chief Justice emphasized the danger posed by restricted and prohibited firearms. She described the categories of prohibited and restricted firearms as follows:

[7]        The Criminal Code imposes severe restrictions and sanctions on two classes of firearms. A “prohibited firearm” includes short-barrelled handguns, sawed-off rifles and shotguns, and automatic firearms: Criminal Code, s. 84(1). It is unlawful to possess a prohibited firearm unless the individual possessed the firearm prior to the prohibition coming into force: Firearms Act, s. 12. This grandfathering also applies to next of kin. A “restricted firearm” includes any handgun that is not a prohibited firearm, some semi-automatic firearms, and some firearms that are less than the specified length: Criminal Code, s. 84(1). These weapons are inherently dangerous and are commonly used in criminal activity.

[51]      In Nur, the Court held that a 40-month sentence for a 19-year-old first offender who simply possessed a loaded firearm in a public place for a short period of time was not demonstrably unfit. In Oud, Justice Saunders noted the important distinction between s. 95 of the Criminal Code and ss. 244 and 244.2: the latter address the use, as opposed to the possession of firearms which is “qualitatively different, and of elevated concern to the public and Parliament”: at para. 33.

[52]      Section 718.1 establishes the fundamental principle that a sentence must be proportionate to the gravity of the offence and the degree of responsibility of the offender. Mandatory minimum sentences must be measured against that fundamental principle as well as the other sentencing principles set out in s. 718.2 of the Criminal Code. The difference in the seriousness of the offence when committed with a restricted or prohibited firearm is reflected in the mandatory minimum provisions of both ss. 244(2) and 244.2(3). Oud determined that a four-year mandatory minimum sentence for committing the offence with a non-restricted or prohibited firearm did not offend s. 12 of the Charter. I fail to see how the one-year difference in the mandatory minimum sentence for use of a more dangerous category of firearm could offend s. 12 of the Charter. This is particularly the case as the gravity and seriousness of an offence cannot be attenuated by the personal circumstances of an offender: R. v. Sharma, 2022 SCC 39 at para. 108.

[53]      In summary, it is my view that the judge erred in failing to consider himself bound by the decision in Oud.

Did the sentencing judge err in reconsidering Oud in light of the Cardinal hypothetical?

[54]      The judge appropriately referred to Nur for the proposition that a court should not reconsider the constitutionality of a mandatory minimum sentence provision that has withstood constitutional challenge unless there has been a significant change in the reasonably foreseeable application of the law: at para. 82. However, he found that “the existence of Cardinal” (combined with the fact that the provisions at issue in this case were different from those considered in Oud) was “sufficient to justify examining the constitutionality of the mandatory minimum prescribed by s. 244.2(3)(a)(i)”: at para. 92.

[55]      I have already found that the difference in the s. 244.2(1) and (3) Criminal Code provisions did not justify reconsideration of Oud. As I will explain below, I am also of the view that the Cardinal hypothetical does not meet the threshold set out in Nur and Bedford, which requires circumstances or evidence that “fundamentally shift” the parameters of the debate about the gross disproportionality of the challenged mandatory minimum sentence. Further, the Cardinal hypothetical does not represent a significant development in the law. Accordingly, I am of the view that the judge erred in concluding otherwise.

[56]      In Al-Isawi, this Court upheld the decision of a sentencing judge who dismissed the offender’s challenge to the constitutionality of mandatory sentencing provisions in s. 85(3) and (4) under s. 12. On appeal in Al-Isawi, this Court had to consider whether it was bound by the decision in R. v. Brown, 1994 CanLII 29 (SCC), [1994] 3 S.C.R. 749. In Brown, the Court rejected a challenge to the mandatory minimum sentence provisions in s. 85 based on a hypothetical positing a series of 10 to 20 offences subject to the consecutive sentencing requirement in s. 85(4). Justice Stromberg‑Stein concluded that the new proposed hypothetical did not amount to a “reasonable hypothetical as envisioned by Goltz” (at para. 61) and the appeal was dismissed.

[57]      Justice Stromberg-Stein, with reference to Nur and Bedford, set out the proper approach to stare decisis where a previously upheld mandatory minimum is challenged. She noted that the relevant question is whether, since the previous decision, there have been “different circumstances, new evidence or a significant change in the reasonably foreseeable application of the law”: Al-Isawi at para. 49. She held that while an offender can argue that a provision violates s. 12 by imposing a grossly disproportionate sentence, despite a prior constitutional determination, “stare decisis plays a significant role at the stage of analyzing reasonable hypotheticals”: at para. 50. She referenced the Court’s reasoning at para. 71 of Nur that, “…the threshold for revisiting the constitutionality of a mandatory minimum is high and requires a significant change in the reasonably foreseeable applications of the law”: Al-Isawi at para. 50. She also quoted the Court’s finding at para. 44 of Bedford that, “the threshold for revisiting a matter is not an easy one to reach”: Al‑Isawi at para. 51.

[58]      The application of this framework to the present circumstances raises two questions. First, whether the circumstances presented by the Cardinal hypothetical fundamentally shift the parameters of the debate about the constitutionality of the mandatory minimum. Second, whether new legal issues have been raised since Oud was decided. In considering these questions, it must be remembered that the threshold for revisiting the constitutionality of a mandatory minimum sentence is high.

Does Cardinal fundamentally shift the parameters of the debate?

[59]      A prior declaration of constitutionality can be reconsidered where new circumstances or evidence have “shifted the parameters of the debate”: Bedford at para. 46. This was intended to address the need for finality and stability while recognizing that a lower court should be able to revisit precedent in an appropriate case: Bedford at para. 44. Unfortunately, it can be difficult to determine just when that threshold has been met, making the concept somewhat uncertain in practice. In Bedford, the Court noted that a mere “recharacterization” of the nature of the question before the court does not convert an argument into a new legal issue: at para. 46. To the same effect, in Canada (Attorney General) v. Confédération des syndicats nationaux, 2014 SCC 49, Justices LeBel and Wagner held that a precedent must be followed where “the legal issue remains the same and arises in a similar context”: at para. 24.

[60]      The question is more difficult when considering a s. 12 challenge to a mandatory minimum sentence because the generalized inquiry includes consideration of “reasonable hypotheticals”. In Nur, the Chief Justice emphasized that the inquiry into hypotheticals must be focused on “reasonable hypothetical circumstances, as opposed to far-fetched or marginally imaginable cases”: at para. 54, quoting R. v. Goltz, 1991 CanLII 51 (SCC), [1991] 3 S.C.R. 485 at 506 (emphasis in original). She noted that the inquiry must be “grounded in judicial experience and common sense” and exclude fanciful or remote situations: at para. 62. She held that “personal characteristics cannot be entirely excluded” from reasonable hypotheticals, but that personal features should not be used “to construct the most innocent and sympathetic case imaginable”: at paras. 73–75. These limitations on the consideration of new hypotheticals are necessary in order to maintain some certainty in the law. If a new hypothetical could be considered just because it had not been raised before, valuable judicial resources would be wasted dealing with already‑settled legal issues.

[61]      First, I note that Justice Charbonneau did not, nor did she have to, consider whether she was bound by Oud. She did not have to ask whether the same legal question had previously been decided by a superior court. Accordingly, she did not consider the threshold question that is raised here. As I have indicated, I am of the view that the legal question raised by the Cardinal hypothetical is the same as that considered in Oud. It deals with whether the s. 244.2(3) mandatory minimum sentences offend s. 12 where the actions of the offender meet the “double mens rea” requirement of the offence: the intentional discharge of a firearm with knowledge or recklessness as to the risk to the lives or safety of others. In this case, the threshold question of stare decisis arises. Ms. Dingwall argues that the Cardinal hypothetical shifts the parameters of the debate because the posited circumstances of the offence and the offender are different from those considered in Oud. However, the threshold for reconsideration is high and requires a significant change in the reasonably foreseeable application of the law. Acknowledging the difficulty in applying this threshold to the Cardinal hypothetical, I would arrive at a different conclusion than the judge. In my view, the Cardinal hypothetical does not stretch the parameters of the debate in any real way.

[62]      Undertaking the generalized inquiry, the Court in Oud considered whether the mandatory minimum sentence would result in a grossly disproportionate punishment for a person charged with a s. 244.2 offence. The Court considered scenarios where the mitigating circumstances of the offender and the offence meant that the sentence would be at the lowest end of the possible range. The specific hypothetical before the Court dealt with a young offender who fired a gun into a house he believed was unoccupied, but was reckless in that regard, in order to ward off or intimidate his bullies. The result of the Court’s analysis was stated emphatically by Justice Saunders: “I cannot conceive of an offence under s. 244.2 for which the mandatory minimum penalty would be said to be cruel and unusual punishment”: Oud at para. 50.

[63]      The Cardinal hypothetical posits an offender who is a “young Aboriginal person, suffering from lived and inter-generational trauma”. In R. v. Gladue, 1999 CanLII 679 (SCC), [1999] 1 S.C.R. 688 and R. v. Ipeelee, 2012 SCC 13, the Court confirmed that sentencing judges are required to undertake a specific analysis in determining a fit sentence for an Aboriginal offender, based on s. 718.2(e) of the Criminal Code: Gladue at para. 33. This analysis must account for the systemic factors impacting Aboriginal people in Canada and the extent to which an Aboriginal offender’s community has a conception of sentencing which emphasizes restorative justice. However, the Court in Gladue and Ipeelee emphasized that the Gladue analysis does not result in an automatic sentence reduction. Rather, the sentence imposed will “depend upon all the factors which must be taken into account in each individual case”, including the severity of the offence at issue: Gladue at para. 88 (emphasis in original). The Court noted that “the more violent and serious the offence” the more likely it is that sentences for Aboriginal and non-Aboriginal offenders will be similar: Gladue at para. 79. The Court in Ipeelee affirmed that Gladue principles must nevertheless be applied to violent and serious offences, so that the remedial power of s. 718.2(e) to “reduce overreliance on incarceration” remains strong: at para. 86.

[64]      In Oud, this Court determined that the s. 244.2 offence has such a high degree of moral blameworthiness that it could not conceive of an offence for which the mandatory minimum sentence would be said to be cruel and unusual punishment. I take the Court’s expression of that conclusion to include consideration of both the circumstances of the offence and the hypothetical offender, who may have been Aboriginal and suffering from lived and inter-generational trauma. Accordingly, I do not see that including the further mitigating circumstances of the Cardinal hypothetical offender can be said to shift the parameters of this mandatory minimum sentence debate. It would be incorrect to assume that the Court in Oud failed to account for s. 718.2(e) or the possibility that a s. 244.2 offence could be committed by an Aboriginal offender with strong Gladue factors.

[65]      The analysis that a judge undertakes in sentencing an Aboriginal offender is different than for non-Aboriginal offenders. However, there is no mandatory reduction in sentence for Aboriginal offenders because sentencing must account for various factors, including the seriousness of the offence. The extent to which Gladue factors are present will always impact the court’s sentencing determination, including for the very serious offences under s. 244.2. However, the analysis in Oud as to the weight placed on the moral blameworthiness of the offence is applicable to the Cardinal hypothetical, meaning that the presence of Gladue factors does not render the mandatory minimum sentence grossly disproportionate.

[66]      Second, it is my view that the Cardinal hypothetical is unreasonable because it is uncertain whether the mens rea of the offence is established. This is evident when the circumstances of the Cardinal hypothetical (reproduced at para. 11 of this judgment) are compared to Mr. Cardinal’s actual case. The circumstances of the actual offence, while similar, include an important distinction. In contrast to the hypothetical, after Mr. Cardinal fired the first shot in an effort to kill himself, he fired a second shot while inside the house and a third shot after he went outside.

[67]      At sentencing, the Crown and defence agreed that Mr. Cardinal did not intend to harm anyone other than himself. However, they also agreed that he was reckless as to the safety of other people when he fired the prohibited firearm. The reasons for judgment do not make it clear whether his agreed recklessness applied to both the initial shot, and to the shots he fired after his suicide attempt failed. It was probably not necessary to determine that issue because there was no doubt about his recklessness as to the lives and safety of others because he fired the additional shots. Significantly, Justice Charbonneau found, as conceded by Mr. Cardinal, that a five-year mandatory jail term would not be grossly disproportionate in his case: at para. 63.

[68]      From the brief description of the circumstances of the offence set out in the Cardinal hypothetical, it is not clear that the offender had both the intent to discharge the firearm and was reckless as to the lives and safety of others. The hypothetical circumstances appear to satisfy the first component of the double mens rea requirement, but it is not clear that they would satisfy the second. The actual Cardinal facts clearly established the second mens rea component as Mr. Cardinal fired additional shots in circumstances that established his recklessness for the lives and safety of others. Problematically, in the hypothetical, the court is told that the offender intended to kill himself when he fired a single shot, without sufficient factual context establishing that he acted with recklessness as to the life and safety of others as required by s. 244.2(1)(b). It is problematic because, if the offender acted with the requisite recklessness as to the life and safety of others, it is difficult to see how the gravity and seriousness of the offence is reduced because he also intended to kill himself. It is my view that the hypothetical is uncertain and is, accordingly, unreasonable.

[69]      I note that I am not the first to make this observation. In R. v. Ookowt, 2020 NUCA 5, the Court doubted whether the circumstances of the Cardinal hypothetical “would ever satisfy beyond a reasonable doubt either the actus reus or mens rea requirements for intentionally discharging a weapon into or at a place while knowing or being reckless as to whether others are present”: at para. 92. Although the court was considering a charge under s. 244.2(1)(a), it must follow that if the mens rea could not be satisfied under (1)(a) it could not be established under (1)(b).

[70]      The inclusion of an intervening act by a third party also renders the hypothetical uncertain and therefore unreasonable. This is because the mens rea of the hypothetical offender would need to be determined based on the offender’s actions in discharging a firearm in the immediate presence of others in circumstances where recklessness to their lives and safety was established. The actions of the intervening actor create confusion as to whether the offender had the requisite mens rea. Positing an intervening act introduces uncertainty into the hypothetical offence because it raises questions as to whether the discharge of the firearm and the offender’s recklessness at the time of the discharge were caused by the intervening actor.

[71]      Leaving that question aside, I accept that the hypothetical circumstances could be modified in a way that satisfies the recklessness component. The second mens rea component could be established where a hypothetical offender who was intoxicated and in distress obtained possession of a restricted or prohibited firearm, loaded the firearm (or knew it was loaded), and discharged the firearm in order to attempt suicide in the immediate presence of others, in circumstances where an inference could be drawn that he was reckless as to their lives or safety. Although the offender’s distress and suicidal intention present mitigating circumstances that evoke sympathy, it is not evident to me that these circumstances would significantly lessen the offender’s moral culpability. Accepting that the hypothetical offender intentionally discharged the firearm in circumstances where he was reckless as to the life or safety of others as well as his own life would not make the mandatory minimum sentence grossly disproportionate to the fit sentence for that offender. This would be analogous to the actual facts in Cardinal where the offender, even though he did not intend to harm anyone, acted recklessly in discharging the firearm in circumstances that endangered the lives or safety of others. The intentional discharge of the firearm with reckless intent would still be “no small offence” involving the double mens rea component.

[72]      In short, I am of the view that the Cardinal hypothetical does not meet the Bedford/Nur test: it does not raise new evidence or circumstances sufficient to shift the parameters of the debate about the constitutionality of the mandatory minimum sentence in s. 244.2(3).

Have new legal issues been raised since Oud was decided?  

[73]      Has there been a significant change in the reasonably foreseeable application of the law since Oud? The unanimous answer of appellate courts across Canada is no. Appellate courts have considered the hypothetical advanced in Cardinal, as well as hypotheticals where the offender uses a less deadly weapon as in Oud. No appellate court has ruled that the mandatory minimum sentences contained in s. 244.2(3) are unconstitutional. Since Oud, the mandatory minimum in the former s. 244.2(3)(b) has been upheld by appellate courts in Manitoba, Nunavut and Alberta.

[74]      A number of trial courts in Quebec have found violations of s. 12 by mandatory minimums under s. 244.2, based on a particularized inquiry (R. c. Gunner, 2017 QCCQ 12563; R. c. Neeposh, 2020 QCCQ 1235). The Quebec Court of Appeal has not yet considered the constitutionality of the mandatory minimums under s. 244.2. However, Vézina c. R., 2018 QCCA 739 dealt with an appeal of a conviction under s. 244.2(1)(a), in a case where the trial judge had also ruled that the former s. 244.2(3)(b) violated s. 12 (R. c. Vézina, 2017 QCCQ 7785). The Court of Appeal found that the appellant had committed an offence under s. 244.2(1)(a) by intentionally discharging a firearm inside his residence, knowing that there were other occupants in the residence. It cited Gunner and Cardinal for the proposition that shots fired by someone within a building, towards the interior of the building, are captured by the provision. It ruled that the shots do not have to be directed at another person.

[75]      As discussed earlier in this judgment, Justice Charbonneau concluded that the mandatory minimum in s. 244.2(3)(a)(i) infringed s. 12 based on a hypothetical in Cardinal. She made a similar ruling in R. v. Kakfwi, 2018 NWTSC 13. There have also been trial level decisions upholding mandatory minimum sentences under s. 244.2 (R. v. Mohammad, 2017 ONCJ 297; R. v. Reis, 2017 ONSC 1961) as well as striking them down (R. v. Valade, 2019 ONSC 3033).

[76]      In R. v. Nungusuituq, 2019 NUCJ 6, a trial court held that the mandatory minimum in the former s. 244.2(3)(b) infringed s. 12 based on a reasonable hypothetical. However, the former s. 244.2(3)(b) mandatory minimum was later upheld by the Nunavut Court of Appeal in Ookowt and R. v. Itturiligaq, 2020 NUCA 6. In both cases, the Court found that the mandatory minimum was not a grossly disproportionate sentence for the accused. Both offenders were Inuit, but the Court found that Gladue factors were not strongly present. The Court emphasized the high moral blameworthiness associated with the serious offence at issue, and firearm offences generally. The Court in Ookowt rejected a number of proposed hypotheticals. It found that where the offender fired a gun at a residence as a prank, the gravity of the conduct captured by the offence was not lessened. It found that where “the offender intended to discharge the firearm into or at a place knowing that or being reckless as to whether another person is present … the seriousness of the offence remains grave even if committed by an offender who is under some mental stress or anxiety…”: Ookowt at para. 87. The Court considered the Cardinal hypothetical and held that it would likely not satisfy the actus reus or mens rea requirements for the s. 244.2(1)(a) offence.

[77]      In R. v. McMillan, 2016 MBCA 12, the Manitoba Court of Appeal considered a trial decision which struck down the four-year mandatory minimum in the former s. 244.2(3)(b) as a violation of s. 12 of the Charter, and imposed a one-year term of imprisonment followed by two years of supervised probation: at para. 4. The Court of Appeal held that this was a wholly unfit sentence: at para. 6. The Court did not fully canvas the constitutionality of s. 244.2(3), but simply held that the mandatory minimum was not a grossly disproportionate sentence for the particular accused: at para. 46.

[78]      R. v. Hills, 2020 ABCA 263 is currently under appeal before the Supreme Court of Canada. In Hills, the Alberta Court of Appeal considered a trial court decision holding that the former s. 244.2(3)(b) of the Criminal Code violated s. 12 of the Charter. The reasonable hypothetical under review dealt with a young person intentionally discharging an air-powered pistol or rifle at a residence. Justice Antonio noted that, “Psychological and social harm is inherent in the reckless use of a firearm, even where the risk of physical harm is somewhat reduced” and found that the mandatory minimum was not grossly disproportionate: at paras. 84–85. Justice O’Ferrall came to the same conclusion, but additionally held that the current s. 12 jurisprudence ought to be revisited. He noted that the reasonable hypothetical approach results in “an air of unreality” and is not supported by statutory interpretation grounded in the text of s. 12 of the Charter: at para. 103. Justice Wakeling also concurred in the result, but argued in favour of revisiting the reasonable hypothetical approach based on an originalist theory of constitutional interpretation.

Disposition

[79]      I would allow the appeal and set aside the declaration that the five-year mandatory minimum sentence in s. 244.2(3)(a)(i) is unconstitutional. I would also set aside the four-and-a-half-year sentence and impose a five-year sentence for the s. 244.2(1)(b) conviction to be served concurrently with the sentence for the s. 244(1) offence that has been served. As the sentence has been served, the respondent should not be further incarcerated.

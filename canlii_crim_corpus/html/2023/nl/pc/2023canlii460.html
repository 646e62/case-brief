Place of Hearing: Corner Brook, NL.

Date of Decision: January 11, 2023.

 

Summary: The accused was charged with the offences of assault, assault with a weapon, assault causing bodily harm, and the commission of an assault in which the complainant was choked, contrary to sections 266, 267(a), 267(b) and 267(c) of the Criminal Code of Canada, R.S.C. 1985.

 

She is convicted of the offences of assault and assault causing bodily harm, contrary to sections 266 and 267(b) of the Criminal Code. She is acquitted of the offences of assault with a weapon and the commission of an assault in which the complainant was choked, contrary to sections 267(a) and 267(c) of the Criminal Code.

JUDGMENT OF GORMAN, P.C. J.

Introduction:

 

[1]     On January 31, 2022, the police received a complaint of an assault from a residence in Pasadena.  The police responded to the call and spoke to the person who had made the complaint (Ms. X) at the residence. She alleged that she had been assaulted by Ms. Laite, her former partner.  A subsequent investigation resulted in Ms. Laite being charged with the offences of assault, assault with a weapon, assault causing bodily harm, and the commission of an assault in which the complainant was choked, contrary to sections 266, 267(a), 267(b) and 267(c) of the Criminal Code of Canada, R.S.C. 1985.  Ms. Laite pleaded not guilty to these charges and a trial was conducted.

The Issue:

[2]     The sole issue that I must decide is whether the Crown has proven beyond a reasonable doubt that Ms. Laite committed the offences with which she is charged. 

 

 

My Decision:

[3]     Based upon the totality of the evidence presented, I am satisfied that the Crown has proven beyond a reasonable doubt that Ms. Laite assaulted Ms. X and that she assaulted her and caused her bodily harm, contrary to sections 266 and 267(b) of the Criminal Code.  However, the Crown has failed to prove that Ms. Laite assaulted Ms. X with a weapon or that she choked her during the commission of an assault.  As a result, Ms. Laite is acquitted of the charges laid against her contrary to sections 267(a) and 267(c) of the Criminal Code.

[4]     Let me explain my reasons for these conclusions by commencing with a review of the evidence presented at the trial.

The Evidence Presented at the Trial:

[5]     The evidence presented at the trial established that Ms. Laite and Ms. X had been involved in a relationship over an extended period of time.  Both Ms. X and Ms. Laite testified that the relationship involved a lot of arguing.  Both testified and described various physical altercations.  

[6]     In September of 2016, they began to live together in Pasadena.  The complaint the police received on January 31, 2022, originated from this residence.  Corporal Christopher Pittman and Constable Jensen Stanley, who responded to the complaint, went to the residence and spoke to Ms. X on that day.   This conversation was audio taped.

[7]     Corporal Pittman described Ms. X as being “visibly upset”.  Constable Stanley described Ms. X as “shaken”.  Neither officer saw any injuries to Ms. X.

Ms. X’s Testimony:

[8]     Ms. X testified that she and Ms. Laite had been together since 2015.  She testified that “at first”, their relationship was “good”.  However, “early on” they began to argue a lot.  Ms. X testified that this did not initially involve any physical violence, but that it escalated from “yelling to being physical”.

The Choking:

[9]     Ms. X testified that during arguments, Ms. Laite would often place her hands around her neck.  She indicated that initially this would occur about once a month, but became a more regular occurrence.  Ms. X testified that Ms. Laite would routinely push her head into a pillow if they argued while in bed.  She indicated that “she would get mad at me and I think that was an attempt to stop it, or stop me from, you know, nitpicking or being upset or angry or continuing on with the argument”. 

[10]   Ms. X testified that the choking “was not hard enough to leave marks”, but that it sometimes affected her breathing.  

[11]   Ms. X also testified that Ms. Laite would at times grab her by her shirt collar, “restricting” her neck.  

[12]   Finally, Ms. X testified that Ms. Laite also routinely pushed her against the walls in the residence they shared in Pasadena.  She described Ms. Laite grabbing her arms and placing her forearm against her throat. She testified that this occurred “often” and that it made it difficult for her to breathe.

Throwing the Box:

[13]   The evidence presented at the trial established that on January 15, 2022, Ms. Laite contacted the Royal Canadian Mounted Police because she was concerned about Ms. X’s wellbeing, based upon a comment Ms. X had made before leaving the residence.  After the police spoke to Ms. X and concluded there were no concerns, Ms. X returned home.

[14]   Ms. X testified that after this contact with the police, she and Ms. Laite had an argument about a movie.  She testified that Ms. Laite threw a cardboard box and a dog bed at her and grabbed her by the collar:

We got into a fight about a movie that I got upset over, and she had a headache at the time and didn’t really wanna [sic] argue about it.  So she went in the spare room, but I was angry, so I got up and I was arguing and I hit the door, and that made her angry so she got up at that point and came out and she got angry as well.  So I remember that was when the things were thrown, so the cardboard and the dog bed and the things, and she grabbed my collar then while she was yelling at me.   

 

[15]   Ms. X testified that she was struck by something that was thrown by Ms. Laite and received a cut on her hand as a result of this incident.  

New Year’s Eve:

[16]   Ms. X testified that on New Year’s Eve, 2021, she and Ms. Laite went to a house party hosted by people Ms. Laite worked with.  She indicated that while at the party, Ms. Laite made a comment about her having broken her cellular phone on Christmas Eve.  According to Ms. X, Ms. Laite suggested to her friends that Ms. X had done so purposely.  Ms. X testified that she found this to be embarrassing.  She agreed that she was very upset.

[17]   Ms. X testified that during the drive home from the party, and after they arrived at their residence, she and Ms. Laite were arguing over this comment.  Ms. X indicated that she was very upset and was “yelling” at Ms. Laite. 

[18]   Ms. X testified that that she walked into their bedroom.  Ms. X described Ms. Laite entering the bedroom and pushing her with her two hands.  Ms. X testified that this caused her to fall, striking the bed before ending up on the floor:

When we got home I was really upset over it.  You know, these were her people and I was angry and embarrassed, then so I got upset and I got angry.  I started to get mad at her for it and I was yelling as I went into the bedroom.  I don’t remember when she came in but when she came in she pushed me and that’s when I fell and I hit the bed and I fell to the floor then.

Q.      Are you able to describe that push for us?  How was the push done?

 

A.      I was facing the door, I was back onto the bed.  I don’t remember exactly how she came at me.

 

Q.      What part of, I guess, her body would make contact with your body for the push?

 

A.      Just the chest area, just pushing.

 

Q.      What did she push you with?

 

A.      Her hands.

 

Q.      Can you describe the force of the push?

 

A.      It was hard.  I cut the top of my butt on the corner of the bed and that’s where most of the bruising came from.  And then I--as I went down my side and my arm hit the board along the side of the bed and it scraped and dragged across that.

 

Q.      Sorry, so you talked about you cut the top of your butt off the corner of the bed, your side just to make sure we get it all.

 

A.      So the side of my right side of the body would hit the board that runs along the bottom of the bed as I went to the floor.

 

Q.      And then anything else?  I thought you mentioned one more thing.

 

A.      I cut my arm, so the whole kind of the right side, my rib, my tri-cep area elbow.

 

Q.      And what happened then?

 

A.      It hurt.  I remember that one being painful.  And I know when I got up she was trying to leave.  I remember trying to stop her. 

[19]   Photographs that were entered as an exhibit, show marks and bruising to various parts of Ms. X’s body, including her right side.

Striking Ms. X in the Jaw:

[20]   Ms. X testified that as Ms. Laite was leaving the bedroom, she got up off of the floor and grabbed Ms. Laite by her arm.  She described Ms. Laite pulling her arm away and striking her in the jaw.  Ms. X felt that this occurred accidentally as Ms. Laite moved her arm away.

[21]   Ms. X testified that Ms. Laite then went toward the porch so as to leave the residence through the front door.  Ms. X indicated that she grabbed the door leading in to the porch in order to prevent Ms. Laite from leaving.  Ms. X testified that Ms. Laite stuck her hand, causing her to lose her grip, opened the door and left the residence.

[22]   However, Ms. X also testified that Ms. Laite slept on a sofa in the living room after the incident in which she described being pushed against the bed.  Ms. X was uncertain about the sequence or timing of these events.

At The New Year’s Eve Party:

[23]   On December 31, 2021, Mr. Robert Mercer, a co-worker and friend of Ms. Laite, had a New Year’s Eve party at his residence.  Ms. Laite and Ms. X attended the party.

[24]   Mr. Mercer testified that Ms. X was consuming alcohol and becoming “increasingly intoxicated.”  He testified that while she was sitting on a stool in his kitchen, Ms. X “slipped off of the stool” and fell on to the floor, spilling a drink she had in her hand against a wall. 

[25]   Mr. Mercer testified that he was not “concerned” that Ms. X was hurt and he did not see any injuries.  He indicated that the fall was not a “bad fall” nor “a violent one”. 

[26]   Mr. Mercer testified that when Ms. X and Ms. Laite left his residence, Ms. X was “drunk”.

[27]   Mr. Chris Horan, one of Ms. Laite’s co-workers and a friend of hers, was also at the party. He described Ms. X as being “intoxicated”. He indicated that she was “loud, her speech was slurred and she stumbled”.

[28]   Mr. Horan testified that he was standing next to Ms. X near a kitchen counter.  He indicated that he was talking to her and that Ms. X fell on to the floor.  He described her feet “slipping out” from underneath her. 

[29]   Mr. Horan testified that Ms. X hit the floor hard, striking her “butt” as she did so.  However, Mr. Horan agreed that Ms. X did not appear to have been hurt by the fall.  He was not concerned about her being injured.  He also testified that she subsequently got up and answered a telephone call she received.

[30]   When asked if Ms. X could have fallen because she was bending over to pick something up, Mr. Horan replied that he “did not believe” she would have fallen in the manner she did if this had occurred.

[31]   Ms. X denied that she “fell over” by the counter.  She testified that she had “squatted” down to pick up something she had dropped and lost her balance.  She testified that she did not hit her back or her buttocks or cause any injury to herself.  Ms. X was not cross-examined on whether she slipped off a stool at the party.

Alcohol Consumption on December 31, 2021:

[32]   Ms. X testified that Ms. Laite had one alcoholic drink that evening.  She described herself as “drinking all night”, having “quite a lot to drink”, and being “very drunk”.  Ms. X agreed that she subsequently vomited when she returned home. However, she maintained that the alcohol consumption had not affected her memory of the assault that occurred when she and Ms. Laite returned home. 

[33]   In the statement Ms. X provided to the police on January 31, 2022, she described being “very drunk” on New Year’s Eve and indicated that she could not remember “a whole lot about it”.  In a statement she provided to investigators on February 9, 2022, she indicated that she could not “remember that night completely because I was drinking”. 

[34]   In her testimony, Ms. X said that she could “not remember a lot about that night” but that she recalled the assault that occurred.  She indicated that she was “not saying” the alcohol had no effect, but that she could recall the assault. 

January 2022, At Work:

[35]   Mr. JP works with Ms. X.  He testified that in early January, 2022, she was “not herself” while at work.  He testified that he spoke to her and she began to “cry”.  He described her as being “distraught”.

[36]   Mr. JP testified that that Ms. X told him that she had been assaulted by Ms. Laite on New Year’s Eve.  She showed him bruises on her upper arm and he noticed that there was some “redness” in the area of her jaw.  He described the bruises as being a “dark purple” in color with one on the front of her arm and one on the back. [37]      In cross-examination, Mr. JP was asked to describe what Ms. X told him about the assault.  He indicated that she had told him that Ms. Laite struck her, causing her to fall on to the floor.  He also indicated that Ms. X said that Ms. Laite struck her while she was on the floor.  He was “not sure” if she said she had been punched or kicked while on the floor.

[38]   This description does, in part, contradict Ms. X’s description of how she was assaulted by Ms. Laite on December 31, 2021.  However, Mr. JP was attempting to relate a conversation he had with Ms. X a considerable period of time prior to testifying.  He did not take any notes.  Significantly, Mr. JP’s testimony concerning the bruises he saw on the front and back of Ms. X’s upper arm is consistent with  bruising shown in photographs of Ms. X’s arm that were entered as an exhibit.

January 31, 2022:

[39]   Ms. X testified that after December 31, 2021, she and Ms. Laite separated and Ms. Laite left the residence.  Ms. X indicated that Ms. Laite did, however, return to the residence at times. 

[40]   Ms. X testified that around January 26, 2022, while at the residence, Ms. Laite testified positive for COVID-19.  As a result, both she and Ms. Laite ended up isolating at the residence.

[41]   Ms. X testified that on January 31, 2022, an argument occurred.  Ms. X testified that Ms. Laite went to leave the residence and she tried to stop her from doing so.  Ms. X testified that Ms. Laite turned around and “put her arm around my neck and kind of dropped me to the floor”.  Ms. X was asked if she touched Ms. Laite.  She replied that she could “not remember”. 

[42]   Ms. X testified that Ms. Laite held her down, but released her.  She testified that she got up and grabbed Ms. Laite’s phone out of her hand.  She indicated that she may have pushed Ms. Laite.  She agreed that she knocked Ms. Laite’s phone out of her hand twice, as Ms. Laite was leaving.

[43]   Ms. Laite agreed that the manner in which Ms. X described being “taken-down” to the floor by her was generally consistent with her training, as was the use of a “pressure point” under an individual’s chin.  However, she indicated she was not trained to grab people by the throat with her hand as Ms. X described.

[44]   On behalf of the Crown, Ms. O’Reilly placed great significance upon the manner in which this alleged assault occurred.  She argued that an inference could be drawn that Ms. Laite resorted to her training and that it was not a coincidence that Ms. X described the use of force that is consistent with that training. In some situations this would be a valid and compelling argument.  However, the manner in which Ms. X described being pushed to the floor is not sufficiently unique for me to draw this inference.

Calling the Police:

[45]   Ms. X testified that she called the police as Ms. Laite was leaving.  She hung up before the police answered, but subsequently had contact with them.  When asked if she had told Ms. Laite that if she left she would call the police, Ms. X indicated that she said “I’m calling the police” or “something of that nature”.

[46]   Ms. X testified that she did not want Ms. Laite to leave.  When asked why, she said that she “did not know”, but felt that Ms. Laite “would never come back”.

Ms. X testified that though “it was crappy” most of the time, she “didn’t feel like [she] had anyone else”.

[47]   Ms. X testified that the police arrived at the residence and she spoke to them outside.

The Injuries:

[48]   Ms. X took a series of photographs of injuries she says were inflicted upon her by Ms. Laite.  She provided these to the investigators. Ms. X testified that the photographs were taken on December 27, 2021, January 1, 2022, January 3, 2022, and January 7, 2022.  Ms. X testified that she recalled when some of the photographs were taken and determined when the other photographs were taken through her “Google Cloud”.

[49]   The photographs taken on December 27, 2021, show bruising to Ms. X’s upper arm on both the outside and inside of her arm and a bruise on her leg on the side of her knee.  She testified that these were caused by Ms. Laite grabbing her and pushing her against a wall.  She was unable to indicate when this occurred, but indicated that it was one of the first times that she received a physical injury.

[50]   The photographs taken on January 1, 2022, show a large, very dark and prominent bruise on the top of Ms. X’s hip; a bruise and scrape on the upper portion of her right arm; a bruise on the upper portion of her left arm; bruising and a red mark on the top of her butt; and a bruise and red marks on her back at the top of her ribs.  Ms. X testified that these injuries were caused by Ms. Laite pushing her and her having fallen against the bed at her residence on New Year’s Eve.  

[51]   In the photographs taken on January 1, 2022, Ms. X is wearing a pair of pajama pants which have a plaid pattern.  In the photographs, the pants look to be reddish-pink and black in color.

[52]   The photographs taken on January 3, 2022, show a red mark above Ms. X’s knee, which can be clearly seen.  Ms. X testified that this injury was also caused by Ms. Laite pushing her and her having fallen against the bed on New Year’s Eve.  Ms. X testified that the bruises were “getting darker” and “coming out a little worse”.

[53]   The photographs taken on January 7, 2022, show a light colored bruise to Ms. X’s butt and a very dark bruise above her butt.

[54]   Ms. Laite testified that she had never seen any of the injuries depicted in the photographs entered as an exhibit (my emphasis).  She testified that Ms. X did not complain about being hurt, she “did not tell” her about her injures, and that she did not “see them”:

Q.      And I’m just going to show you this photograph here.  It’s photograph no. 1 and Ms. X testified it was taken on December 27, 2021 and she has--you can see the upper right arm is quite bruised.  So this was before New Year’s.  Did you observe those injuries on her in the house?

 

A.      I did not.

 

Q.      So at no point were you--

 

A.      At no point.

 

Q.      And you lived together and she had these very visible, significant bruises on her arms.

 

A.      I never seen them.  I never seen any of those pictures.

 

Q.      And after New Year’s there are some other photographs that you’ve seen in the course of this trial when they were being entered.  The photographs from January 1, I’ll point specifically to photograph no. 4 of Consent no. 4 and that photograph is bruising sort of in the lower back area.  I’ll also point you to photograph no. 11 which shows bruising--it looks like a purplish bruise, it’s right above the elbow, and then another older bruise at the top of the arm.  So living together those would have been injuries that you would have seen. 

 

A.      Well we weren’t together and I mean I didn’t see her naked or coming out of the shower or anything like that.  I had never seen those bruises on her body.

 

Q.      If she wore a t shirt you would have seen the bruises on her arms.

 

A.      I didn’t see the bruises.

 

Q.      And I mean you sort of fought about things that you know had happened on New Year’s and previously, and so I’d put to you that you know Ms. X would have made you aware of the injuries she had and that she was in pain.

 

A.      She did not tell me she was in pain or that she was injured or she had any bruising on her body.

 

Q.      So you lived in the home together and she spent time just in her room after New Year’s.  You went out, you did things all day, and I’d put to you that she was still in her room when you returned in the evening. 

 

A.      She might have been, but she came out.  She was arguing with me. 

 

Q.      And at that time was she wearing a t shirt and pajama bottoms?

 

A.      She was wearing a t-shirt, I remember that, and I can’t remember specifically.  She had a white t shirt on, but I can’t remember if she was wearing those pajama pants or what, or pajama pants at all.

 

Q.      And you didn’t see her the next morning when you left for your sister’s for brunch; did you?

 

A.      Not when I left, no.

 

Q.      Okay. And so you were in her company when she wore a t-shirt in the house‑‑

 

A.      Yeah.

 

Q.      --in the days after January 1.

 

A.      I didn’t see the bruises.  If she wore a t-shirt and had bruises I didn’t see the bruises.  But she did not make me aware that she was hurt in the way that she’s described here.  She did not let me know.  She didn’t tell me, I didn’t see it.

The Onus and Standard of Proof:

[97]   Every person charged with a criminal offence in Canada is presumed to be innocent until the Crown has proven beyond a reasonable doubt that she or he committed the offence with which that person is charged.  The onus of proof, as regards proving guilt, never switches from the Crown to the accused.  In deciding whether the Crown has proven its case to the criminal standard, I must consider the whole of the evidence and I may only convict if I am satisfied that the Crown has established Ms. Laite’s guilt beyond a reasonable doubt.

[98]   In R. v. Lifchus, 1997 CanLII 319 (SCC), [1997] 3 S.C.R. 320, the Supreme Court of Canada indicated that proof beyond a reasonable doubt “does not involve proof to an absolute certainty, it is not proof beyond any doubt nor is it an imaginary or frivolous doubt” (at paragraph 36).   In R. v. Starr, 2000 SCC 40 (CanLII), [2000] 2 S.C.R. 144, however, the Court pointed out that the burden of proof placed upon the Crown lies “much closer to absolute certainty than to a balance of probabilities” (at paragraph 242).  

[99]   In R. v. Darnley, 2020 ONCA 179, it was indicated that a reasonable doubt “need not arise from the evidence…It can arise from the absence of evidence, from what the Crown has failed to prove” (at paragraph 33).

[100] In R. v. J.K., 2015 NLCA 14, the Court of Appeal stressed the importance of trial judges applying the reasonable doubt standard “to the cumulative effect of all the evidence pointing away from the guilt of the accused, weighed against the cumulative effect of all the evidence pointing toward the guilt of the accused” (at paragraph 5). The Court of Appeal cautioned trial judges to refrain from applying the reasonable doubt standard “to items of evidence in a piecemeal fashion. It is settled law that this constitutes a legal error” (at paragraph 5). 

[101] Similarly, in R. v. Blanchard, 2022 NLCA 15, the Court of Appeal pointed out that “[i]t is well-established law that a Court, in a criminal case…is to assess the evidence as a whole with a view to determining whether all of it, taken together, establishes whether the subject offence is made out beyond a reasonable doubt…This does not mean that pieces of evidence cannot be carefully examined. All evidence must be examined with a view to determining its weight, if any.  What it does mean is that a ‘case is not decided by a series of separate and exclusive judgments on each item or by asking what does that by itself prove, or does it prove guilt?  That is not the process at all.  It is the cumulative effect’” (at paragraphs 53 and 56).  As a result, “pieces of evidence are not meant to be evaluated in isolation from each other.  It is the whole of the evidence, composed of all pieces, that must be considered together, and it is that whole to which the standard of proof beyond a reasonable doubt applies” (Blanchard, at paragraph 57).

When an Accused Person Testifies:

[102] When an accused person testifies, a trial judge must assess his or her evidence applying the reasonable doubt standard. The judgment of the Supreme Court of Canada in R. v. W.(D.), 1991 CanLII 93 (SCC), [1991] 1 S.C.R. 742, supports the adopting of an approach to the assessment of an accused person’s testimony by using a three step analysis. 

 

 

[103] Those steps are as follows:

(1) in the first step, the trial judge should ask him or herself whether she or he believes the testimony provided by the accused.  If so, an acquittal must be entered.  If not, then

(2) the second step requires a consideration by the trial judge as to whether the accused person’s evidence causes her or him to have a reasonable doubt concerning the accused person’s guilt. If so, an acquittal must be entered. However, if the answer to the first and second questions is no, then

(3) the final step in the analytical process developed in W.(D.) requires the trial judge to consider the totality of the evidence presented to determine if the accused’s guilt has been proven by the Crown beyond a reasonable doubt.

[104] It has been noted that “[m]uch ink has been spilled over W.(D.)” but that it “is simply a methodology that triers use to determine whether the prosecution has met its burden of proving each element of the offence beyond a reasonable doubt. In cases like this one, involving ‘conflicting testimonial accounts’, the methodology can be helpful in ensuring that triers keep the burden squarely on the Crown” (see R. v. A.J.K., 2022 ONCA 487, at paragraph 22). 

[105] In R. v. Gauthier, 2022 ABCA 121, the Alberta Court of Appeal pointed out that “[t]hese steps must be undertaken in the context of all the evidence, cumulatively and not in isolation…and specifically applied to all exculpatory evidence that the Crown must negate beyond a reasonable doubt…The purpose of the W(D) approach is to ensure the burden never shifts from the Crown to prove every element of the offence beyond a reasonable doubt. When faced with competing stories, the court must not simply prefer one over the other…In other words, if, after a careful consideration of all of the evidence, the court is unable to decide whom or what to believe, it must acquit…If the court is unable to resolve conflicting material evidence, it has a reasonable doubt as to guilt and must acquit” (at paragraphs 28 and 29).   

[106] In R. v. Best, 2016 NLCA 10, the Court of Appeal considered the approach a trial judge should adopt when an accused person testifies and indicated that what “is required is that a judge consider whether he or she believes an accused, and even if he or she does not, whether the accused’s evidence leaves him or her with a reasonable doubt about the accused’s guilt.  If he or she has no reasonable doubt about the accused’s guilt after these considerations, he or she must still ask him or herself if he or she is convinced of the accused’s guilt on the basis of the evidence he or she does accept” (at paragraph 7). 

A Summary:

[107] In summary, the Crown must prove beyond a reasonable doubt that the accused committed the offence with which she or he is charged. Though this standard of proof does not require proof to an absolute certainty, it is closer to such proof than it is to proof on a balance of probabilities.

[108] If the accused testifies, the criminal standard of proof must be applied to the accused person’s evidence.  The W.(D.) three-step analytical framework is designed to ensure that a trial judge does not follow a forbidden line of reasoning: do I prefer the evidence presented by the Crown or the evidence presented by the accused? This line of reasoning is forbidden because its adoption offends two principal elements of our criminal law: that the accused is presumed innocent and the Crown must prove the accused’s guilt beyond a reasonable doubt.  Thus, the “lack of credibility of an accused does not equate to proof of guilt beyond a reasonable doubt” (see R. v. Lewin, 2020 MBCA 13, at paragraph 22).  As pointed out in R. v. DG, 2019 ABCA 96, the “ultimate aim of W(D) is to ensure that a trier of fact does not simply engage in a credibility contest between the complainant and the accused, determining conviction or acquittal based on who is more believable. The evidence of the accused may be disbelieved in whole or in part and still lead to a reasonable doubt.  W(D) also guards against the shifting of the onus of proof, which always remains with the Crown” (at paragraph 6).

1. if I accept Ms. Laite’s testimony, I must acquit her of all of the charges laid against her;

2. if I do not accept Ms. Laite’s testimony, I must consider whether her testimony, and the other evidence presented by her, causes me to have a reasonable doubt in relation to any of the charges laid against her; and

Assessing the Reliability of a Witness’ Evidence:

[110]   It has been noted that “the process of assessing credibility cannot be reduced to or confined by legal rules…there is no fixed set of rules to use in assessing the credibility of a witness” (see R. v. Bowers, 2022 ABCA 149, at paragraphs 39 and 40). However, in assessing a witness’ testimony, a trial judge must distinguish between honesty and reliability.  As noted in R. v. Kishayinew, 2019 SKCA 127, per Justice Tholl, honesty should not always be equated with reliability (at paragraph 59):

…it is important to understand the distinction between credibility and reliability because the assessment of credibility is a separate consideration from the assessment of reliability. It is the difference between the witness’s willingness to testify truthfully (credibility) and the accuracy of the witness’s testimony (reliability). Finding a witness to be credible does not equate to finding a witness’s testimony to be reliable. While a non-credible witness’s testimony will not be reliable, a credible witness’s testimony is not necessarily reliable: see Morrissey, H.C., Patrick, Schaff, Wollf, and R v Pelletier, 2019 SCKA 113 at paras 138–139.

 

[111] In R. v. Wolff, 2019 SKCA 103, the Saskatchewan Court of Appeal indicated that it “is clear that there are two important aspects for a trier of fact to consider in assessing the testimony of witnesses: (i) credibility, and (ii) reliability. Credibility has to do with the veracity of witness’s testimony; reliability has to do with its accuracy. A witness who is credible may provide unreliable evidence, because honest witnesses can misperceive events, have poor memory, or just be wrong” (at paragraph 38).  The Court of Appeal also indicated that a trial judge “must consider both aspects of a witness’s testimony” (at paragraph 39).

[112]   Justice Tholl noted in Kishayinew, that there “are many reasons why witnesses – who are forthright, acting in good faith and honestly doing their best to tell the truth during testimony – may not be reliable. Some of those reasons include (but are not limited to) the following (at paragraph 60):

(a) inability to properly observe the events in the first instance;

 

(b) confusion with a different event;

(c) intoxication;

(d) passage of time;

(e) nervousness during testimony; or

(f) other factors affecting a witness’s ability to observe, remember and recount the events.

[113]   In R. v. Vickerson, 2005 CanLII 23678 (ON CA), [2005] O.J. No. 2798, the Ontario Court of Appeal indicated that in “assessing the evidence of a witness, a judge must consider the witness's reliability as well as credibility…the integrity of the witness relates to the witness's credibility and is of little assistance in assessing the reliability of the witness's evidence” (at paragraph 28).  Similarly, in R. v. Perrone, 2014 MBCA 74, the Manitoba Court of Appeal noted that “the fact that a witness may be found to be credible does not answer the question of whether the evidence provided is reliable…The distinction is an important one and must be recognized by a trial judge in order to reach a just verdict” (at paragraphs 27 and 28).

[114] The Court of Appeal of this Province has indicated that there is no rule that precludes a trial judge “from using collateral evidence for purposes of assessing credibility” (see R. v. M.A., 2022 NLCA 41, at paragraph 11). 

Motive and Intoxication:

[115] Intoxication can obviously effect the reliability of a witness’ evidence.  It can impair memory and the ability to accurately perceive and recall events.

[116] A motive to fabricate can also be an important factor in assessing a witness’ credibility.  It has been pointed out that it “is difficult to think of a factor which, as a matter of common sense and life experience, would be more germane to a witness’ credibility than the existence of a motive to fabricate evidence” (see R. v. Batte (2000), 2000 CanLII 5751 (ON CA), 49 O.R. (3d) 321 (C.A.), at paragraph 120). 

[117] In this case, Ms. Laite testified that Ms. X indicated that she would “pay” for having contacted the police and that she was going to contact the police and make a complaint if Ms. Laite left the residence. Neither counsel pursued these as being important, but I must address them.  As pointed out by the Saskatchewan Court of Appeal, “[w]here credibility is a central issue and there is evidence suggesting that a complainant may have been motivated to fabricate an allegation against the accused, the trier of fact is required to consider this information” (see R. v. W.D.M., 2022 SKCA 64, at paragraph 42). 

[118] Both of the comments referred could be seen as constituting evidence of a motive to make a false complaint.  In other words, it could be argued that Ms. X has fabricated these allegations because of Ms. Laite having left her and because Ms. Laite contacted the police about her safety. However, well in advance of the complaint to the police, Ms. X told Mr. JP about the alleged New Years’ Eve assault and she was documenting the injuries she says were inflicted upon her by Ms. Laite as early as December 27, 2021.  In addition, her injuries are generally consistent with her description of what occurred on December 31, 2022. 

[119] The fact that a person reports an assault to the police because of something else the accused did, does not mean that a false complaint was made.  Individuals make decisions on whether to complain to the police for various reasons.  Having said this, I must keep the evidence referred to in mind when assessing Ms. X’s credibility.  It is also important that I keep in mind that lack of proof of a motive to fabricate is not the same as no such motive existing and that there is no onus on an accused person to establish such a motive (see R. v. Gerrard, 2022 SCC 13).

The Offence of Assault:

[121]   The offence of assault is defined in section 265 of the Criminal Code.  For the purposes of this trial, sections 265(1)(a) is the relevant provision.  It states as follows:

A person commits an assault when

(a) without consent of another person, he applies force intentionally to that other person directly or indirectly.

 

[122]   In R. v. Ewanchuk (1999), 1999 CanLII 711 (SCC), 131 C.C.C. (3d) 481 (S.C.C.), the Supreme Court of Canada, in the context of considering the offence of sexual assault, indicated that a sexual assault is “an assault within any one of the definitions in s. 265(1) of the Code”.  The Court held that “the Crown need only prove that, the accused intended to touch the complainant in order to satisfy the basic mens rea requirement” (at paragraph 41).   Similarly, in R. v. LBC, 2019 ABCA 505, it was held that the “force” requirement for the offence of assault is “satisfied by any degree of touching, however light” (at paragraph 33).  More recently, in R. v. Kirkpatrick, 2022 SCC 33, the Supreme Court, also in the context of considering the elements of the offence of sexual assault, indicated that under s. 265(1)(a) of the Criminal Code, “a person commits an assault by intentionally applying force to another person, directly or indirectly, without their consent…The actus reus of the offence is ‘unwanted sexual touching’, while the mens rea is the intention to touch, knowing, being reckless of, or being wilfully blind to a lack of consent from the person being touched” (at paragraphs 27 and 28).

[123]   In R. v. Barton, 2019 SCC 33, the Court held that a “person commits the actus reus of sexual assault ‘if he touches another person in a sexual way without her consent’…The mens rea consists of the ‘intention to touch and knowing of, or being reckless of or wilfully blind to, a lack of consent on the part of the person touched’” (at paragraph 87).

[124]   Thus, the least touching of another person without their consent constitutes an assault (see R. v. Dawydiuk, 2010 BCCA 162). The strength of the force is immaterial (see R. v. Palombi, 2007 ONCA 486), but the application of the force must be intentional.

The Intentional Application of Force:

[125]   In R. v. Patrick, 2017 SKCA 95, it was pointed out that section 265(1)(a) "is concerned with an actual or direct assault on a person. This concept is understood to mean the intentional application of force, directly or indirectly, to a person without their consent" (at paragraph 29).  In Dawydiuk, the British Columbia Court of Appeal held that in the context of section 265(1)(a) of the Criminal Code the word "intentionally" simply means, "'not done by accident or through honest mistake'" (at paragraph 29).  Similarly, in R. v. D.B., 2022 SKCA 76, it was held that the actus reus of an assault is established by proof a touching and the “absence of consent. The first element is established by proof that the accused’s actions were voluntary” (at paragraph 68).

Assault With a Weapon:

[126]     Section 267(a) of the Criminal Code defines the offence of assault with a weapon.  It states as follows:

Every one who, in committing an assault,

 

carries, uses or threatens to use a weapon or an imitation thereof...    

 

is guilty of an offence.

 

[127] In R. v. Steele, 2007 SCC 36 (CanLII), [2007] 3 S.C.R. 3, the Supreme Court of Canada, in interpreting the word “uses” in the context of section 85(1) of the Criminal Code (“Every person commits an offence who uses a firearm…”) held that “an offender ‘uses’ a firearm, within the meaning of s. 85(1), where, to facilitate the commission of an offence or for purposes of escape, the offender reveals by words or conduct the actual presence or immediate availability of a firearm. The weapon must then be in the physical possession of the offender or readily at hand” (at paragraph 32).

[128]     Section 2 of the Criminal Code defines a "weapon" as follows:

"weapon" means any thing used, designed to be used or intended for use

(a) in causing death or injury to any person, or

(b) for the purpose of threatening or intimidating any person and, without restricting the generality of the foregoing, includes a firearm.

 

[129] In Rowe v. The King, 1951 CanLII 7 (SCC), [1951] S.C.R. 713, it was held that the accused, who had a handgun, had “used” it when he “pulled it out and held it in his hand. That was a use, under any definition of that very ordinary word” (at page 717).

Assault Causing Bodily Harm:

[130]   The offence of assault causing bodily harm is defined in section 267(b) of the Criminal Code in the following manner:

Every one who, in committing an assault

(b) causes bodily harm to the complainant is guilty of an...offence.

What Constitutes Bodily Harm?

[131]     "Bodily harm" is defined in section 2 of the Criminal Code as meaning "any hurt or injury to a person that interferes with the health or comfort of the person and that is more than merely transient or trifling in nature”.

[132]   In R. v. Bulldog, 2015 ABCA 251, the Alberta Court of Appeal suggested that the manner in which “bodily harm” is defined in the Criminal Code creates a “low threshold” (at paragraph 44):

Section 2's definition of "bodily harm" states a low threshold: R v Dorscheid, 1994 ABCA 18, at para 11, [1994] A.J. No. 56 (CA). It means something more than "a very short time period and an injury of very minor degree which results in a very minor degree of distress": R v Dixon (1988), 1988 CanLII 2824 (BC CA), 42 CCC (3d) 318 at 332, [1988] 5 WWR 577 (Esson JA, as he then was, concurring). Not surprisingly, then, indisputably minor injuries have been found to constitute "bodily harm": R v Rabieifar, [2003] O.J. No. 3833 (QL) (CA) (scratches and abrasions of less than one inch on the complainant's body, and some bruising and swelling on her face, thigh and hand); R v CK, 2001 BCCA 379, at para 3, [2001] BCJ No 1119 (QL) (small bruise on the complainant's right calf, a small anal tear, and a deviated septum that resulted in some bruising and swelling, all of which was said by a physician to be "not serious and ... expected to resolve itself within a few days"); R v Moquin, 2010 MBCA 22, at paras 32-33, 251 Man R (2d) 160 (several bruises lasting 11 days, a sore hand and a sore throat); and Dorscheid (scrapes, lacerations and bruises).

[133]   In R. v. Dixon (1988), 1988 CanLII 2824 (BC CA), 42 C.C.C. (3d) 318 (Y.T.C.A.), it was held, at paragraph 45, that a transient or trifling injury is "an injury of very minor degree which results in a very minor degree of distress."  It has been held that the concept of bodily harm “covers everything from minor injuries that, while neither transient or trifling, resolve relatively quickly, all the way to permanent and life-altering injuries that approach the seriousness of a fatality” (see R. v. Gejdos, 2017 ABCA 227, at paragraph 63).

[134]   In R. v. Moquin, 2010 MBCA 22, it was indicated that interference “with comfort – that is, discomfort -- is sufficient to constitute bodily harm, if it is more than trifling and transient. Pain causing discomfort, if it is more than trifling and transient, is sufficient, even if it does not impair a person's ability to function” (at paragraph 31).

Choking, Suffocating and/or Strangling During an Assault:

[135] Section 267(c) of the Criminal Code states as follows:

Every person is guilty of an…offence…who, in committing an assault,

 

(c) chokes, suffocates or strangles the complainant.

 

[136] This section requires that the Crown prove that the accused committed an assault and in doing so, choked, suffocated or strangled the complainant.  The words “chokes, suffocates or strangles” are not defined in the Criminal Code, but are words of common usage (see R. c. Calvaire, 2021 QCCQ 15468, at paragraph 52). 

[137] It has been held that these words “must be construed in the context of the provision in which they appear…they generally refer to conduct designed to prevent or seriously impair a person’s ability to breathe. In this sense, there is overlap between the concepts of ‘choking,’ ‘suffocating’ and ‘strangling’. They include actions designed to block or constrict a person’s airway, such as by squeezing the neck or throat, or covering the nose and mouth” (see R. v. Bear-Knight, 2021 SKQB 308, at paragraph 77).  However, section 267(c) does not require that the accused intended to constrict a complainant’s airway or ability to breathe. Section 267(c) incorporates the mens rea for an assault. Thus it requires proof of an intentional application of force without consent.  In contrast to section 265(1)(a), section 267(c) specifies the type of force that must be proven: choking, suffocating or strangling.  This is proven if the accused places their hand or hands around a person’s neck and applies any force, no matter how minimal. 

[138] In R. v. C.M., 2022 ONCJ 505, it was held that “the Crown can prove the Assault by Choking offence even though bodily harm is not present. ‘Chokes’, ‘suffocates’ or ‘strangles’ are not defined in the Criminal Code. Generally speaking, these elements are made out where the Crown proves beyond a reasonable doubt that the accused took some action ‘designed to block or constrict a person’s airway, such as by squeezing the neck or throat, or covering the nose and mouth’.  To these elements, I would add the alternative that the offence can also be proven where there is evidence beyond a reasonable doubt that the accused took some action which applies pressure to the respiratory system of the complainant such that her ability to breathe is interfered with in meaningful way” (at paragraph 96).

[139] The use of the word “includes” illustrates that choking does not always involve attempting to constrict an airway.  Placing your hands on another person’s throat and squeezing will constitute the act of choking.

 

The Positions of the Parties:

[140] Both counsel made thorough submissions.  I thank them for their assistance. 

The Position of the Crown:

[141] The Crown submitted that Ms. Laite should be convicted of each of the charges laid against her.

[142] Ms. O’Reilly described Ms. X’s evidence as “frank and forthright”.  She argued that this case illustrated a progression in violence which led to Ms. X eventually, though reluctantly, contacting the police.  She submitted that Ms. X was a credible witness who provided honest and reliable evidence.

[143] Ms. O’Reilly conceded that Ms. X was intoxicated on December 31, 2022, but pointed out that Ms. X testified that she recalled what occurred and noted that much of what Ms. X described occurring was confirmed by Ms. Laite’s evidence.

[144] Finally, Ms. O’Reilly placed significant emphasis on the importance of the injuries depicted in the photographs.  She argued that the injuries could not have been caused by the falls at the New Year’s Eve party and that they provide significant corroboration of Ms. X’s descriptions of being assaulted.   In addition, she submits that considering that Ms. Laite and Ms. X were isolating in the same residence, it would have been impossible for Ms. Laite not to have seen some of the injuries, despite her evidence to the contrary.  

 

Ms. Laite’s Position:

[145] Ms. Laite submitted that she should be acquitted of all of the charges laid against her.

[146] Mr. Ash described Ms. Laite’s evidence as being “frank, sincere and genuine”.  He submitted that it did not contain any significant contradictions or inconsistencies and was corroborated by other evidence presented.  Referring to W.(D.), he argued that this must lead to Ms. Laite being acquitted in relation to each of the charges laid against her.  In the alternative, he argues that the totality of the evidence presented should cause me to have a reasonable doubt that Ms. Laite committed any of the offences with which she has been charged.  Mr. Ash conceded that Ms. Laite applied force to Ms. X on two occasions, but argued that the force applied was reasonable and excused by sections 34 and 35 of the Criminal Code. 

[147] In contrast, he submitted that Ms. X’s evidence was unreliable.  He pointed out that Ms. X was highly intoxicated on December 31, 2022, and argued that this should cause me to doubt the accuracy and reliability of her description of what occurred that evening. In addition, he also pointed out that Ms. X fell on two occasions while at Mr. Mercer’s residence on New Year’s Eve, which would explain the bruising shown in the photographs.  Finally, he argued that considering the limited nature of the contact that occurred between Ms. Laite and Ms. X after December 31, 2022, it is not surprising that Ms. Laite would not have seen any of the injuries to Ms. X that were depicted in the photographs entered as exhibits.  

Analysis:

[148] The evidence presented at the trial establishes that Ms. X and Ms. Laite were involved in a relationship over a number of years.  They moved to Pasadena in 2016 and lived together, with separations occurring, until January of 2022.  Both described the relationship as being one in which there were continuous arguments.

[149] Ms. X described being assaulted by Ms. Laite on several occasions in various ways.  The assaults were said to include:

-choking;

-pushing;

-grabbing; and

-the throwing of things. 

[150] Ms. Laite denied having assaulted Ms. X.  She testified that she did not react physically to the difficulties she described having with Ms. X, though she agreed that on one occasion she threw some items around the residence in Pasadena and on another, punched a wall, leaving a hole.  

[151] When considering diametrically opposed descriptions of a purported incident, a trial judge must refrain from approaching the evidence solely on the basis of which version they prefer or find more credible.  In addition, as pointed out by the Nova Scotia Court of Appeal in R. v. Mah, 2002 NSCA 99, a “judge at a criminal trial is not attempting to resolve the broad factual question of what happened. The judge's function is the more limited one of deciding whether the essential elements of the charge have been proved beyond reasonable doubt” (at paragraph 41).  This may appear to be an incongruous method of dealing with conflicting testimony, but it is not in the context of a criminal trial because of the presumption of innocence. 

[152] Trial judges must refrain from assessing conflicting evidence by asking, do I prefer the evidence of the Crown or the accused.  This line of reasoning is forbidden because it leads to a form of reasoning in which the standard and onus of proof is ignored.  As recently pointed out, also by the Nova Scotia Court of Appeal, “the path to conviction can never degenerate into a contest between whether to believe the complainant or exculpatory evidence.  In order to convict, the trier of fact must be satisfied beyond a reasonable doubt of the accused’s guilt” (see R. v. C.E.G., 2023 NSCA 1, at paragraph 39).

[153] In this case, there was a substantial amount of conflicting evidence, particularly in relation to the essence of the assault allegations.  Though in such a case, confirmatory or corroborative evidence is not required for a conviction to be entered, such evidence can be important.  In this case, such evidence was presented.  For instance, the evidence provided by Ms. Laite’s sister corroborates Ms. Laite’s testimony in relation to hiding between two vehicles outside the residence in Pasadena after one incident.  The evidence of Mr. Mercer and Mr. Horan confirms that Ms. X fell on two occasions while at the New Year’s Eve party.  Finally, there is also evidence that confirms portions of Ms. X’s evidence.

The Injuries:

[154] As mentioned earlier, a series of photographs of injuries to various parts of Ms. X’s body was presented as evidence.  These photographs were taken by Ms. X and she provided them to the police and investigators. 

[155] The photographs “speak for themselves” and I accept Ms. X’s evidence that these photographs were taken on December 27, 2021, January 1, 2022, January 3, 2022, and January 7, 2022.  Some of these injuries were seen by Mr. JP in early January 2022.   As pointed out by the Supreme Court of Canada in R. v. Nikolovski, 1996 CanLII 158 (SCC), [1996] 3 S.C.R. 1197, videotapes and photographs are "real evidence in the sense that that term has been used in earlier cases, but it is to a certain extent, testimonial evidence as well.  It can and should be used by a trier of fact in determining whether a crime has been committed and whether the accused before the court committed the crime. It may indeed be a silent, trustworthy, unemotional, unbiased and accurate witness who has complete and instant recall of events.  It may provide such strong and convincing evidence that of itself it will demonstrate clearly either the innocence or guilt of the accused." 

[156] In this case, the photographs, as some video recordings do, do not show the commission of an incident said to constitute the offence.  However, they are highly probative and constitute significant evidence supporting portions of Ms. X’s evidence.  For instance, Ms. X testified that when she was pushed into the bed by Ms. Laite she was wearing pajamas and they were ripped.  A photograph of Ms. X’s pajamas were entered as an exhibit and they show a rip in the pajamas. Ms. Laite testified that Ms. X had a dress on when she fell in the bedroom.  However, a photograph taken the next day, shows Ms. X wearing the pajamas she described wearing the night before. 

[157] The photographs of Ms. X’s injuries, which I conclude are reliable and accurate, show the following injuries to her:

-large bruises to her upper arm on both the outside and inside of her arm and a bruise on her leg on the side by her knee (these photograph’s were taken on December 27, 2021);

-a very dark and prominent bruise on the top of her hip;

-bruises and scrape on the upper portion of her right arm;

-a bruise on the upper portion of her left arm;

-bruising and a red mark on the top of her butt; 

-a bruise and red marks on her back at the top of her ribs (these photograph’s were taken on January 1, 2022, the day after Ms. X testified that Ms. Laite pushed her, causing her fall against the bed in the master bedroom in the residence in Pasadena); and

-subsequent photographs of the same injuries (these photographs were taken on January 3 and 7, 2022) showing the progression in the visibility of the injuries to her hip, her buttocks, her legs, and her side.

[158] The injuries shown in the photographs are readily apparent, numerous, and at various parts of Ms. X’s body.  These injuries do not in and by themselves prove that Ms. Laite assaulted Ms. X, but they provide significant confirmatory evidence in relation to Ms. X’s testimony in which she indicated that she was assaulted in various ways by Ms. Laite, including having been pushed against a bed. 

[159] The photographs provide confirmatory evidence of Ms. X’s evidence in which she described the assaults causing her bodily harm.   Though Ms. Laite does not have to explain their origin, she puts forward two possibilities: the two falls at the New Years’ Eve party and the accidental fall in the bedroom.  However, some of the bruising occurred prior to December 31, 2001, as they can be seen in the photographs taken on December 27, 2021.  The bruises shown in these latter photographs, which are significant in size, could not have been caused by Ms. X falling at the party or at home.  In addition, to being numerous, the bruises and marks are significant in size and are clearly visible.  Anyone living with Ms. X would had to have seen some of them.

[160] I do not accept the proposition that the multitude of other injuries shown in the photographs were caused by Ms. X falling on to the floor at the New Year’s Eve party.  No one who saw her fall was concerned nor did they feel she was hurt.   Mr. Mercer, for instance, testified that the fall off of the stool was not a “bad fall” nor “a violent one”.  Mr. Horan described Ms. X falling to the floor by her feet slipping out from underneath her.  The descriptions of these two falls are not consistent with such a multitude of injuries being caused by them.

[161] The injuries are numerous and on different parts of Ms. X’s body.  The evidence establishes that Ms. Laite and Ms. X lived in the same residence for a number of days after December 31, 2021 and between January 26 and January 31, 2022 (the period that Ms. X and Ms. Laite isolated together at the Pasadena residence because of COVID-19). Based upon the number of marks and injuries, their locations, and their clear visibility, I conclude that it would have been impossible for Ms. Laite not to have seen some of them.  I conclude that her evidence to the contrary is not reliable or honest.  This impacts all of the evidence provided by Ms. Laite.   

[162] I found Ms. X to an impressive witness.  She testified in a clear, direct and frank manner.  She did not attempt to diminish her role in what occurred.  I conclude that her evidence was credible and reliable. There were contradictions and inconsistencies in her evidence, but they did not impact her overall credibility or her descriptions of the various assaults.  Her description of the assaults was reasonable and logical.  Significant evidence of a confirmatory nature was presented. I am satisfied that Ms. X did not provide false or misleading evidence. Having said this, each of the alleged offences must be assessed to determine if the Crown has proven the specific charges alleged.  Those charges are as follows:

1. section 266, assault by pushing and grabbing Ms. X;

2. section 267(a), assault with a weapon by hitting Ms. X with items that she threw at Ms. X;

3. section 267(b), assaulting Ms. X by pushing her against the bed and thereby causing her bodily harm; and

4.  section 267(c), choking Ms. X.

Section 266, Assault by Pushing Ms. X:

[163] Ms. X described a number of incidents in which she described Ms. Laite grabbing her and pushing her against the walls at the residence in Pasadena. She testified that many of these occurred prior to December 31, 2021.  The photographs taken on December 27, 2021, show two large bruises to Ms. X’s upper arm on both the outside and inside of her arm and a bruise on her leg on the side of her knee.  These injuries are clearly visible.  Obviously these injuries occurred prior to or upon the date the photographs were taken.  The December 27, 2021, photographs provide confirmatory evidence of Ms. X’s claim of having been assaulted by Ms. Laite prior to that date. There is no evidence of her having fallen prior to December 31, 2021.

[164] The photographs show injuries that are consistent with someone having grabbed Ms. X by her arm and pushing her against a wall, though they are not conclusive.  However, when combined with Ms. X’s evidence, the overall evidence on this charge is compelling.

[165] Ms. Laite denied having grabbed Ms. X in the manner Ms. X described or pushing her against a wall.  She indicated that she did not react violently to Ms. X’s behavior.  However, we know that on one occasion she reacted by punching a wall.

[166] As mentioned earlier, I conclude that Ms. Laite did not always provide honest evidence in describing what she saw or what occurred.  I do not accept Ms. Laite’s testimony as being accurate or truthful and the defence evidence, including Ms. Laite’s evidence, does not cause me to have a reasonable doubt.

[167] Based upon the totality of the evidence presented, I conclude that the Crown has proven beyond a reasonable doubt that on or before December 27, 2021, Ms. Laite grabbed Ms. X and pushed her against a wall.  I am unable to determine an exact number of occasions upon which this occurred, but based upon the evidence presented, I am satisfied that it occurred on several occasions.

[168] The grabbing and pushing of Ms. X by Ms. Laite constitutes the application of force to which Ms. X did not consent.  Accordingly, the Crown has proven that Ms. X assaulted Ms. X, contrary to section 266 of the Criminal Code.

 

Section 267(b) Assault Causing Bodily Harm by Pushing Ms. X Against the Bed:

[169] Ms. X testified that after she and Ms. Laite returned home from the New Year’s Eve party, Ms. Laite pushed her and she fell against the bed in the master bedroom. She described receiving a number of injuries. The photographs she subsequently took, show such injuries.

[170] As I mentioned before, I conclude that the injures depicted in the photographs would have been visible and obvious to anyone living with Ms. X, particularly to someone who was isolating with her alone over several days.  Ms. Laite’s evidence to the contrary was nonsensical and in my view disingenuous.

[171] The evidence presented at the trial establishes that Ms. X was intoxicated on New Years’ Eve.  This is significant evidence because it could affect her ability to provide reliable and accurate evidence.  However, despite being intoxicated, Ms. X described a series of events corroborated by other evidence.  Ms. X recalled for instance, falling to the floor (though in a different manner than Mr. Horan described); she recalled arguing with Ms. X in the drive home and at home; and she recalled the nature of the argument. The accuracy of these portions of her evidence were confirmed by Ms. Laite’s evidence.  In C.E.G., it was pointed out that “an accused’s evidence may be looked at to see if it corroborates aspects of the Crown’s case” (at paragraph 49).

[172] I am satisfied that Ms. X’s alcohol consumption has not prevented her from providing an accurate and reliable description of what occurred in the master bedroom when she and Ms. Laite returned home on December 31, 2021.

[173] I do not accept Ms. Laite’s evidence in relation to this incident to be honest or accurate.  The evidence presented by the defence, including Ms. Laite’s evidence, does not cause me to have a reasonable doubt.  Based upon the totality of the evidence presented, I conclude that the Crown has proven beyond a reasonable doubt that Ms. Laite pushed Ms. X, causing her to fall against the bed, resulting in her receiving the injuries shown in the photographs taken the next day.

[174] In addition, I am satisfied that this caused the rip in the pajama pants Ms. X was wearing. 

[175] The pushing of Ms. X against the bed by Ms. Laite constitutes the application of force to which Ms. X did not consent and which resulted in bodily harm being caused to her.  Accordingly, the Crown has proven that Ms. Laite assaulted Ms. X. and caused her bodily harm, contrary to section 267(b) of the Criminal Code.

Section 267(a), Assault With a Weapon by Throwing Items at Ms. X:

[176] Ms. X testified that Ms. Laite threw items at her, some of which struck her.  Ms. Laite agreed that she threw items, but denied throwing them at Ms. X or that any of them struck her.  

[177] Ms. X’s description of this incident was very general in nature.  Though I have concerns about Ms. Laite’s veracity, the evidence presented in relation to this charge is simply not sufficient to establish beyond a reasonable doubt that Ms. Laite assaulted Ms. X with a weapon by throwing household items at her.  I am not saying that Ms. X provided false or unreliable evidence as regards this incident.  Only that when the evidence is considered in its totality, it is not sufficient for the Crown to have met its burden of proof.

[178] Accordingly, Ms. X is acquitted of the offence of assault with a weapon, contrary to section 267(a) of the Criminal Code.

Section 267(c), Choking Ms. X:

[179] Ms. X testified that she was choked by Ms. X and that Ms. X pushed her head into a pillow on numerous occasions.  Ms. Laite denied that any of these incidents ever occurred, except for the consensual and limited choking that occurred during sexual activity.  Ms. X denied that any choking occurred at that time. 

[180] The evidence in relation to this charge is diametrically opposed.  Though I have concerns about Ms. Laite’s veracity, the evidence presented in relation to this charge is simply not sufficient to establish beyond a reasonable doubt that Ms. Laite choked Ms. X.  Again, I am not saying that Ms. X provided false or unreliable evidence as regards this incident.  Only that when the evidence is considered in its totality, it is not sufficient to prove beyond a reasonable doubt that Ms. Laite committed this offence. 

[181] Accordingly, Ms. X is acquitted of the offence of choking, contrary to section 267(c) of the Criminal Code.

Conclusion:

[182] The Crown has proven beyond a reasonable doubt that Ms. Laite assaulted Ms. X and assaulted her, causing her bodily harm.  It has failed to establish that Ms. Laite assaulted Ms. X with a weapon or that she choked Ms. X while assaulting her.  Accordingly, Ms. Laite is convicted of the offences of assault and assault causing bodily harm, contrary to sections 266 and 267(b) of the Criminal Code.  Ms. Laite is acquitted of the offences of assault with a weapon and the commission of an assault involving choking, contrary to sections 267(a) and 267(c) of the Criminal Code.

[183] Judgment accordingly.

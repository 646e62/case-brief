         STATUTES CONSIDERED: Criminal Code, R.S.C. 1985

 

Summary: Accused sentenced to a blended custodial sentence two months in custody followed by 8 months Conditional Sentence for Sexual Assault, Uttering Threats, Pointing a Firearm, Unlawfully possessing a firearm.

 

 

REASONS FOR JUDGMENT

 

ORR, PCJ:

Introduction

 

[1]            Mr. P.K. was convicted after trial of the following offences: Uttering Threats, Pointing a Firearm, Unlawfully having possession of a firearm, possessing a firearm knowing that he was not the holder of a license to possess it and sexual assault. The issue here is an appropriate sentence for the offences.

 

[2]            The facts briefly are as follows:

 

The first offence in time is alleged to have occurred on Friday the 5th of February 2021. Ms. T.C. testified that she was sexually assaulted by Mr. P.K. at that time. T.C. said that on that evening she was at her home on Indian Meal Line. She and her partner A.S. had invited his sister Ms. S.E. and brother-in-law G. to their home that weekend. They were intending on watching the Super Bowl on the 7th of February.  Mr. P.K. lived across the road from them at 741 Indian Meal Line and was at the time a neighbor and friend. T.C. testified that they often would go to the shed on their property and have social gatherings where they would play music, drink, dance and sing. She said Mr. P.K. had frequently been a visitor and participated in the gatherings. Mr. P.K. arrived that evening and brought some beer with him. T.C. testified that she, her partner, G.E. and Mr. P.K. went into the shed and were playing guitar, singing, drinking and playing music.

 

[3]            T.C. testified that G.E. left the shed, as did all the others. She and Mr. P.K. remained in the shed. She testified that they were listening to music and she was dancing by herself to the music. She asked Mr. P.K. to join her dancing and he initially indicated he would not. He then stood up came over to her and suddenly grabbed her between the legs touching her vagina. She said that she was shocked and went over to the love seat and sat down. She stated he came over to her and kissed her and touched her breast. She stated that she did not consent to this touching.  The touching occurred on the outside of her clothing.

 

[4]            Mr. P.K. agreed with many of the facts as set out by T.C. But at trial he denied that he touched her. He stated that she had invited him to dance and had invited him to engage in sexual acts but that he had refused. He stated that he had consumed eleven bottles of beer and had also consumed some Cannabis. I found his evidence not to be credible as it conflicted with that of the other witnesses and was inconsistent with the phone calls entered as evidence at the trial.

 

[5]            The firearms offences and uttering threats offence relates to his possession of a shotgun. Described in Consent #2 as a Cooey Model 84 12 Gauge single shot shotgun.

 

[6]            On the evening of February 7, Mr. P.K. called A.S. and asked him to come and get his ATV. The ATV was in Mr. P.K.’s garage and Mr. P.K. had been repairing it. He insisted that A.S. come and get it right away. A.S. testified that at the time he was reluctant to do it. He did go and met Mr. P.K. at his house. They went to the garage together.

 

[7]            At the garage, A.S. testified that Mr. P.K. started to shout at him and grabbed a shotgun taking it down off the wall. He pointed the shotgun at A.S’s face and ordered him to get out of the garage. A.S. described the shotgun as a “topper” old and rusted. He said he had seen it in Mr. P.K.’s garage on many occasions and it was always hung on the wall. He said he did not believe it was operable. He took his ATV and left the shed. He immediately went back home and described what had happened to T.C., S.E. and G.E. They all described A.S. as being extremely upset when he entered the home.

 

[8]            The gun is depicted in photographs 22-24 of Consent #4. The gun is quite old and rusted but was determined to be a working firearm. The condition of the gun was very poor as it was missing a piece which was later discovered in the garage when the police searched Mr. P.K.’s property. The piece was described as the forestock and was located beside a snow blower on the floor of the garage.

 

[9]            Mr. P.K. acknowledged that he owned the gun and it was in his garage for a number of years. He said he had found it years previously in a crawl space. He stated he did not think it worked and had never used it. He admitted that he did not have a permit for it and had never applied for one. The gun was not loaded when found by the police and there was no ammunition for it discovered on the property.

 

[10]         In addition to the above facts on the sentence hearing, there was a presentence report presented and a criminal record for Mr. P.K..  A victim impact statement was submitted by A.S., T.C. did not file a victim impact statement.

 

[11]         Counsel are very far apart on what they have submitted is an appropriate sentence for Mr. P.K.. Counsel for the Crown has submitted that a federal term of imprisonment is appropriate. She has asked for a period of 12 months imprisonment for the sexual assault offence, she has asked for a further 20 months for the offence of pointing the firearm and 2 to three months for uttering threats. She has submitted any sentence for the possession of the firearm offences might be made concurrent to each other and has suggested one to two months.

 

[12]          She has relied on the following cases:

 

Sexual Assault: R v Friesen, [2020] SCC 9, R v Jakupaj, [2018] NLSC 93, R v Pettit, [2021] ABQB 773.

 

On the Firearms and Threats Offences: R v Attutuvaa, NUCJ 10, R v King, [2022] CanLii 60917, R v McKinnon, [201]1 ONSC 3160, R v Pumphrey, [1995] CanLii 9834, R v Riggs, [2016] NLTD (G) 214.

 

[13]         Counsel for the accused has submitted that the accused should be sentenced to a community-based sentence. He has asked that the sentence for the sexual assault be suspended. He submits that the accused has no history of sexual offending and the offence itself involved a touching and did not involve any direct skin-to-skin contact. He has asked that with respect to the firearms offences that the accused be sentenced to a conditional period of imprisonment in the range of 8 months, he has relied on the following cases:

 

R v. W.P. [2018] NLSC 113, R v Bhatt, [2021] ABPC 109, R v Carson, [2021] NSPC 109, R v J.P., [2017] NSPC 54, R v Elgeneily, [2021] NSSC 97, R v Henry, [2019] ONSC 4978, R v Berseth, [2019] ONSC 888, R v J.J., [2014] ONSC 2288, R v Burton, [2012] ONSC 5920, R v Rai, [2018] ONCJ 949, R v Abdullahi, 201 YKTC 76, R v Crocker, [1991] CanLii 2737, R v Mercer, [2019] NLSC 230, R v M.T., [2018] NLSC 195, R v Huson, [2009], R v McKinnon, [2011] ONSC 3160, R v Fernandes, [2015] ONCJ 669, R v Jensen, [2022] NLPC, R v Marsh, [2017] NLTD 202, R v Blanchard, NLPC 1318.  

[14]         Sections 718 and following of the Criminal Code set out the principles and purpose of sentencing, denunciation, specific and general deterrence, separation of the offender from society where necessary, rehabilitation, reparation for the harm done to victims or to the community and the promotion of responsibility in offenders.

The fundamental principle is set out in section 718.1. A sentence must be proportionate to the gravity of the offence and the degree of responsibility of the offender. In R. v. Nasogaluak, 2010 SCC 6 (CanLII), [2010] 1 S.C.R. 206, at paragraph 43 the court held:

 

No one sentencing objective trumps the others and it falls to the sentencing judge to determine which objective or objectives merit the greatest weight, given the particulars of the case.

 

[15]         In  R. v. Hutchings, [2012] NLCA 2 (CanLII), [2012] N.J. the Court set out how a sentence should be imposed when imposing sentences for multiple offences on the same sentencing date. At paragraph 84 of that decision, the court sets out a nine-step process that instructs the judge to identify the proper sentence for each offence and then throughout the process adjust the periods of consecutive and concurrent sentences until arriving at a fit sentence for the accused.

 

[16]          In considering the range of sentence for each of the offences starting with the sexual assault offence in R v. Maclean, Mr. Justice Murphy stated at paragraph 30 of his decision that:

 

I believe it is important to recognize, as stated by Goodridge, C.J., that sexual assault encompasses a wide range of human behaviour. The type of conduct that can constitute a sexual assault can range from the touching of the leg of a person in a sexual way to a violent rape. Not surprisingly, therefore the range of sentences for sexual assault is as wide ranging as the scope of human behaviour that can attract criminal liability under the offence of sexual assault.

 

[17]         In sentencing for sexual assault offences in Newfoundland, Courts have followed the decision of the Newfoundland Court of Appeal in R. v. A. (K.J.) (1988), 1988 CanLII 201 (NL CA), 69 Nfld. & P.E.I.R. 99, 211 A.P.R. 99 (Nfld. (C.A.)) in which Goodridge, C.J. identified a number of factors as sentencing guidelines. The following have become known as the "Atkins factors":

 

1)      the extent of the assault...;

 

2)      the degree of violence or force used;

 

3)      the impact of the crime upon the victim, the family of the victim and the offender;

 

4)      the degree of trust involved;

 

5)      public abhorrence to the type of crime involved;

 

6)      the attitude of the offender to what he has done and his plea;

 

7)      the biological and psychiatric factors that lead to the commission of the offence;

8)      the need for specific and general deterrence;

 

9)      the prospect of successful rehabilitation;

10)        the antecedents and age of the offender;

11)        the time spent in custody prior to trial; and

 

12)         sentences imposed by other courts in Newfoundland and elsewhere in Canada.

 

 

Applying this analysis to this case:

The Extent of the Assault

13)            The offence committed against T.C. is at the lower end of the spectrum for sexual assault. It involved touching over the clothing and a kiss.

The Degree of Violence or Force Used

14)            While all sexual assaults are acts of violence, with respect to the offence committed against T.C., the force used was not great and was brief. T.C. did not suffer any bruising or physical injury.

The Impact of the Crime upon the Victim

15)            In this case, the victim did not file an impact statement. However based on the testimony of the victim at trial it was obvious that she was very upset by the assault and that it caused considerable anxiety and disruption in her home.

The Degree of Trust Involved.  

 

16)            This was a situation between two people known to each other. It was not a situation where the accused was in a positon of trust toward the complainant.

 

Public Abhorrence to the Type of Crime Involved.

 

17)            All sexual assaults are serious and the Supreme Court of Canada in Friesen has stated that need to emphasize deterrence in sentencing particularly where the offences involve children.

 The Attitude of the Offender to what he has done and his Plea

18)            As was his right, the Offender entered a plea of not guilty and was convicted following trial. The absence of a guilty plea is not an aggravating factor but neither is it a mitigating one.

The Biological and Psychiatric Factors that Led to the Commission of the Offences

19)            No evidence has been presented to demonstrate the presence of any underlying biological or psychiatric factors.

The Need for Specific and General Deterrence

20)            It cannot be disputed that a conviction for the crime of sexual assault carries with it the need to specifically deter the offender from committing similar offences as well as the need to deter other like-minded persons from committing similar offences.

The Prospect of Successful Rehabilitation

 

21)            The presentence report is quite positive with respect to Mr. P.K. and indicates that he was co-operative with the writer would be a suitable candidate for a community based sentence.

 

22)             In relation to the circumstances of the accused, he is 58 years old and has a chronic medical condition. He suffers from COPD. In a letter supplied for the sentencing hearing it is stated that the accused has been receiving treatment for the illness and is being followed by the Pulmonary Function Lab at the Health Science Centre. His next appointment with them is in March of this year.  Mr. P.K. has been employed throughout his adult life and continues to work when he is able. His COPD condition has limited his ability to work. He is in a long-term relationship and his partner has supported him throughout the trial. He has a criminal record which contains two dated unrelated charges but in 2019 the accused was found guilty and granted a conditional discharge for assault with a weapon and mischief.

 

23)            A review of the cases submitted by counsel for the sexual assault offence sets out a sentence range from a discharge (R V Henry 2019 ONSC 4978, R v Berseth 2019 ONSC 888) to lengthy terms of imprisonment in the range of twenty months (R v Jakupaj 2018 NLSC 93.) I would note that in this jurisdiction, discharges or suspended sentence dispositions for sexual assault convictions are rarely imposed. In this case, the accused does not have the benefit of a guilty plea. On the mitigating side, the accused was not in a positon of trust and the victim was an adult and not a child. Unlike in Juakupaj supra, or R v Carson, [2021] NSPC he was not either the victim’s employer or a taxi driver or other service provider. The accused did exploit his friendship with the victim and assaulted her in her home. He has a prior finding of guilt for assault as a result a custodial sentence is appropriate in the circumstances. A conditional sentence is not available to the accused for this offence. On balancing the mitigating and aggravating factors, I find that a sentence of two months is appropriate.

 

24)            In relation to the offence of uttering threats in  R. v. Lyver, 2010 CanLII 11910 (NL PC), [2010] N.J. No. 92 (N.L.P.C.), Gorman, J. considered a large number of cases and held with respect to the range of sentence for an offence under section 264.1(1)(a) of the Criminal Code. At paragraph 41:

 

41 For the offence of uttering threats, not involving an intimate relationship, the range of sentence extends from a conditional discharge (see R. v. L.H.B. (2006), 2006 NLTD 87 (CanLII), 257 Nfld. & P.E.I.R. 307 (N.L.S.C.)) to three years imprisonment (see R. v. Janes (1999), 1999 CanLII 18925 (NL CA), 170 Nfld. & P.E.I.R. 269 (N.L.C.A.)). Periods of imprisonment of fourteen days (see R. v. Lewis (2002), 2002 CanLII 54024 (NL SC), 212 Nfld. & P.E.I.R. 145 (N.L.S.C.)); one month (see R. v. H.P. (1995), 1995 CanLII 9875 (NL CA), 133 Nfld. & P.E.I.R. 20 (N.L.C.A.) and R. v. Ryan (2006), 2006 NLTD 167 (CanLII), 261 Nfld. & P.E.I.R. 326 (N.L.S.C.)); two months (see R. v. Sheppard (2003), 2003 NLCA 2 (CanLII), 220 Nfld. & P.E.I.R. 299 (N.L.C.A.); R. v. Savchenko (2000), 2000 CanLII 28797 (NL SC), 191 Nfld. & P.E.I.R. 225 (N.L.S.C.) and R. v. Stacey, [2010] N.J. No. 55 (P.C.)); three months (see R. v. Cormier (2005), 2005 NLTD 35 (CanLII), 245 Nfld.& P.E.I.R. 271 (N.L.S.C.); R. v. Rowbottom, [2006] N.J. No. 31 (P.C.) and R. v. Eveleigh, [2007] N.J. No. 411 (P.C.)); four months (see R. v. Pumphrey (1995), 1995 CanLII 9834 (NL CA), 127 Nfld. & P.E.I.R. 286 (N.L.C.A.)); and six months (see R. v. J.R.B., [2004] N.J. No. 101 (P.C.) and R. v. Hunt (2007), 2007 NLTD 204 (CanLII), 273 Nfld. & P.E.I.R. 308 (N.L.S.C.)) have been imposed.

 

25)            It would appear as a result that the range is quite broad but in most instances a sentence in the range of two to three months is imposed where the accused has uttered a threat while brandishing a weapon.

 

26)            In respect of the offence of pointing a firearm, the maximum sentence for the offence is 5 years. In reviewing the case law, the range of sentence is from a fine (R V Coughlan [1982] NSJ No.148. To a lengthy term of imprisonment R V Riggs, [2016] NLTD where the offender was sentenced to 15 months imprisonment as he had pointed and held a loaded shotgun to the neck of his partner. Some of the aggravating factors that courts have looked at is whether or not the gun was loaded (R V Mckinnon 2011 ONSC 3160) or if the weapon was actually discharged at some point (R v Attutvaa 2013 NUCJ, R V King 2022 NLPC 132200242 and R V Riggs supra).

 

27)            In this case, the accused had not loaded the firearm and did not believe that it would actually fire. In addition, the victim did not think the weapon was functional. However, given the context in which this occurred it was the intention of the accused to frighten Mr. Stacey by pointing the gun at him to embellish the threats he was making. I find that a sentence of four months for pointing the firearm and three months for uttering threats is appropriate. In all the circumstances, I find that an additional one-month consecutive for the unauthorized possession of the firearm is appropriate. On the charge of possession of a weapon while not being the holder of a license I will enter a stay.

 

28)            Counsel for the accused has asked that the accused be granted a conditional sentence for the offences of uttering threats and pointing and possessing the firearm.

 

29)            The  Newfoundland Court of Appeal has held on several occasions that the nature of a particular offence not specifically listed as unavailable by Parliament does not exclude it from the imposition of a conditional sentence as has the Supreme Court of Canada in R. v. Proulx, [2000] S.C.J. No. 6, 2000 SCC 5.

 

30)             In R v. C.M., [1998] N.J. No. 209, [Docket: 98/41] Mr. Justice Marshall stated at page 15:

 

"Neither is the contention tenable that the conditional sentence does not fulfil the essential purpose of maintaining public respect for the criminal justice system. As a general proposition this aspect of the Crown's argument appears to be a reiteration of its stance, rejected in R v. W. (L.F.), 1997 CanLII 10868 (NL CA), [1997] N.J. No. 234, that crimes of this nature merit incarceration. Once more, the Crown appears to be insisting that no punishment short of incarceration would be a just sanction for these types of crimes. This time he advances this premise by arguing that public confidence in the justice system will be impaired if this approach is adopted. As was noted in R. v. L. (W.F.) however no offences were excluded from a consideration for conditional sentencing, nor were more rigorous standards imposed for some as opposed to others."

31)            In R v. Proulx, supra, Chief Justice Lamer stated at page 46:

 

"Thus, a conditional sentence can achieve both punitive and restorative objectives. To the extent that both the punitive and the restorative objectives can be achieved in a given case, a conditional sentence is likely a better sanction than incarceration. Where the need for punishment is particularly pressing, and there is little opportunity to achieve any restorative objectives, incarceration will likely be the more attractive sanction. However, even where restorative objectives cannot be readily justified, a conditional sentence will be preferable to incarceration in cases where a conditional sentence can achieve the objectives of denunciation and deterrence as effectively as incarceration. This follows from the principle of restraint in section 718.2(d) and (e), which militates in favour of alternatives to incarceration where appropriate in the circumstances."

 

32)            In this case I am satisfied that the sentence to be imposed for the offences is less than two years. There are no mandatory minimum sentences in effect and I am satisfied that the accused will not pose a danger to the community. In R. v. Ploumis (2000) 150 C.C.C. 3(d) 424 the Ontario Court of Appeal held that a custodial sentence and a conditional sentence could be imposed at the same time:

 

“In R. (R.A.), the Supreme Court blended a custodial sentence for one offence with conditional sentences for two others where the total sentence did not exceed two years less one day. Had the majority been of the view that blending the two forms of sentence in these circumstances contravened either the spirit or letter of s. 742.1 of the Code, I believe they would have said so. More to the point, they would not have imposed an illegal sentence.”

33)             I will as a result impose a blended sentence. The accused will be sentenced to two months in custody for the offence of sexual assault. He will be sentenced to a total of eight months conditionally for the offences of pointing a firearm, uttering threats and possession of a firearm without a license. Following the two periods of imprisonment, the accused will be on probation for two years and will be bound by the following orders:

1)    An order under the Sex Offender Information Registration Act pursuant to section  490.013

 

2)    On order that he provide a sample of  his DNA pursuant to section 487.051(1)

 

3)    A Prohibition Order pursuant to section 109(1) prohibiting him from possessing any firearms or any of the items listed therein for a period of ten years

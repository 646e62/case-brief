Nature of the Proceedings

[1]               The Accused is charged with three counts of sexual assault, all allegedly arising during the Complainant’s work shift at a local fast-food restaurant, June 20th, 2020.  The Complainant was at that time designated a Supervisor Level 1 and the Accused who was also employed by the same restaurant was designated a Supervisor Level 3.  On the day in question during the shift 8:00 a.m. to 4:00 p.m. the Accused was the primary supervisor at the restaurant.  Essentially, I believe, in charge of the restaurant operation during that shift.

[2]               The alleged sexual assaults involve a slap on the Complainant’s buttocks by the Accused as he walked by her at work, a short time after that an alleged groping of the Complainant by the Accused whereby he allegedly reached under her blouse and touched her breast.  A video recording of both of these alleged incidents were admitted into evidence as Exhibit #2 and Exhibit #1 respectively.

[3]               The third allegation relates to an incident in the storage shed which is a separate building from the restaurant and in which the Accused is alleged to have touched and sucked the Complainant’s breast[s] and digitally penetrated her vagina.

[4]               The Crown asserts these three incidents to be sexual assault, that is the non-consensual application of force to the Complainant by the Accused in circumstances of a sexual nature such that the sexual integrity of the Complainant was violated.

[5]               The defence asserts that neither of the first two touching as described aforesaid were of a sexual nature.  The Accused asserts that the slap on the buttocks was just playful activity between them, as was the touching at the till which did not include, he asserts, the touching of her breasts, but only her upper chest area, and that the Complainant’s evidence in that regard in any event is not reliable on that point nor does the video clip support her allegation.

[6]               With respect to the third allegation of touching in the shed, the Defence asserts that her conduct, actions and words in the context of her interaction with the Accused demonstrate consent on her part, or raise a reasonable doubt as to the absence of consent to the touching described in the evidence.

[7]               The Crown asserts that if the Court concludes that there was consent or is left with a reasonable doubt as to absence of consent that in any event, the Accused induced the Complainant to engage in the subject activity by abusing a position of trust, power or authority, that being his supervisory role at the restaurant, which the Crown argues pursuant to s273.1(2)(c), vitiates the voluntariness of the Complainant’s consent thereby demonstrating the Complainant’s absence of consent in law.

Legal Framework of Sexual Assault

[8]               In the Supreme Court of Canada decision R v JA, 2011 SCC 28, Chief Justice McLachlin, for the majority of the Court, describes the offence of sexual assault at paras 23, 24 and 25 of that judgment:

            23        A conviction for sexual assault under s.271(1) of the Criminal Code requires proof beyond a reasonable doubt of the actus reus and the mens rea of the offence.  A person commits the actus reus if he touches another person in a sexual way without her consent.  Consent for this purpose is actual subjective consent in the mind of the complainant at the time of the sexual activity in question: Ewanchuk.  As discussed below, the Criminal Code, s.273.1(2), limits this definition by stipulating circumstances where consent is not obtained.

 

24        A person has the required mental state, or mens rea of the offence, when he or she knew that the complainant was not consenting to the sexual act in question, or was reckless or wilfully blind to the absence of consent.  The accused may raise the defence of honest but mistaken belief in consent if he believed that the complainant communicated consent to engage in the sexual activity.  However, as discussed below, ss.273.1(2) and 273.2 limit the cases in which the accused may rely on this defence.  For instance, the accused cannot argue that he misinterpreted the complainant saying “no” as meaning “yes” (Ewanchuk, at para.51).

 

25        The issue in this case is whether the complainant consented, which is relevant to the actus reus; the Crown must prove the absence of consent to fulfill the requirements of the wrongful act.

[9]               In R v Ewanchuk, 1999 SCC 711, (referenced in R v JA), Major J, speaking for the Court at paras 25-26, stated that the actus reus of sexual assault is established by proof of three elements: 

(i)         touching,

            (ii)        the sexual nature of the contact, and

            (iii)      the absence of consent.

 

The first two of these elements are objective in nature and may be proven by establishing on the

evidence that the accused’s actions were voluntary.  The “absence of consent” is purely

subjective and is determined by reference to the complainant’s internal state of mind with respect

to the touching as at the time it purportedly occurred.

[10]           Despite the fact that the absence of consent is purely subjective, it is open to the accused to claim that the complainant’s words and actions in the context of the incident raise a reasonable doubt against her assertion that she, in her mind, did not want the sexual touching to take place.  At paragraph 27 of Ewanchuk, Major, J explained:

...For the purpose of determining the absence of consent as an element of the actus reus, the actual state of mind of the complainant is determinant.  At this point the trier of fact is only concerned with the complainant’s perspective, the approach is purely subjective.

[11]           At paragraph 31, Major J puts the matter into further perspective saying:

...If the trier of fact accepts the complainant’s testimony that she did not consent no matter how strongly her conduct may contradict that claim, the absence of consent is established and the third component of the actus reus of sexual assault is proven.

[12]           However, the credibility of the complainant as to the assertion that she did not want the sexual touching to take place is subject to being assessed in light of all the evidence before the Court.  At paragraph 29 and 30 of Ewanchuk, Major J states:

While a complainant’s testimony is the only source of direct evidence as to her state of mind, credibility must still be assessed by the trial judge, or a jury, in light of all the evidence.  It is open to the accused to claim that the claimant’s words and actions, before and during the incident raise a reasonable doubt against her assertion that she, in her mind, did not want the sexual touching to take place.  If, however, as occurred in this case, the trial judge believes the complainant that she subjectively did not consent, the Crown has discharged its obligation to prove the absence of consent.

The complainant’s statement that she did not consent is a matter of credibility to be weighed in light of all the evidence, including any ambiguous conduct.  The question at this stage is purely one of credibility, and whether the totality of the complainant’s conduct is consistent with her claim of non-consent.  The accused’s perception of the complainant’s state of mind is not relevant.  That perception only arises when the defence of honest and mistaken belief in consent is raised in the mens rea stage of the inquiry.

[13]           In this regard one must remember that the question is the state of the Complainant’s mind at the time of the contact, not before or after.  The actus reus has been committed if the Complainant was not consenting in her mind when the touching took place, even if she expressed her consent before or after; R v JA, at para 46.  However, in considering the issue of the credibility of the Complainant as to assertion of non-consent at the material time, the Court may consider what the Complainant did leading up to, during or following the sexual interaction.

Proof Beyond a Reasonable Doubt and Credibility

[14]           The Crown has the burden of satisfying this Court beyond a reasonable doubt that the accused sexually assaulted the complainant; that is the Court must be satisfied that all the elements of sexual assault (s271 of the Criminal Code) have been proven beyond a reasonable doubt.

[15]           Proof beyond a reasonable doubt is not proof to the level of certitude; such a level may not be attainable even in scientific matters, let alone human affairs. Proof beyond a reasonable doubt is, however, proof to near certitude. It is much closer to the level of complete certainty than to the level of probability; R v Starr, 2000 SCC 40 at para 242.

[16]           In R v Lifchus, 1997 CanLII 319 (SCC), [1997] 3 SCR 320 at para 36, Cory J explained the essential elements of the principle of reasonable doubt as follows:

It should be explained that:

•         That standard of proof beyond a reasonable doubt is inextricably intertwined with that principle fundamental to all criminal trials, the presumption of innocence;

•         The burden of proof rests on the prosecution throughout the trial and never shifts to the accused;

•         Reasonable doubt is not a doubt based upon sympathy or prejudice;

•         Rather it is based upon reason and common sense;

•         It is logically connected to evidence or absence of evidence; it does not involve proof to an absolute certainty, it is not proof beyond any doubt, nor is it an imaginary or frivolous doubt; and

•         More is required than proof that the accused is probably guilty – a jury which concludes only that the accused is probably guilty must acquit.

[17]           In the recent decision of the Supreme Court of Canada, R v Villaroman, 2016 SCC 33, in discussing the principle of reasonable doubt, the Court, at para 28, comments as follows:

The reasonable doubt instruction describes a state of mind — the degree of persuasion that entitles and requires a juror to find an accused guilty: Berger, at p. 60. Reasonable doubt is not an inference or a finding of fact that needs support in the evidence presented at trial: see, e.g. R. v. Schuldt, 1985 CanLII 20 (SCC), [1985] 2 S.C.R. 592 (S.C.C.), at pp. 600-610. A reasonable doubt is a doubt based on "reason and common sense"; it is not "imaginary or frivolous"; it "does not involve proof to an absolute certainty"; and it is "logically connected to the evidence or absence of evidence”: Lifchus, at para. 36. The reasonable doubt instructions are all directed to describing for the jurors how sure they must be of guilt in order to convict.

[18]           At the end of the day, based upon the totality of the evidence, if the Court is satisfied beyond a reasonable doubt that the accused committed the alleged offence, meaning that the Crown has proven beyond a reasonable doubt all the necessary elements of the offence, the Court must find the accused guilty.  If the Court concludes on the evidence or lack thereof when considering it as a whole, only that there is a strong suspicion of guilt or that the accused is only probably guilty or likely guilty, that is not enough and the Court must acquit.

[19]           In circumstances where the assessment of credibility is essential, the issue of credibility is subject to the rule of reasonable doubt and the Crown’s onus with respect to proof beyond a reasonable doubt.  Accordingly, where different versions of the events are presented, it is not a matter of relegating the issue to one of simply determining which version the Court prefers.  To follow that approach would mean that the accused would have the onus of proving his or her innocence by presenting a more believable version of events.  That is, of course, not the accused’s obligation.  It is not a credibility contest between the complainant and the accused as to who has the best or more likely version of events.  It is whether the Crown has proven the case beyond a reasonable doubt (See: R v JHS, 2008 SCC 30 at paras 8-13 (JHS); R v CYL, 2008 SCC 2 at para 6).   It is possible that the trier of fact may well prefer the complainant’s narrative to that offered by the accused, but that does not resolve the issue of reasonable doubt as to guilt.  A preference alone is not sufficient to find guilt. 

[20]           In this context, however, it is important to note that although the issue of proof beyond a reasonable doubt cannot be determined by a credibility contest as described aforesaid, the Court may, in circumstances where the accused is not disbelieved in the sense that there is no reason to disbelieve the testimony such as internal inconsistencies or the manner in which the evidence was given or from comparison to other creditworthy testimony, the Court may nonetheless convict the accused despite the accused’s testimony not being disbelieved.  This may occur as described by Doherty JA in R v JJRD, 2006 CanLII 40088 (ON CA), 2006 OJ No 4749, at para 53 (CA):

... an outright rejection of an accused’s evidence based on a considered and reasoned acceptance beyond a reasonable doubt of the truth of conflicting credible evidence is as much an explanation for the rejection of an accused’s evidence as is a rejection based on a problem identified with the way the accused testified or the substance of the accused’s evidence.

[21]           If that is to occur, however, the Court may only explain a bare rejection of the accused’s testimony by demonstrating a “considered and reasoned” acceptance of the complainant’s evidence.  It is not enough to make a bare acceptance of the complainant’s evidence and then reject the accused’s bare denial because of that acceptance.  That cannot amount to sufficient reasons for rejection (see:  R v RD, 2016 ONCA 574 at para 18 (RD)).  Normally, to reach that conclusion when the accused denies the allegations there is something more in the evidence which allows the court to accept the testimony of the complainant beyond a reasonable doubt.  For example, in RD there was the evidence of the complainant and evidence arising from a diary which enhanced the value of the complainant’s testimony and when stacked up against the accused’s evidence, despite the absence of anything undermining the accused’s testimony, nonetheless satisfied the Court beyond a reasonable doubt as to the offence being committed.  In R v Slatter, 2019 ONCA 807, there was a piece of evidence about what happened between the accused and complainant at a barbecue that undermined the accused’s evidence on that point and that, according to the dissenting justice, undermined his overall credibility, and the dissenting judge, based upon the strength of the complainant’s testimony and disbelief of the accused for the reasons described, concluded that the Crown had proven the case beyond a reasonable doubt despite denial by the accused.  This dissenting version was accepted by the Supreme Court of Canada on appeal in R v Slatter, 2020 SCC 36.

[22]           The credibility of a witness is in essence the believability of a witness, and in part depends upon the assessment of truthfulness of the witness based upon his or her actions and conduct, credible or incredible as they may be.  Credibility is also based upon the reliability of the witness’s testimony having regard to a number of considerations including:

1.         the witness’s opportunity to perceive;

2.         his or her actual perception;

3.         the witness’s ability to recall and communicate to the Court;

4.         the stress that the particular witness may have been under at the time;

5.         the witness’s demeanour and the manner of testifying, and

6.         whether the witness has an interest in the outcome of the proceedings.

[23]           In considering the issue of credibility and the factors mentioned above, I am entitled to apply common sense and human experience in determining whether the evidence is credible and deciding what use, if any, I will make of it in coming to my conclusion.  At the end of that process, I am entitled to believe the whole or part of the testimony of any witness; disbelieve the whole or part of the testimony of any witness; and to weigh the individual value of the testimony. 

[24]           The testimony of a witness must be weighed carefully and considered in light of many factors, including the internal consistency or inconsistency of the testimony itself, as well as the consistency or inconsistency of the testimony when measured against evidence over which there is no real dispute.  That is, how does the testimony of the witness dovetail or fit with the facts that have been clearly established on the evidence?

[25]           Determining credibility is an issue of fact and it is a task that cannot be fulfilled by following any specific set of rules.  No witness is entitled to greater credibility because of who or what they are.  There is no hierarchy of witness credibility.  It is only after the individual’s evidence has been tested and assessed that a finding of credibility can be made, if at all.  The general trustworthiness or believability of the witness, the personal circumstances of the witness, the capacity to properly interpret what they are seeing, hearing or experiencing, and the memory and ability to communicate, and any circumstances which might cause an individual to choose to offer inaccurate information, are all matters that may be considered. 

[26]           If the evidence of a witness is seen as implausible or incredible and therefore unreliable, it will carry no weight in the case.  The acceptance of evidence only means that the trier of fact is prepared to give to it some weight in assessing the issue that must be resolved.  A weakness in the credibility of a witness’s evidence is a plausible reason for concluding that a reasonable doubt remains when the whole of the evidence is considered.  The Court does not have to conclude that a witness is lying or unreliable to reject or place little weight upon the testimony thereof.  If there are indications that a particular testimony may not be accurate, the evidence of that witness should be approached with great caution or given no weight at all in the determination of the issue before the Court.

[27]           The relationship between the rule of reasonable doubt and the issue of credibility in a criminal case is explained by Cory J in the Supreme Court of Canada decision R v W(D), 1991 CanLII 93 (SCC), [1991] 1 SCR 742 (also cited as DW v Her Majesty the Queen) here at pp 757-58 he explains:

Ideally, appropriate instructions on the issue of credibility should be given, not only during the main charge but on any re-charge.  A trial judge might well instruct a jury on the charge of credibility along these lines:

First, if you believe the evidence of the accused, obviously you must acquit. 

Second, if you do not believe the testimony of the accused, but you are left in reasonable doubt by it, you must acquit. 

Third, even if you are not left in doubt by the evidence of the accused, you must ask yourself whether, on the basis of the evidence which you do accept, you are convinced beyond a reasonable doubt by that evidence of the guilt of the accused.

[28]           In the B.C. Court of Appeal case of R v CWH (1991), 1991 CanLII 3956 (BC CA), 68 CCC (3d) 146 (BCCA), Wood JA suggested an additional instruction be added to Cory J’s direction:

I would add one more instruction in such cases, which logically ought to be second in the order, namely: “If, after careful consideration of all the evidence you are unable to decide whom to believe, you must acquit.” [p.155]

This added instruction appears to meet the approval of Binnie J in JHS, at para 12.

[29]           In JHS, Binnie J rationalized the second stage of W(D) as follows:

As to the second question, some jurors may wonder how come, if they believe none of the evidence of the accused, such rejected evidence may nonetheless of itself raise a reasonable doubt.  Of course, some elements of the evidence of an accused may raise a reasonable doubt even though the bulk of it is rejected.  Equally, the jury may simply conclude that they do not know whether to believe the accused’s testimony or not.  In either circumstance, the accused is entitled to an acquittal.  

[30]           If I am reasonably left unsure that the exculpatory statement or exculpatory version offered by the Accused is an accurate rendition of what happened, then I have a reasonable doubt about whether the allegation made by the Crown is true.  As Justice Blair stated in R v BD, 2011 ONCA 51 at para 114:

The trial judge must...[make] it clear to the jurors that it is not necessary for them to believe the defence evidence on a vital issue; rather it is sufficient if – viewed in the context of all of the evidence – the conflicting evidence leaves them in a state of reasonable doubt as to the accused’s guilt…If so, they must acquit.

[31]           There has been much discussion and debate about the meaning and application of the W(D) approach to credibility and reasonable doubt.  In a 2017 law review publication entitled; Doubt About Doubt, Coping with R v W(D) and Credibility Assessment (2017), 22 CANL Rev 31, Justice David M. Paciocco (as he then was) summarized the W(D) formulization as follows:

1.      Criminal trials cannot properly be resolved by deciding which conflicting version of events is preferred;

2.      A criminal fact finder that believes evidence that is inconsistent with the guilt of the accused cannot convict the accused;

3.      Even if a criminal fact finder does not entirely believe evidence inconsistent with guilt, if the fact finder cannot decide whether that evidence is true, there is a reasonable doubt and an acquittal must follow;

4.      For even where the fact finder entirely disbelieves evidence inconsistent with guilt, the mere rejection of that evidence does not prove guilt;

5.      Even when the fact finder entirely disbelieves evidence inconsistent with guilt, the accused should not be convicted unless the evidence that is given credit proves the accused guilty beyond a reasonable doubt.

[32]           In that vein, the fact that the trial judge may believe the complainant to be reliable does not mean that the accused must provide some explanation for the complainant’s allegation. To require an explanation would be to shift the onus to the accused which is prohibited.  This is explained by Hamilton J in R v Liberatore, 2010 NSCA 82 at para12, which articulates the principle as follows:

12        W.(D.) prohibits rejecting the appellant’s evidence solely because it is inconsistent with the Crown’s evidence, which he prefers, and making a finding of guilt without further consideration of reasonable doubt in light of the whole of the evidence – thus treating the standard of proof as a simple credibility contest.

[33]           In R v YV, 2010 ONCA 619 at para 26, the Ontario Court of Appeal makes the point as well:

26        ...A finding that the complainant was credible did not mean that the onus shifted to the appellant to show that he was not guilty.  In other words, the appellant did not have an obligation to put forward a theory that would explain why the complainant would make a false complaint.

[34]           In the Alberta decision of R v Ryon, 2017 ABCA 36 at para 51, as further refined in R v Achuil, 2019 ABCA 299 at para 18, and as reiterated in R v SMC, 2020 ABCA 19 at para 24, the court proposed a four-point formula outlining how the principles set out by Cory J in W(D) ought to be applied;

i.        The burden of proof is on the Crown to establish the accused’s guilt beyond a reasonable doubt and that burden remains on the Crown so that the accused person is never required to prove his innocence, or disprove any of the evidence led by the Crown (subject to the caveat that this does not apply to defences, such as that found in s16 of the Criminal Code where the onus rests with the proponent of the defence).

ii.      In that context, if the accused’s evidence denying complicity or guilt (or any other exculpatory evidence to that effect) is believed, or even if not believed still leaves the jury with a reasonable doubt that it may be true, then the jury is required to acquit (again subject to defences with additional elements such as an objective component);

iii.   While the jury should attempt to resolve conflicting evidence bearing on the guilt or innocence of the accused, a trial is not a credibility context requiring them to decide that one of the conflicting versions is true.  If, after careful consideration of all the evidence, the jury is unable to decide whom to believe, they must acquit; and

iv.   Even if the jury completely rejects the accused’s evidence (or where applicable, other exculpatory evidence), they may not simply assume the Crown’s version of events must be true.  Rather, they must carefully assess the evidence they do believe and decide whether that evidence persuades them beyond a reasonable doubt that the accused is guilty.  Mere rejection of the accused’s evidence (or where applicable, other exculpatory evidence) cannot be taken as proof of the accused’s guilt.

[35]           Further, with respect to credibility determination, even if the events on a multi-count information are found to be separate events as opposed to arising from the same event, the Court may apply any credibility finding with respect to a witness made with respect to one count or event, in deciding whether to believe what that witness says about a different event charged in the same information or indictment; R v PEC, 2005 SCC 19.  A finding of non-creditworthiness or reliability or both with respect to testimony given as to one of the events before the Court could lead the Court to conclude on that basis that the witness’ testimony with respect to another charge or event before the Court is not credible or reliable either.

Facts and Analysis

[36]           As noted in this proceeding there are three allegations of sexual assault involving the same Complainant and Accused.  It is clear that there was touching of the Complainant by the Accused in all three circumstances, but the circumstances of each touching vary.  These events occurred over the course of a few hours on the 20th of June, 2020, and are essentially separate incidents, although the first two incidents are, according to Accused, linked together contextually.  The last one in time was clearly sexual in nature and the issue turns on the absence of consent and given the different versions presented by the Complainant and Accused in their respective testimonies, a credibility analysis is fundamental to determination as to whether the Crown has proven the absence of consent beyond a reasonable doubt.  With respect to the first two events, which were separated by minutes, both raise the issue as to whether the touching of the Complainant by the Accused is an assault of a sexual nature, that is, a non-consensual touching that undermines the sexual integrity of the Complainant.  If the touching was not of a sexual nature, the question is whether it was a non-consensual application of force so as to amount to an assault simpliciter.

[37]           The first allegation in time involves a slapping of the Complainant’s buttocks by the Accused.  The Accused acknowledges this form of touching and the video evidence, Exhibit #2 shows the actual happening. 

[38]           With respect to the second event, the testimonies of the Complainant and the Accused differ substantially.  The Complainant asserts the Accused touched her breasts under her shirt.  The Accused states that he did touch her on her upper chest area, not her breasts, outside her clothing as part of horseplay between the two of them.

[39]           The Accused’s asserts that the Complainant had earlier that day slapped his butt and that there had been a discussion between the two of them to the effect that, “whatever she does to him, he will do to her”.

[40]           The Accused asserts that these events were simply part of that friendly horseplay: she had slapped him on the butt, he slapped her, minutes thereafter she put her hands on his upper back and chest area which involved placing of her hands upon him two or three times in quick succession followed by him touching her upper chest area, not her breasts, in response after which they again went about their daily duties.

[41]           The video, Exhibit #1, shows her approach him, touch him as described, and the Accused then stepped close to her as she moved a few steps away to the till location where he stood for a few seconds beside her.  The video does not show him actually touching her with his hands.

[42]           The first two events as described aforesaid are separated only by minutes and have a foundation, according to the Accused, that is based upon friendly horseplay between the Complainant and the Accused.

[43]           The third event occurs later outside in the restaurant storage shed has nothing to do with horseplay, and the Accused does not rely upon or assert that event to be an instance of the horseplay between the two; that is, although he asserts that it was consensual on the part of the Complainant, he does not suggest that consent is premised on the horseplay touching as he describes relative to the first two events.

[44]           On the date of the alleged offences, June 20th, 2020, the Complainant and the Accused were both employed by a local fast-food restaurant in the City of Lethbridge, the Complainant held a Supervisor 1 status, and the Accused a Supervisor 3 status that placed him above the Complainant in the staff hierarchy.  The differences between the two in terms of authority or power while on the job was not really explored other than to establish that he was above her in rank, so to speak.  There is no suggestion that he had any control or influence over hiring or firing, promotion or demotion.  It would appear he was in charge on the day in question of the restaurant running according to protocol.  There was no suggestion that he was part of actual management.  It appears that his job, despite his supervisory level, included servicing customers, making up and receiving orders and indeed, cleaning floors and equipment.

[45]           On the day in question the Complainant and Accused were either working the same shift or their shifts overlapped.  It is clear from their respective testimonies that they were friends at work and that there had been some history of friendly horseplay between them at work which the Complainant acknowledged, including non-sexual touching.  The Accused asserts in his testimony that the Complainant had slapped him on the bottom and he had reciprocated at some point shortly thereafter.  He testified he indicated to her that what she did to him, he would do to her and she agreed.  The evidence was somewhat confusing about whether he slapped her bottom twice including the event shown in video Exhibit #1 or only the once that day.  The Complainant does not deny that she slapped the Accused’s bottom earlier that day, but says she does not recall.  Neither does she deny the discussion concerning “what she does, he does back”,, although she does not recall that conversation either.

[69]           The first and second touchings, and the circumstances in which they occurred, raise the issue as to whether either or both were sexual in nature.  The seminal case with respect to the distinction between assault simpliciter and sexual assault is the Supreme Court of Canada decision in R v Chase, 1987 CanLII 23 (SCC), [1987] SCJ No 57 (Chase), where speaking for the majority of the Court, McIntyre J stated at para 11;

Sexual assault is an assault ... which is committed in circumstances of a sexual nature, such that the sexual integrity of the victim is violated.  The test to be applied in determining whether the impugned conduct has the requisite sexual nature is an objective one: “viewed in light of all the circumstances, is the sexual or carnal context of the assault visible to a reasonable observer?” ... The part of the body touched, the nature of the contact, the situation in which it occurred, the words and gestures accompanying the act, and all other circumstances surrounding the conduct including the threats which may or may not be accompanied by force will be relevant ... The intent or purpose of the person committing the act, to the extent that this may appear from the evidence, may also be a factor in considering whether the conduct is sexual.  If the motive of the accused is sexual gratification it may be a factor in determining whether the conduct is sexual.  It must be emphasized however that the existence of such a motive is simply one of many factors to be considered the importance of which will vary depending on the circumstances.

[70]           The question as to whether a touching is sexual or non-sexual is a question of fact; the determination of which requires the court to apply the objective test set out in Chase as to whether the sexual context of the assault is visible to a reasonable observer.  In making a decision in that regard the court should consider, the part of the body touched, the nature of the contact, the situation in which the contact occurred, the words and gestures that accompany the contact, the intent or purpose of the assault, the motive of the accused especially whether it is sexual gratification and all other circumstances surrounding the conduct including any threats; R v Litchfield, [1993] 2 SCR 333, R v KBV, 1993 CanLII 109 (SCC), [1993] 2 SCR 857, R v Higginbottom (2001), 2001 CanLII 3989 (ON CA), 156 CCC (3d) 178 (ONCA).

Vitiation of Consent

[98]           The Crown asserts in the alternative that s273.1(2)(c) has application in this circumstance.  It is argued that the Accused was a person in authority or power over the Complainant given his supervisory role in the restaurant on the day in question, and that her ostensible consent or participation in the sexual activity is vitiated thereby.  

[99]           In R v Hutchinson, 2014 SCC 19, the Supreme Court outlined that the Criminal Code sets out a two-step process for analyzing consent to sexual activity.  First, the court must consider whether there was no voluntary agreement of the complainant to engage in the sexual activity in question.  If the Complainant consented, or her/his conduct and actions raises a reasonable doubt as to lack of voluntary agreement to the sexual activity, the court must then consider ss265(3)(d) and 273.1(2)(c) as to whether there are any circumstances that may vitiate the complainant’s ostensible consent or participation.

[100]      The Crown in this case specifically relied upon s273.1(2)(c) as the basis of vitiation of her ostensible consent.  The Crown neither raised nor made argument about whether s265(3)(d) has any application.  Section 273.1 provides:

1.      Subject to s265(3), “consent” means, for the purposes of s271, 272 and 273, the voluntary agreement of the complainant to engage in the sexual activity in question.

2.      For the purposes of subsection (1), no consent is obtained if ... (c) the accused induces the complainant to engage in the activity by abusing a position of trust, power or authority ...

[101]      The related provision set out in s265(3)(d) provides:

(3)        for the purposes of this section, no consent is obtained where the complainant submits or does not resist by reason of ...

            (d)      the exercise of authority.

[102]      Section 265(3)(d) vitiates consent when there is a relationship of authority between the complainant and accused, and the complainant’s consent results from the exercise of the accused’s authority; Sexual Offences of Canadian Law, Hamish Stewart, Thomson Reuters Canada, para 3.300.20.40.  In R v Lutoslawski, 2010 ONCA 207, Doherty JA described the distinction between ss265(3)(d) and 273.1(2)(c), stating:

Section 273.1(2)(c) speaks not only to the abuse of a position of authority but also to the misuse of a position of power or trust.  The section addresses the kinds of relationships in which an apparent consent to sexual activity is rendered illusory by the dynamics of the relationship between the accused and the complainant, and by the misuse of the influence vested in the accused by virtue of that relationship.  The term “exercise of authority” in s265(3)(d) suggests a coercive use of authority to overcome resistance to a consent.  Inducing consent by abusing the relationship set out in 273.1(2)(c) does not imply the same kind of coercion.  An individual who is in a position of trust over another may use the personal feelings and confidence engendered by that relationship to secure apparent consent to sexual activity.

[103]      In R v Geddes, 2015 ONCA 292, the court commented that the “exercise of influence to the point of coercion” constitutes “the exercise of authority” for the purposes of s265(3)(d) (at para 35).

[104]      In R v Snelgrove, 2018 NLCA 59 at para 23, the court, in reference to s265(3)(d) stated:

(23)      While I do not agree that s273.1(2)(c) renders 265(3)(d) redundant, I accept that s273.1(2)(c) engages in a more nuanced or subtle form of pressure or inducement which may be inferred from the circumstances. ...

[105]      Continuing at para 24;

(24)      Section 273.1(2)(c) does not require a finding of dependency by the complainant or a dominant position by the accused.  Rather, s273.1(2)(c) is engaged when the accused abuses, that is, misuses or makes improper use of his position of trust or authority, thereby inducing, that is, persuading or enticing the complainant to consent to sexual activity ...

[106]      At para 25;

(25)      In the absence of the complainant’s direct evidence of inducement, the surrounding circumstances may provide an evidentiary basis for consideration by the trier of fact in determining whether consent was vitiated by the operation of 273.1(2)(c).

[107]      In R c Audet, 1996 CanLII 198 (SCC), [1996] 2 SCR 171, the Supreme Court of Canada made it clear that for s273.1(2)(c) to be effective in vitiating the ostensible consent of a complainant, the Crown must prove beyond a reasonable doubt not only the existence of a position of power [or authority] but that the position was abused.  It is not enough that the accused occupied a position of authority; a person occupying such a position must, through abuse of that position, induce the complainant to engage in the activity in question.  There must be evidence before the court in which it could be inferred that the complainant was induced to consent to sexual activity by the accused’s abuse of his position of authority; Snelgrove, at para 18.

[108]      The Crown asserts that the Accused was in a position of power or authority vis-a-vis the Complainant as he had some supervisory control over her on the day in question.  The only evidence in that regard was given by the Accused, which was that the Complainant was a level 1 Supervisor, and he was a level 3.  What that actually means in terms of power or authority was never explored or explained, nor did the Complainant in her testimony describe the nature of their employment relationship and what may have been his actual authority over her or what she believed was his authority.   There is no suggestion he could fire someone or suspend them or discipline them.  The most that can be inferred from the paucity of evidence is that he was essentially the senior worker on site, and over and above the duties which he shared with the Complainant and others, he had some responsibility to make sure the employees worked in accordance with the restaurant protocol.

[109]      The Complainant did not suggest in her evidence that his status in the restaurant affected her conduct in any way on that day.

[110]      In the absence of such objective evidence, the Court may infer the same from circumstantial evidence before the Court.  Although there may be circumstances where one person is presumptively in a position of authority in an employment circumstance, I do not believe that can be said to be the case in all employment hierarchies.  The relationship between the Accused and Complainant at the time and prior to it does not objectively demonstrate such a presumptive relationship of authority and there is in my view, insufficient evidence to conclude that such a relationship as is contemplated by s265(3)(d) or s273.1(2)(c) was present.  The Complainant gave no evidence in that regard and there is no suggestion that his Level 3 supervisory status related to anything in the employee relationship other than supervision of employee work compliance on that day.  The Accused and Complainant were essentially fulfilling the same duties - taking orders, filling orders, cleaning the facilities as necessary throughout their shift.  The Accused was not the employer, nor the manager nor is there any suggestion or reasonable basis to infer that he hired or fired individuals for or from work.  He was slightly higher than the Complainant in the employee chain but that does not lead to the presumption that he is in a position of authority, capable of coercing or influencing her, indeed they continued to, as explored previously, engage in horseplay together, and seemingly operated as peers.

[111]      The evidence does not prove beyond a reasonable doubt in my view the existence that he was in a position of power or authority as contemplated by s265(3)(d) or s273.1(2)(c).

[112]      Given that finding, neither s265(3)(d) nor s273.1(2)(c) operate to vitiate the Complainant’s ostensible consent in this case.  If I am wrong in arriving at that conclusion and he was in a position of authority vis-a-vis the Complainant as contemplated in s265(3)(d) or s273.1(2)(c) or both, with respect to the application of s265(3)(d), the Crown must demonstrate a coercive use of this authority to overcome resistance to a consent.  The Crown would have to show beyond a reasonable doubt the exercise of influence to the point of coercion; Geddes, at para 35.  In this case there is no evidence of such coercion, and simply being in an authority relationship does not lead to such an inference.  There is no evidentiary basis on which to conclude that the Accused used his position to extract a consent to the sexual activity.

[113]      With respect to the application of s273.1(2)(c) in these circumstances, again, if I am wrong on the issue of the Accused’s being in a position of authority within the meaning of that section and his supervisor 3 status over her supervisor 1 status creates such a relationship of authority, s273.1(2)(c) does not imply the same kind of coercion as demanded in s265(3)(d).  As the court in Snelgrove sets out in para 27, s273.1(2)(c) engages a more nuanced or subtle form of pressure or inducement which may be inferred from the circumstances.  Section 273.1(2)(c) requires no finding of dependence by the Complainant, or a dominant position by the Accused.  It is operative when an accused abuses by misuse or improper use his position of power or authority through persuading or inducing the complainant to consent to the sexual activity.

[114]      The evidence before the Court does not demonstrate that the Accused induced or attempted to induce or persuade the Complainant in a manner related to his position of authority, to consent to the sexual encounter.  In the context of the circumstances leading up to and during the interaction between the parties, I can find no evidentiary basis to support that the Accused used his position to persuade the Complainant to consent to the sexual activity that took place between them as I have so found.

[115]      Although they had a friendly working relationship there is no basis on which to infer that that friendly relationship was used to persuade or entice the Complainant into the sexual activity that occurred in the shed.  The Accused did not use that relationship in the circumstances this Court has found to manipulate or entice her into the sexual activity that occurred.

[116]      In summary, I am not satisfied that the Crown has proven, beyond a reasonable doubt, a relationship of power and authority as between the Accused and the Complainant, nor such coercion as necessary for application of s265(3)(d) or inducement as required by s273.1(2)(c), and accordingly neither of the said sections operates to vitiate the Complainant’s ostensible consent to the sexual activity which occurred in the shed and which is the subject of the allegation set out in Count 3.

[117]      Accordingly, all charges against the Accused are dismissed.
